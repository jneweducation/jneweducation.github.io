28. how is the philippines located in the globe or in the map?name the landmasses and territorial waters surrounding it.​


Answer:

There are no landmasses surrounding the Philippines but there are plenty of bodies of water as the Philippines is an archipelago. There are other countries, however that are near, including Taiwan to the North and Malaysia and Indonesia to the South. The Philippines is strategically located where access from Asian countries are easy. It also serves as a usual stopover for cross-Pacific flights as well as a port of call for various ships internationally because of our many bodies of water.

North

Land: North of the Philippines we have Taiwan. China and and the Korean Peninsula.  

Water: We have the Luzon Strait running across most of Luzon .

East  

Land: Towards the East is the Pacific Ocean including islands like Guam, Palau, the Micronesia and other small island territories. The Philippine Sea is towards the southern part of our eastern seaboard.

Water: We have the Benham Rise, now referred to as the Philippine Rise. This area is said to be rich in natural resources. Of course, we also have the Pacific Ocean.

South

Land: To the south we have Borneo, divided into Malaysia and Indonesia.  

Water: We have the Celebes Sea  towards the south

West

Land: The Philippine EEZ or Exclusive Economic Zone. These are small islands, islets, rocks and other natural formations that are scattered wherein we have sovereign rights. It is an area of dispute with other countries such as China, Viet Nam and Indonesia.  

Water: The West Philippine Sea surrounds the western seaboard. Also in here is the South China Sea which as mentioned above is a point of disagreement among various Asian nations.

Implications

Because we do not share land borders with other nations, and the oceans have always been treated as a shared resource, Filipinos typically do not have the concept of protecting its land boundaries--compared with Tibet for example, where they are strongly standing against China as the bigger country attempts to encroach on its lands.

30. Territory is the second essential element of the state. State is a territorial unit. Define territory is it's essential component. A state cannot exist in the air at sea. It is a essentially a territorial state. The size of the territory of a state can be big or small, nevertheless, it has to be a definite well-marked portion of territory. paki explain po in Tagalog ​


Answer:

Territory, as an essential component of a state, refers to the defined and well-marked geographical area over which a state exercises its sovereignty and jurisdiction. It is a fundamental element that distinguishes a state from other forms of political entities. A state cannot exist solely in the air or at sea; it requires a physical space on land that it governs and controls.

The territory of a state provides the spatial framework within which the state's institutions, laws, and governance systems operate. It establishes the geographical boundaries within which the state exercises its authority, enforces laws, provides public services, and protects the rights and interests of its citizens. The territory represents the tangible manifestation of a state's sovereignty and defines its sphere of influence.

The size of a state's territory can vary significantly, ranging from vast nations with extensive land areas to small countries encompassing only a few square kilometers. Regardless of size, the territory must be clearly defined and demarcated through legal, historical, or geographical means. Well-defined borders, whether natural features like rivers or man-made boundaries like fences or walls, help establish and maintain the territorial integrity of a state.

In summary, territory is an essential component of a state, representing the defined and well-marked portion of land within which a state exercises its sovereignty and authority. It provides the physical space for a state's governance, jurisdiction, and the fulfillment of its obligations to its citizens. The concept of territory is crucial in understanding the nature and functioning of a state as a territorial unit.
