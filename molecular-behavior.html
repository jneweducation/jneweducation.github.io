3. Identify the property of matter that corresponds to the molecular behavior.​


The enormous number of molecules in even a small volume of a dilute gas produces not complication, as might be expected, but rather simplification. The reason is that ordinarily only statistical averages are observed in the study of the behaviour and properties of gases, and statistical methods are quite accurate when large numbers are involved. Compared to the numbers of molecules involved, there are only a few properties of gases that warrant attention here, namely, pressure, density, temperature, internal energy, viscosity, heat conductivity, and diffusivity. (More subtle properties can be brought into view by the application of electric and magnetic fields, but they are of minor interest.)

5. When 0.5 g of a liquid is completely evaporated and collected in liter manometer, the pressure is 0.25 atm and the temperature is 27oc. assume ideal gas behavior, find the molecular weight if the gas constant is r?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P = \text{0.25 atm} \\ & V = \text{1 L} \\ & T = 27^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{300.15 K} \\ & mass = \text{0.5 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of gas using ideal gas equation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n & = \frac{PV}{\text{RT}} \\ & = \frac{(\text{0.25 atm})(\text{1 L})}{\left(0.082057 \: \dfrac{\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}}\right)(\text{300.15 K})} \\ & = \text{0.01015 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molecular weight of the gas.

[tex]\begin{aligned} MW & = \frac{mass}{n} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.5 g}}{\text{0.01015 mol}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{49.26 g/mol}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molecular weight of the gas is 49.26 g/mol.

24. 1. What is kinetic molecular theosy and its assumptions 2. What are the different factors affecting the behavior of gases? 3. What are the different Gas LawsPwede po patolong please ​


Answer:

What is kinetic molecular theory and its assumptions?

Kinetic molecular theory is a model that explains the behavior of gases in terms of the motion of their particles. Its assumptions are that gases are made up of tiny particles that are in constant random motion, the particles have negligible volume and are separated by large distances, the particles have no attractive or repulsive forces between them, and the average kinetic energy of the particles is directly proportional to the temperature of the gas.
What are the different factors affecting the behavior of gases?

The different factors affecting the behavior of gases are temperature, pressure, volume, and the number of particles in the gas. Increasing the temperature of a gas increases the kinetic energy and speed of its particles, causing them to collide more frequently and with greater force. Increasing the pressure of a gas compresses its particles closer together, reducing the volume they occupy. Increasing the volume of a gas allows its particles to spread out more and reduces the frequency of collisions. Increasing the number of particles in a gas increases its pressure and the frequency of collisions between its particles.
What are the different Gas Laws?

The different gas laws include Boyle's law, which states that the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume at a constant temperature; Charles's law, which states that the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature at a constant pressure; Gay-Lussac's law, which states that the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature at a constant volume; and the combined gas law, which combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws into a single equation. Additionally, there is Avogadro's law, which states that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of particles.
