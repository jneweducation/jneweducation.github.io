9. The current in an electrical conductor varies inversely as the resistance R of the conductor. The current is 2 amperes when the resistance is 960 ohms. What is the current when the resistance is 540 ohms? With solving ​


Answer:

The current (c) is 3.6 amperes when the resistance (R) is 540 ohms.

Step-by-step explanation:

Let c be the current and R be the resistance of the conductor.

Mathematical Equation:

[tex]c=\frac{k}{R}[/tex]

For the constant of variation (k):

Given:   [tex]c=2[/tex],   [tex]R=960[/tex]

Find:   [tex]k=?[/tex]

Formula:   [tex]c=\frac{k}{R}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]c=\frac{k}{R}\\2=\frac{k}{960}\\k=(2)(960)\\k=1,920[/tex]

For the current (c):

Given:   [tex]k=1,920[/tex],   [tex]R=540[/tex]

Find:   [tex]c=?[/tex]

Formula:   [tex]c=\frac{k}{R}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]c=\frac{k}{R}\\c=\frac{1,920}{540}\\\boxed{\boldsymbol{c=3.6}}[/tex]

19. A conductor whose diameter is 0.175 inch has a resistance of 0.5 ohm. The wire is drawn through a series of dies until its diameter is reduced to 0.08 inch. Assuming the specific resistance of the material remains constant, what is the resistance of lengthened conductor?


Answer:

The resistance of the lengthened conductor is 2.41 Ω · L₂/L₁

Explanation:

In this problem, we are to solve the resistance when the same conductor reduced in its diameter. This means that the resistivity constant is the same because the conductor is the same.

Formula to use

R = ρL/ A

where

R     is the resistance, the unit is in ohms, Ω

ρ     is the resistivity constant, the unit is in Ω·in

L     is the length of the conductor, the given unit is in inch

A     is the area of the conductor, the unit is in in²

Given information

R₁ = 0.5 Ω

d₁ = 0.175 inch

d₂ = 0.08 in

R₂ = ?

Solving the problem

First, let us solve the area of the conductor for its original length where the cross-sectional area is a circle.

A₁ = πd²/4

A₁ = π(0.175 in)²/4

A₁ = 0.0241 in²

Next, let us also solve the area when the conductor reduced in diameter using the same formula for the area.

A₂ = πd²/4

A₂ = π(0.08 in)²/4

A₂ = 0.005 in²

Now, let us use the given formula to solve the resistivity constant

R₁ = ρL₁/ A₁

Then, substitute the given data

0.5Ω = ρL₁/ 0.0241 in²

ρL₁ = (0.5Ω)(0.0241 in²)

ρ = 0.01205 Ω·in²/ L₁

Finally, let us solve the resistance of the lengthened conductor when its diameter was reduced

R₂ = ρL₂/ A₂

R₂ = ρL₂ /0.005 in²

Then, substitute the resistivity constant since they have the same material

R₂ = (0.01205 Ω·in²/ L₁ )L₂ / 0.005 in²

R₂ = 2.41 Ω · L₂/L₁

Here, there is no given length, that is why the answer is in terms of length.

Therefore, the resistance is 2.41Ω · L₂/L₁

21. What is true about the relationship of temperature and resistance? The temperature and resistance is directly proportional for insulators. The temperature and resistance is inversely proportional for conductors The temperature and resistance is inversely proportional for insulators The temperature and resistance is directly proportional for conductors


The relationship between temperature and resistance depends on the type of material being considered. For conductors, the temperature and resistance are directly proportional, meaning that as the temperature of a conductor increases, its resistance also increases. This is known as a positive temperature coefficient of resistance.

On the other hand, for most insulators, the temperature and resistance are inversely proportional. This means that as the temperature of an insulator increases, its resistance decreases. This is known as a negative temperature coefficient of resistance.

It's worth noting that there are some exceptions to these generalizations, and that the relationship between temperature and resistance can be more complex in certain materials. However, as a general rule, conductors have a positive temperature coefficient of resistance, while insulators have a negative temperature coefficient of resistance.
