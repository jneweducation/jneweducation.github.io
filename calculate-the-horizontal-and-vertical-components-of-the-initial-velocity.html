(b) Calculate the horizontal and vertical displacements (dx and dy) of the ball after 1.5 seconds.(c) Calculate the horizontal and vertical components of the velocity of the ball after 1.5 seconds.​


a) vertical component of initial velocity

[tex]u_{x}= u cos\theta[/tex]

[tex]u_{x}= 10\times cos37[/tex]

[tex]u_{x}= 10\times\frac{4}{5}=8m/s[/tex]

Horizontal component of velocity.

[tex]u_{y}= u sin\theta[/tex]

[tex]u_{y}= 10\times sin37[/tex]

[tex]u_{y}= 10\times\frac{3}{5}=6m/s[/tex]

b) vertical displacement.

[tex]x= (u_{x})t[/tex]

[tex]u_{x}= (8)\times1.5=12m[/tex]

Horizontal displacement.

[tex]y=u_{y}t-\frac{1}{2}gt^{2}[/tex]

[tex]y=(6\times1.5)-\frac{1}{2}\times(1.5)^{2}[/tex]

[tex]y=9-11.25=-2.25m[/tex]

c) Vertical component of velocity is independent of time.

So, [tex]v_{x}=u_{x}= 8 m/s[/tex]

Horizontal component at t=1.5s

[tex]v_{y}=u_{y}-gt[/tex]

[tex]v_{y}=6-(10\times1.5)=-9m/s[/tex]

14. Jason Kendall throws a baseball with a horizontal component of velocity of 25 m/s. It takes 3.0s to come back to its original height. Calculate its horizontal range, its initial vertical component of velocity and its initial angle of projection.


Answer:

velocity is 14.7 m/s and its initial angle of projection is approximately 18.5 degrees.

Explanation:

We can use the formulae of projectile motion to solve this problem. Let's assume the initial vertical velocity of the baseball is "v" and the angle of projection is "θ".

Since the vertical displacement of the ball is zero (it returns to its original height), we can use the following formula to find the initial vertical velocity:

0 = v*sin(θ)*t - (1/2)gt^2

where g is the acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s^2) and t is the time of flight (3.0 s). Solving for v, we get:

v = (1/2)gt

v = (1/2)9.83.0

v = 14.7 m/s

18. Jason Kendall throws a baseball with a horizontal component of velocity of 25 m/s. It takes 3.00s to come back to its original height. Calculate its horizontal range, its initial vertical component of velocity and its initial angle of projection.


Answer:

We can use the following kinematic equations to solve the problem:

1. For horizontal range (R):

R = Vx * t

where Vx is the horizontal velocity component and t is the time of flight.

2. For initial vertical velocity component (Vy):

h = Vyt - 0.5gt^2

where h is the maximum height reached, Vy is the initial vertical velocity component, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and t is the time of flight. Since the ball returns to its original height, we have h = 0.

3. For initial angle of projection (θ):

θ = tan^-1 (Vy / Vx)

where θ is the angle of projection, and Vy and Vx are the initial vertical and horizontal velocity components, respectively.

Given that Vx = 25 m/s and t = 3.00 s, we can calculate the horizontal range (R):

R = Vx * t = 25 m/s * 3.00 s = 75 m

Since the ball returns to its original height, we know that its initial and final vertical velocities are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction. Therefore, the initial vertical velocity component (Vy) is equal to the negative of the final vertical velocity component. Using the equation for vertical displacement, we can find the magnitude of the final vertical velocity component:

0 = Vyt - 0.5gt^2

Vyt = 0.5gt^2

Vyt = 0.5 * 9.81 m/s^2 * (3.00 s)^2 = 44.14 m/s

So the initial vertical velocity component is:

Vy = -44.14 m/s

Now we can find the initial angle of projection (θ) using the equation:

θ = tan^-1 (Vy / Vx) = tan^-1 (-44.14 m/s / 25 m/s) ≈ -60.5°

Therefore, Jason Kendall threw the baseball at an initial angle of projection of about 60.5 degrees below the horizontal.
