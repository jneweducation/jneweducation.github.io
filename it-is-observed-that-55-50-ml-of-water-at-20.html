9. Is the volume of the resulting sugar mixture equal, more than or less than the sum (20 mL sugar + 50 mL water) of the volumes of the unmixed sugar and water?​


When you combine 20 ml sugar to 50 ml water, initially, you may think that this will result to a greater volume through simple addition.

However, considering the atoms which make up these two substances, the resulting mixture will have a lesser volume than the sum of the volumes of the unmixed substances.

Why? This is because water is a liquid. It is composed of tiny particles which are arranged in such a way that spaces can be found between them. When you mix sugar with water, it will be dissolved. Those dissolved sugar particles will be able to fit in the spaces between the particles of water, making the mixture of lesser volume.

However, please take note that these conditions cannot be observed with an unaided eye.

24. is the volume of the resulting sugar mixture equal, more than less than the sum (20 ml sugar + 50 ml water ) of the volumes of the unmixed sugar and water?


Answer:

Volume of the mixture is less than the additive volumes of the unmixed components

Explanation:

Unlike what one would think, volumes are not usually additive. This is because there are intermolecular interactions across two different kinds of molecules. For example, if 10 mL of ethanol and 10 mL of water were mixed in a mixture, it will produce a mixture of less than 20 mL. This is because ethanol has an -OH, which means that it strongly interacts with water, which is polar. This strong interaction with water makes the ethanol molecules clump together with the water molecules, increasing its density and therefore decreasing the total volume.

For the case of a solid-liquid solution, like sugar and water, it will be less as well. This is because sugar has multiple polar -OH groups (like ethanol). This allows the water to come inside the sugar molecules and the clump together, thus allowing more molcules to occupy the same space.

It should be emphasized however, that due to the law of conservation of mass, masses are always additive. So 20 g sugar dissolved in 50 g water should always result in a 70 g mixture.
