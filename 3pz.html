Hund's Rule and Pauli's Principle

In Hund's Rule, one electron moves into each of two or more degenerate (i.e. same energy) orbitals until all of them are half full before pairing up. You may have heard it before in reference to the example of filling seats on a bus: everyone finds their own seat, and they only start sitting together after all of the seats are half full in filling electrons.

According to the Pauli Exclusion Principle, no two electrons can be identified using the same quantum numbers. We have the spin quantum number because it ensures that two electrons in the same orbital have different quantum numbers of electrons.

Hund's Rule states that if 2 or more degenerate (i.e. same energy) orbitals are available, one electron goes into each until all of them are half full before pairing up . ... The Pauli Exclusion Principle states that no two electrons can be identified by the same set of quantum numbers.

What is the principle of Hund's Rule?

Hund's rule states that: Every orbital in a sublevel is singly occupied before any orbital is doubly occupied. All of the electrons in singly occupied orbitals have the same spin (to maximize total spin).

Al-13

Given:  [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3d^{1}[/tex]

Aluminum has an electron configuration of: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3s^{2}3px^{1}[/tex]

- The electron must fill up the 3p orbital first before jumping to 3d-orbital. The added electron must always occupy the orbital with the lowest energy first. Electron orbitals arranged in increasing energy. The 3p orbital has lower energy level than 3d, therefore, 3p must be filled-up first.

Cl-17

Given: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3px^{2}3py^{2}4s^{1}[/tex]

Chlorine has an electron configuration of: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3px^{2}3py^{2}3pz^{1}[/tex] - Lower energy level must be filled first. So, 3p orbital should be filled not the 4s orbital.

Ne-10

Given: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{1}3s^{1}[/tex]

The electron configuration of Neon is: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}[/tex] - Before filling up the 3s orbital, the electron added will fill up the lower energy first, which is the 2p-z orbital.

K-19

Given: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3px^{2}3py^{2}3pz^{2}3d^{1}[/tex]

It should be [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{2}2pz^{2}3s^{2}3px^{2}3py^{2}3pz^{2}4s^{1}[/tex] - The 3d orbital has a greater energy level than 4s. Therefore, filling up 3d orbital instead of 4s is wrong.

N-7

Given: [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2px^{2}2py^{1}[/tex]

Nitrogen electron configuration is [tex]1s^{2}2s^{2}2py^{1}2px^{1}2pz^{1}[/tex] - In filling up p-orbitals, electrons must fill up all up or down spin first in empty subshells of x, y, and z before paring up. The electron will occupy all empty orbitals in a subshell at p-orbital with single electrons having parallel spins before entering half-filled subshell or orbitals.
