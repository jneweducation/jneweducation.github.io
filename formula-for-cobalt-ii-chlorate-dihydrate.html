11. How many grams of potassium chlorate is required to yield 3 L of oxygen at STP?


Hello!

How many grams of potassium chlorate is required to yield 3 L of oxygen at STP ?

We have the following data:

Molar mass of KClO3 (potassium chlorate)

K = 1*(39 u) = 39 u

Cl = 1*(35.5 u) = 35.5 u

O = 3*(16 u) = 48 u

------------------------------------

Molar mass of KClO3 = 39 + 35.5 + 48 = 122.5 g/mol

In STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure), one mole of gas occupies 22.4 liters, so we have the following relation, let us see:

122.5 g of HClO3 ------------- 22.4 L (in STP)

y g of HClO3 ------------------- 3 L (in STP)

[tex]\dfrac{122.5}{y} = \dfrac{22.4}{3}[/tex]

multiply the means by the extremes

[tex]22.4*y = 122.5*3[/tex]

[tex]22.4\:y = 367.5[/tex]

[tex]y = \dfrac{367.5}{22.4}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\boxed{y \approx 16.4\:grams\:of\:HClO_3}}\end{array}}\qquad\checkmark[/tex]

Answer:

16.4 grams of potassium chlorate

22. A 4. 00 g ample of a compound contain 2. 9737 g cobalt and the ret of the ample i oxygen. What i the empirical formula of thi compound?.


Problem:

A 4.00 g ample of a compound contains 2.9737 g cobalt and the rest of the sample is oxygen. What is the empirical formula of this compound?

Solution:

mass of sample = 4.00 g

mass of Co = 2.9737 g

mass of O = 4.00 g - 2.9737 g = 1.0263 g

%Co = 2.9737g/4.00 g × 100 = 74.34%

%O = 1.0263g/4.00 g × 100 = 25.66%

Assume 100-g solution.

74.34 g × (1 mol Co/58.93 g) = 1.26 mol Co

25.66 g × (1 mol O/16.00 g) = 1.60 mol O

Co = 1.26/1.26 = 1.00

O = 1.60/1.26 = 1.27

Multiply 1.00 and 1.27 to a factor that will make a whole-number (or almost) product.

1.00 × 11 = 11

1.27 × 11 ≈ 14

Possible empirical formula:Co₁₁O₁₄


23. Excess cobalt (II) chloride solution is added to 25.0 mL of 0.34 M sodium hydroxide solution. Determine the mass of cobalt (II) hydroxide that precipitates.


Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of NaOH.

moles of NaOH = molarity × volume of solution (L)

moles of NaOH = 0.34 M × 0.0250 L

moles of NaOH = 0.0085 mol

Step 2: Write the balanced chemical equation.

CoCl₂ + 2NaOH → Co(OH)₂ + 2NaCl

Step 3: Calculate the molar mass of Co(OH)₂.

molar mass = (58.93 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 2)

molar mass = 92.946 g/mol

Step 4: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 2 mol NaOH : 1 mol Co(OH)₂

Step 5: Calculate the mass of Co(OH)₂.

[tex]\text{mass of Co(OH)₂ = 0.0085 mol NaOH} × \frac{\text{1 mol Co(OH)₂}}{\text{2 mol NaOH}} × \frac{\text{92.946 g Co(OH)₂}}{\text{1 mol Co(OH)₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of Co(OH)₂ = 0.395 g}}[/tex]

27. What mass of anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride (CoCl2)would be needed inorder to make 650.00 mL of a 4.00 M cobalt(II) chloride solution?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M = 4.00 \: M = \text{4.00 mol/L} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{129.83 g/mol} \\ & V_{\text{solution}} = \text{650.00 mL = 0.65000 L} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = (M)(V_{\text{solution}}) \\ & = \text{(4.00 mol/L)(0.65000 L)} \\ & = \text{2.60 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solute}} & = (n_{\text{solute}})(MM_{\text{solute}}) \\ & = \text{(2.60 mol)(129.83 g/mol)} \\ & = \text{337.558 g} \\ & \approx \boxed{\text{338 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 338 g of anhydrous CoCl₂ would be needed.
