3. Find there Chemical Reactions 1.) CH4+O2=>CO2+H20 2.) Fe+CuSo4=>Cu+FeSO4 3.) P4+O2=>P2O5 Pahelp po ako genchem po


CHEMICAL REACTIONS

Law of Conversion of Mass

— This law of mass states that no atoms can be created or destroyed in chemical reaction. The atoms in the reactants have to balance the number of atoms in the product.

We need to compare the number of atoms for each element on both sides of an equation, then change coefficient so the number is same.

1.) [tex] \bold{CH_4 + O_2 \implies CO_2 + H_2 O\implies\: CH_4 + 2O_2 \implies CO_2 2H_2O}[/tex]

As we can see that C is balanced.

So, to balance H multiply the H20 on the right by 2.

[tex] CH_4 + O_2 \implies CO_2 + 2H_2 O[/tex]

Now, To balance O we need to multiply O2 on the right by 2.

[tex] CH_4 + 2O_2 \implies CO_2 + 2H_2O[/tex]

2.) [tex] \bold{Fe + CuSO_4 \implies Cu+FeSO_4 \implies Fe + CuSO_4 \implies Cu+FeSO_4}[/tex]

The given equation is already balanced. We can see Fe, Cu, S, and O are balanced so you don't have to worry how to balance this. But if you want to make sure the equation is balanced, we can solve this algebraicly :)

[tex] Fe+CuSO_4\implies Cu+FeSO_4[/tex]

[tex] vFe+vCuSO_4\implies wCu+ tFeSO_4[/tex]

As you can see I add some variables in the equation because we're going to use the variables to create a system of equation to find the coefficient.

[tex] \begin{cases} Fe:\:u=t\\ Cu:\:v=w\\ S:\:v=t\\ O:\:4v=4t\\\end{cases}[/tex]

[tex]\begin{cases} u=t\\v=w\\v=t\\4v=4t\\\end{cases}[/tex]

[tex] \begin{cases} u=t\\w=t\\4w=4t\\\end{cases}[/tex]

[tex] \begin{cases} u=t\\4w=4t\\\end{cases}[/tex]

[tex] \begin{cases} 4t=4t\end{cases}[/tex]

Gather all the coefficient then select an arbitrary coefficient: t = 1

(Note: We must ensure that all coefficients are an integers)

[tex] \boxed{u = 1,\:v=1,\:w=1,\:t=1}[/tex]

Therefore, [tex] Fe + CuSO_4 \implies Cu+FeSO_4[/tex]

3.) [tex] \bold{P_4+O_2\implies P_2O_5 \implies P_4+5O_2 \implies 2P_2O_5}[/tex]

This equation is similar to the item #1.

So, To balance P just multiply P2O5 on the right by 2. Therefore,

[tex] P_4 + O_2 \implies 2P_2O_5[/tex]

After that, we need to balance the O just multiply O2 on the right by 2. Therefore,

[tex] P_4+5O_2 \implies 2P_2O_5[/tex]

Further explanation

There are chemical reactions that may occur.

1. A single replacement reaction is a chemical reaction in which one element replaces the other elements of a compound to produce new elements and compounds

Not all of these reactions can occur. We can use the activity series, which is a list of elements that can replace other elements below / to the right of them in a single replacement reaction.

This series is better known as the Volta series, where the metal element with a more negative electrode potential is on the left, while the element with a more positive electrode potential on the right.

The more left the position of a metal in the series, the more reactive metal (easy to release electrons, the stronger the reductor)

Generally, the Volta series used is

Li K Ba Sr Ca Na Mg Al Mn Zn Cr Fe Cd Co Ni Sn Pb H Sb Bi Cu Hg Ag Pt Au

Example:

2Al (s) + 3Zn (NO₃) ₂ (aq) → 2Al (NO₃) ₃ (aq) + 3Zn (s)

The existing aluminum element can replace the zinc element, which is on the right side so the reaction will occur.

12. The phosphorus pentoxide used to produce phosphoric acid for cola soft drinks is prepared by burning phosphorus in oxygen. (a) what is the limiting reactant when 0.200 mol of P4 and 0.200mol of O2 react according to equation; P4 + 5O2 --> P4O10 (b) calculate the percent yield if 10.0g of P4O10 is isolated the reaction.


(a) SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of P₄O₁₀ formed by each reactant.

• Using P₄

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of P₄ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of P₄O₁₀.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} & = \text{0.200 mol} \: \text{P}_4 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4} \\ & = \text{0.200 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using O₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 5 moles of O₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of P₄O₁₀.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} & = \text{0.200 mol} \: \text{O}_2 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{\text{5 mol} \: \text{O}_2} \\ & = \text{0.0400 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Determine the limiting reactant.

Since O₂ produced less amount of P₄O₁₀ than P₄,

[tex]\boxed{\text{O}_2 \: \text{is the limiting reactant.}}[/tex]

------------------------------------------------------------

(b) SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the theoretical yield.

The theoretical yield of a reaction is the maximum mass of a product that can be formed. It is dictated by the limiting reactant. In this case, we will start at the number of moles of P₄O₁₀ formed from the limiting reactant (O₂) which is equal to 0.0400 mol.

The molar mass of P₄O₁₀ is 283.886 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{theoretical yield} & = \text{0.0400 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} \times \frac{\text{283.886 g} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}} \\ & = \text{11.355 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the percent yield.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{percent yield} & = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{\text{10.0 g}}{\text{11.355 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{88.1\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the percent yield of the reaction is 88.1%.

26. a) Determine the mass of tetraphosphorusdecoxide (P4010) formed if 25.0 g ofphosphorus (P4) and 50.0 g of oxygen (O2) are combined. (Place your answerinside the box).moins after the reaction stops?​


Answer:

mass P4O10 = 57.3 g

mass O2 remained = 17.7 g

Explanation:

Writing the balanced chemical equation

P4 + 5O2 → P4O10

Calculating the number moles of P4O10 formed

Starting with 25.0 g P4

n P4O10 = 25.0 g P4 × (1 mol P4 / 123.9 g P4) × (1 mol P4O10 / 1 mol P4)

n P4O10 = 0.2018 mol

Starting with 50.0 g O2

n P4O10 = 50.0 g O2 × (1 mol O2 / 32.00 g O2) × (1 mol P4O10 / 5 mol O2)

n P4O10 = 0.3125 mol

Calculating the mass of P4O10 formed

Since P4 is the limiting reactant,

mass P4O10 = 0.2018 mol P4O10 × (283.9 g P4O10 / 1 mol P4O10)

mass P4O10 = 57.3 g

Calculating the mass of excess reactant remained

Calculating the mass of O2 reacted

mass O2 reacted = 0.2018 mol P4O10 × (5 mol O2 / 1 mol P4O10) × (32.00 g O2 / 1 mol O2)

mass O2 reacted = 32.3 g

Subtracting the mass of O2 reacted from the initial mass of O2

mass O2 remained = initial mass O2 - mass O2 reacted

mass O2 remained = 50.0 g - 32.3 g

mass O2 remained = 17.7 g
