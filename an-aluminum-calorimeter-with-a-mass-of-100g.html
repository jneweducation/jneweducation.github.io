5. A 35.9 g sample of vanadium was heated to 78 ° C ( initial temp), then it was dumped into a calorimeter. The initial temperature of the calorimeter’s water was 23.0 ° C. Afterwards the sample was released, the calorimeter’s water final temperature is 26.35 ° C. What mass of the water was in the calorimeter?​


Answer:

Solving this equation, you find that the mass of the water in the calorimeter is approximately 10.9 g.

Explanation:

To solve this problem, you need to use the equation for calculating heat transfer:

Heat transferred = mass * specific heat capacity * temperature change

In this case, the heat transferred is the heat gained by the water in the calorimeter. The mass of the water is the unknown quantity that you need to solve for. The specific heat capacity of water is 4.184 J/g°C, and the temperature change is the difference between the final temperature of the water (26.35 °C) and its initial temperature (23.0 °C).

Plugging these values into the equation, you get:

Heat transferred = mass * 4.184 J/g°C * (26.35 °C - 23.0 °C)

You also know that the heat transferred is equal to the heat lost by the vanadium sample. The heat lost by the sample can be calculated using the same equation:

Heat lost by the sample = mass of sample * specific heat capacity of sample * temperature change of sample

The specific heat capacity of vanadium is 0.489 J/g°C, and the temperature change of the sample is the difference between its initial temperature (78 °C) and the final temperature of the water (26.35 °C). Plugging these values into the equation, you get:

Heat lost by the sample = 35.9 g * 0.489 J/g°C * (78 °C - 26.35 °C)

Now you have two equations with two unknowns (the heat transferred and the mass of the water). You can solve for the mass of the water by setting the two equations equal to each other and solving for the mass:

mass * 4.184 J/g°C * (26.35 °C - 23.0 °C) = 35.9 g * 0.489 J/g°C * (78 °C - 26.35 °C

Explanation:

Data:

Heat = 24 KJ = 24,000 J

mass = 1.3kg = 1300 g

Cs = 3.41 J / (g °C)

Ti = 25.5 °C

Question: Tf

Formula: Heat = mCsΔT =>

ΔT = Heat / (mCs) = 24000J/[1300g*3.41J/(g°C)] = 5.4 °C

ΔT = Tf - Ti => Tf = ΔT + Ti = 25.5°C + 5.4°C = 30.9°C

Answer: 30.9°

15. Aluminum oxide has a composition of 52.9% aluminum and 47.1% oxygen by mass. if 16.4g of aluminum reacts with oxygen to form aluminum oxide, what mass of oxygen reacts?


Answer:

The balanced chemical reaction is written as:

4Al + 3O2 = 2Al2O3

To determine the mass of oxygen gas that would react with the given amount of aluminum metal, we use the initial amount and relate this amount to the ratio of the substances from the chemical reaction. We do as follows:

moles Al = 16.4 g ( 1 mol / 26.98 g ) = 0.61 mol Al

moles O2 = 0.61 mol Al ( 3 mol O2 / 4 mol Al ) = 0.46 mol O2

mass O2 = 0.46 mol O2 ( 32.0 g / mol ) = 14.59 g O2

Therefore, to completely react 16.4 grams of aluminum metal we need a minimum of 14.59 grams of oxygen gas.

The law of conservation of energy can be applied to heat changes, i.e. the heat received/absorbed is the same as the heat released  

Q in = Q out  

Heat can be calculated using the formula:  

Q = mc∆T  

Q = heat, J  

m = mass, g  

c = specific heat, joules / g ° C  

∆T = temperature difference, ° C / K  

Water absorbs the heat from aluminum, so

Q water = Q aluminum

m x 4.184 J/g ⋅ C x (27.3 - 25.8) = 14.2 g x 0.9  J/g ⋅ C x (92.6 - 27.3)

m x 6.276 = 834.534

m = 132.972 g

26. A Physics student wants to determine experimentally the heat of vaporization of water. He weighed 8.50 grams of water which is heated in a steam boiler until it all turns to vapor. All of the vapor is directed to an Aluminum calorimeter containing 125.0 grams of water initially at 18.5°C. After some time, the temperature of the mixture becomes 54.2°C. What is their experimental value for the latent heat of vaporization of water? The mass of the calorimeter is 13.0 grams. Assume no heat is transferred to the surrounding. C (water) = 1.00 cal/gCº C (aluminum) = 0.215 cal/gCº


Answer:

First, we need to calculate the amount of heat transferred from the steam to the water in the calorimeter:

Q = m1 × L + m1 × c1 × ΔT1

where Q is the heat transferred, m1 is the mass of the steam (which is equal to the mass of water used in the experiment), L is the latent heat of vaporization of water, c1 is the specific heat capacity of water, and ΔT1 is the change in temperature of the steam from its initial temperature to 100°C.

We can assume that the initial temperature of the steam is the same as the boiling point of water, which is 100°C. Therefore, ΔT1 = 100°C - 0°C = 100°C.

The specific heat capacity of water is 4.18 J/g°C, and the mass of the water used in the experiment is 8.50 grams. Therefore:

Q = 8.50 g × L + 8.50 g × 4.18 J/g°C × 100°C

Q = 8.50 g × L + 3571 J

Next, we need to calculate the amount of heat absorbed by the water in the calorimeter:

Q = m2 × c2 × ΔT2

where m2 is the mass of the water in the calorimeter, c2 is the specific heat capacity of water, and ΔT2 is the change in temperature of the water in the calorimeter.

The mass of the water in the calorimeter is 125.0 grams, and its initial temperature is 18.5°C. The final temperature of the mixture is 54.2°C, so:

ΔT2 = 54.2°C - 18.5°C = 35.7°C

The specific heat capacity of water is 4.18 J/g°C, so:

Q = 125.0 g × 4.18 J/g°C × 35.7°C

Q = 18713 J

Finally, we can use the law of conservation of energy to equate the two values of Q:

8.50 g × L + 3571 J = 18713 J

Solving for L, we get:

L = (18713 J - 3571 J) / 8.50 g

L = 1884 J/g

Therefore, the experimental value for the latent heat of vaporization of water is 1884 J/g.
