24. 1. Calculate the vapor pressure of a solution containing 440 g of carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) at 30°C. The P ° of H20 at 30°C is 33.5 torr. (molar masses: CO2= 44.01 g/mol, H2 O = 18.0 g/mol) 2. Calculate the freezing point of a solution containing 300g of sucrose (molar mass= 342 g/mol,) and 235g water H20 with freezing constant of 1.86 ° (H20 molar mass= 18 g/mol) 3. Calculate the vapor pressure of a solution containing 200 g of salt and water (H2O) at 25°C. The P ° of H20 at 25°C is 26.5 torr. (molar masses: NaCl= 58.01 g/mol, H2 O = 18.0 g/mol)


1. To calculate the vapor pressure of the solution, we first need to calculate the mole fraction of CO2 in the solution:

moles of CO2 = 440 g / 44.01 g/mol = 9.996 mol

moles of H2O = 1000 g / 18.0 g/mol = 55.56 mol

mole fraction of CO2 = moles of CO2 / (moles of CO2 + moles of H2O) = 9.996 / (9.996 + 55.56) = 0.152

Next, we can use Raoult's law to calculate the vapor pressure of the solution:

P = X_CO2 * P°_H2O

P = 0.152 * 33.5 torr

P ≈ 5.1 torr

Therefore, the vapor pressure of the solution containing 440 g of carbon dioxide and water at 30°C is approximately 5.1 torr.

2. We can use the freezing point depression equation to calculate the freezing point of the solution:

ΔTf = Kf * m

m = molality = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg)

moles of sucrose = 300 g / 342 g/mol = 0.877 mol

mass of H2O = 235 g / 1000 = 0.235 kg

molality = 0.877 mol / 0.235 kg = 3.73 m

ΔTf = 1.86 °C/m * 3.73 m = 6.95 °C

The freezing point of pure water is 0 °C, so the freezing point of the solution is:

freezing point = 0 °C - 6.95 °C = -6.95 °C

Therefore, the freezing point of the solution containing 300g of sucrose and 235g water with a freezing constant of 1.86 °C is approximately -6.95 °C.

3. To calculate the vapor pressure of the solution, we first need to calculate the mole fraction of salt in the solution:

moles of NaCl = 200 g / 58.01 g/mol = 3.448 mol

moles of H2O = 1000 g / 18.0 g/mol = 55.56 mol

mole fraction of NaCl = moles of NaCl / (moles of NaCl + moles of H2O) = 3.448 / (3.448 + 55.56) = 0.0588

Next, we can use Raoult's law to calculate the vapor pressure of the solution:

P = X_H2O * P°_H2O

P = (1 - X_NaCl) * P°_H2O

X_H2O + X_NaCl = 1

Solving for X_H2O and X_NaCl:

X_H2O = 1 - X_NaCl = 1 - 0.0588 = 0.9412

P = X_H2O * P°_H2O

P = 0.9412 * 26.5 torr

P ≈ 25.0 torr

Therefore, the vapor pressure of the solution containing 200 g of salt and water at 25°C is approximately 25.0 torr.

26. II. Task: Determine the molar mass Water (H₂O) Elements No. of Atoms Atomic Mass Total Mass Molar Mass (MM)=​


Explanation:

The molar mass of water (H2O) can be determined by adding up the atomic masses of each element present in the compound.

Water is composed of two hydrogen atoms (H) and one oxygen atom (O).

The atomic mass of hydrogen is 1.008 amu (atomic mass units).

The atomic mass of oxygen is 16.00 amu.

Therefore, the total mass of water is:

(2 x 1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 amu

And the molar mass (MM) is equal to the total mass in amu, so the molar mass of water is 18.016 g/mol.

How do I find molar mass?

The molar mass is the mass of a given chemical element or chemical compound (g) divided by the amount of substance (mol). The molar mass of a compound can be calculated by adding the standard atomic masses (in g/mol) of the constituent atoms.

However, molar mass can also be calculated by multiplying the atomic mass in amu by the molar mass constant (1 g/mol). To calculate the molar mass of a compound with multiple atoms, sum all the atomic mass of the constituent atoms.
