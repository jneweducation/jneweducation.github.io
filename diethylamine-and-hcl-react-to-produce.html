13. How many milliliters of 0.383 m hcl are needed to react with 16.2 g of caco3?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the volume of HCl solution needed by using dimensional analysis.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of CaCO₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of HCl.

The molar mass of CaCO₃ is 100.086 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{volume of HCl} & = \text{16.2 g} \: \text{CaCO}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CaCO}_3}{\text{100.084 g} \: \text{CaCO}_3} \times \frac{\text{2 mol HCl}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CaCO}_3} \times \frac{\text{1 L soln}}{\text{0.383 mol HCl}} \times \frac{\text{1000 mL soln}}{\text{1 L soln}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{845 mL}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 845 mL of HCl is needed to react with 16.2 g of CaCO₃.

Answer:

If you start with 4.50 grams of Magnesium (Mg), how many grams of Hydrogen gas (H2) will you theoretically produce? If you actually produce 0.25 grams, what is your percent yield?

The balanced equation tells us that we'll produce 1 mole of H2 for every 1 mole of Mg metal. So let's find out how many moles are in 4.5 grams of Mg. Divide the mass of Mg by it's molar mass (24.3 g/mole). That is 0.185 moles of Mg. Since the molar ratio is 1, we can set a conversion formula:

(1 mole H2)/(1 mole Mg)

So we should obtain 0.1875 moles of H2, assuming that there is sufficient hydrochloric acid (HCl). The theoretical yield of H2, in grams, can be found by multiplying the 0.1875 moles by the molar mass of H2 (2 grams/mole). We would expect, and love to find, 0.37g in our possession. Doesn't sound like much, but it makes a nice bang, when mixed with oxygen. But we only got 0.25 grams, a (0.25g/0.37g) 67.5% yield.

22. What volume (in ml) of 4.35 M HCl solution is needed to react with 18.5 of CaOH2


Neutralization is the reaction between an acid and base yielding water and salt. For this problem, HCl (hydrochloric acid) is the acid and Ca(OH)2 (calcium hydroxide) is the base. The equation of the neutralization for this is:

2 HCl + Ca(OH)2  CaCl2 + 2 H2O

Here, one can see 2 before HCl that means you need two moles of HCl to react with one mole of Ca(OH)2 (even though no number is indicated on the left of the Ca(OH)2 but the present compound is counted as 1) to yield one mole of CaCl2 (calcium chloride) and 2 moles of H2O (water). Moles is a chemistry term for atoms or molecules needed in a reaction.

For the problem, one is looking for the volume of HCl (in mL) needed to react with Ca(OH)2 when given with 4.35 M HCl (M stands for molarity that means moles of solute/L of solution) and 18.5 g of Ca(OH)2. The first thing to do is to solve for the moles of HCl in terms of Ca(OH)2.

18.5 g Ca(OH)2 x (1 mole of Ca(OH)2 )/(74.092 g Ca(OH)2 ) x (2 moles of HCl)/(1 mole of Ca(OH)2 ) = 0.49938 moles of HCl

One can get 74.093 g of Ca(OH)2 by adding the atomic weight of Ca (Calcium - 40.078), O (Oxygen - 15.999) and H (Hydrogen - 1.00794) and OH is multiplied by 2 (meaning 2 moles of it is needed for the reaction to occur and be balanced). (Note: It is acceptable to round off the values at the tenths or hundredths place but answers will differ a little.)


Next is solving for the volume of HCl using the equation of molarity.

Molarity (M) = (moles of solute )/(liter (L)of solution)

Volume of solution = (moles of solute )/Molarity

= ( 0.49938 moles HCl)/(4.35 M HCl)

= 0.1148 L or 114.8 mL (by using the conversion of 1 L = 1000 mL)


Answer: 114.8 mL of HCl is needed to react with 18.5 g of Ca(OH)2

27. If 0.86 mole of MnO2 and 48.2 g of HCl react, which reagent will be used up first? How many grams of Cl2 will be produced?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

• Which reagent will be used first?

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

MnO₂ + 4 HCl → MnCl₂ + Cl₂ + 2 H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of Cl₂ formed by each reactant.

• Using MnO₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of MnO₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of Cl₂.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{Cl}_2 & = 0.860 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{MnO}_2} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Cl}_2}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{MnO}_2}} \\ & = \text{0.860 mol} \: \text{Cl}_2 \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using HCl

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 4 moles of HCl is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of Cl₂. Note that the molar mass of HCl is 36.46 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{Cl}_2 & = 48.2 \: \cancel{\text{g HCl}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol HCl}}}{36.46 \: \cancel{\text{g HCl}}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Cl}_2}{4 \: \cancel{\text{mol HCl}}} \\ & = \text{0.330 mol} \: \text{Cl}_2 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Determine the limiting reagent.

Since HCl produced less number of moles of Cl₂ than MnO₂,

[tex]\boxed{\text{HCl will be used up first.}}[/tex]

------------------------------------------------------------

• How many grams of Cl₂ will be produced?

Note that the mass of a product that can be produced is dictated by the limiting reagent. Since HCl is the limiting reagent, the mass of Cl₂ (molar mass = 70.90 g/mol) that can be formed is

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{Cl}_2 & = 0.330 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{Cl}_2} \times \frac{\text{70.90 g} \: \text{Cl}_2}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{Cl}_2}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{23.4 g} \: \text{Cl}_2} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mass of Cl₂ that can be produced is 23.4 g
