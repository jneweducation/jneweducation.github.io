3. share your experience about general organic chemistry laboratory during this pandemic?​


Explanation:

During the COVID-19 pandemic, an at-home laboratory program was created and implemented for a section of the general chemistry course at the University of Southern California. The experiments were designed to only utilize safe household items and no special equipment. These laboratory activities, spanning over 4 weeks, focused on concepts usually covered in the final one-third of our second-semester chemistry laboratory, including pH, acid–base titrations, buffers, solubility, phase equilibria, and thermodynamics. In this article, we describe the design of the laboratories and our experience with this experiment, while also providing an assessment on how similar activities could be integrated profitably into a regular general chemistry course.

17. Proper waste disposal in the laboratory organic chemistry heavy metals


Proper waste disposal is a critical aspect of laboratory safety, particularly in the field of organic chemistry where hazardous chemicals are frequently used. Inadequate waste disposal can lead to serious consequences, including environmental contamination, injury or illness to laboratory personnel, and damage to equipment and facilities. In order to ensure the safety and health of those working in the laboratory, it is essential that proper waste disposal procedures be followed at all times.

One of the key aspects of proper waste disposal is the segregation of hazardous and non-hazardous waste. Hazardous waste, such as chemicals and solutions that are toxic, flammable, or reactive, should be separated from non-hazardous waste, such as paper and plastic, in order to minimize the risk of accidental contamination or injury. Proper containers, such as hazardous waste drums or bottles with screw-top lids, should be used to store hazardous waste until it can be disposed of safely.

Heavy metals, such as mercury, lead, and cadmium, can be especially hazardous and should be handled and stored carefully. These metals should be labeled and stored in designated areas, such as a fume hood or chemical storage cabinet, in order to minimize the risk of exposure. It is also important to follow local regulations for the disposal of heavy metals, as these may vary from one location to another.

In addition to segregating and storing hazardous waste properly, it is also important to follow the guidelines provided by the manufacturer or supplier of the chemical for its proper disposal. Some chemicals, such as highly reactive or toxic substances, may require special handling and disposal procedures. Failing to follow these guidelines can result in serious consequences, including environmental contamination and injury or illness to those handling the waste.

Overall, proper waste disposal is an essential aspect of laboratory safety in the field of organic chemistry. By following proper procedures and guidelines, laboratory personnel can minimize the risk of accidents, injury, and environmental contamination, and ensure that their work is conducted safely and responsibly.

19. List 10 other apparutuses made of glass found in the chemistry laboratory​


Answer:

1. Burettes - These are used to dispense exact quantities of liquid into another vessel.

2. Beakers - Simple containers used to hold samples and reagents.

3. Volumetric flasks - Similar to beakers, these are used to hold samples, but usually come in a conical or spherical shape with a tapering neck.

4. Condensers - Specifically used to cool heated liquid or gas.

5. Retorts - These are used for distillation purposes.

6. Funnels - The tapered neck of a funnel allows easy pouring of a liquid into a narrow orifice.

7. Petri dishes - Shallow dishes used to culture living cells.

8. Graduated Cylinders - Similar to beakers, these cylindrical vessels have volumetric markings to allow for monitoring of volume.

9. Vials - Small bottles used to store samples or reagents.

10. Slides - Used to hold items under a microscope for inspection and study.
