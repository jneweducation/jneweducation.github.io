Answer: N2 + 3 H2 = 2 NH3

28.0 g N2 + 6.02 g H2 = 24.0 g NH3

28.0/6.0 = 4.7

77.2/14.2 = 5.43

The system you describe has an excess of nitrogen; therefore, the hydrogen is the limiting reagent.

If the reaction of 6.02g H2 could, theoretically, produce 24 g N2, the reaction of 14.2 g H2 could, theoretically, produce 56.8 g NH3.

The problem is that the reaction of nitrogen and hydrogen to produce ammonia is, in practice, very hard to carry out. The reactants are fairly inert (“reluctant” to react) and a high temperature is needed to get the reaction going at a decent rate. At that high temperature, the ammonia tends to decompose to give the H2 and N2 starting materials. The reaction is usually carried out in a flow mechanism, allowing the NH3 to be removed and cooled as soon as it is produced. so the problem you’ve posed is an acceptable way to discuss limiting reagents and theoretical yield, but doesn’t describe a practical method of producing amonia.

19. What is the percent yield of NH3 if 23.5 grams NH3 is produced from 13.0 mol H2 and excess N2.


Given:

actual yield = 23.5 g

moles of H₂ = 13.0 mol

reactants: N₂ and H₂

product: NH₃

Required:

percent yield

Solution:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of NH₃.

molar mass of NH₃ = (14.01 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 3)

molar mass of NH₃ = 17.034 g/mol

Step 3: Calculate the theoretical yield of the reaction by using dimensional analysis (factor-label method).

Since [tex]\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_{2} \bumpeq \text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}[/tex], the theoretical yield of the reaction is

[tex]\text{theoretical yield} = \text{13.0 mol} \: \text{H}_{2} × \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}}{\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_{2}} × \frac{\text{17.034 g} \: \text{NH}_{3}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{theoretical yield = 147.628 g}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the percent yield.

[tex]\text{percent yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent yield} = \frac{\text{23.5 g}}{\text{147.628 g}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent yield} = 15.9\%}[/tex]

23. How many grams of ammonia (NH3) can be produced from the reaction of 30.0 grams of nitrogen gas (N2) and 25.0 grams of hydrogen gas (H2)?​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of NH₃ formed by each reactant.

• Using N₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of N₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of NH₃.

The molar mass of N₂ is 28.014 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{NH}_3 & = \text{30.0 g} \: \text{N}_2 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{N}_2}{\text{28.014 g} \: \text{N}_2} \times \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_3}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{N}_2} \\ & = \text{2.1418 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using H₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 3 moles of H₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of NH₃.

The molar mass of H₂ is 2.016 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{NH}_3 & = \text{25.0 g} \: \text{H}_2 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{H}_2}{\text{2.016 g} \: \text{H}_2} \times \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_3}{\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_2} \\ & = \text{8.2672 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the limiting reagent.

Since N₂ produced less amount of NH₃ than H₂,

Since N₂ produced less amount of NH₃ than H₂,N₂ is the limiting reagent.

Step 4: Calculate the mass of NH₃ formed.

The mass of the product that can be formed is dictated by the limiting reactant. In this case, we will start at the number of moles of NH₃ formed from the limiting reactant (N₂) which is equal to 2.1418 mol.

The molar mass of NH₃ is 17.031 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{NH}_3 & = \text{2.1418 mol} \: \text{NH}_3 \times \frac{\text{17.031 g} \: \text{NH}_3}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{NH}_3} \\ & = \boxed{\text{36.5 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mass of NH₃ that can be formed is 36.5 g.

26. . What is the maximum mass in grams of NH3 that can be produced by the reaction of1.0 g ofN2 with 3.0 g of H2 ?N2 (g) + H2 (g) NH3 (g)​


Strategy:

Because there are two reactants, this is likely to be a limiting reagent problem. The reactant that produces fewer moles is the limiting reagent. How do we convert from amount of reactant to amount of product? Perform this calculation for each reactant, then compare the moles of product, NH₃, formed.

Solution:

First, we need to write the balanced chemical equation for the formation of NH₃. The balanced chemical equation for this reaction is

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Next, carry out two sep-a-rate calculations to see which of the two reactants is the limiting reagent. Starting with 1.0 g of N₂, calculate the number of moles of NH₃ that could be produced if all the N₂ reacted. The conversions are

grams of N₂ → moles of N₂ → moles of NH₃

so that

[tex]moles \: of \: NH_{3} = 1.0 \: g \: N_{2} \times \frac{1 \: mol \: N_{2}}{28.01 \: g \: N_{2}} \times \frac{2 \: mol \: NH_{3}}{1 \: mol \: N_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]moles \: of \: NH_{3} = 0.0714 \: mol \: NH_{3}[/tex]

We calculate the number of moles of NH₃ formed from 3.0 g of H₂. The conversion steps are

grams of H₂ → moles of H₂ → moles of NH₃

and we write

[tex]moles \: of \: NH_{3} = 3.0 \: g \: H_{2} \times \frac{1 \: mol \: H_{2}}{2.016 \: g \: H_{2}} \times \frac{2 \: mol \: NH_{3}}{3 \: mol \: H_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]moles \: of \: NH_{3} = 0.9921 \: mol \: NH_{3}[/tex]

Therefore, N₂ is the limiting reagent because it produces a smaller amount of NH₃. The mass of NH₃ formed is

[tex]0.0714 \: mol \: NH_{3} \times \frac{17.03 \: g \: NH_{3}}{1 \: mol \: NH_{3}} = \blue{1.2 \: g \: NH_{3}}[/tex]

The mass of NH₃, which is equal to 1.2 g, is the maximum mass of NH₃ that can be produced by the reaction.
