Definition/Explanation Clues

Sometimes a word's or phrase's meaning is explained immediately after its use.

Example: "Haberdashery, which is a store that sells men’s clothing, is becoming more

common today.”

2. Restatement/Synonym Clues

Sometimes a hard word or phrase is said in a simple way.

Example: "Lou was sent to the haberdashery to find a new suit. He needed to wear

one for his uncle’s wedding."

Because the sentence says that Lou would find a suit at the haberdashery, then

it must be a place where clothes for men are sold.

3. Contrast/Antonym Clues

Sometimes a word or phrase is clarified by the presentation of the opposite meaning

somewhere close to its use. Look for signal words when applying context clues.

Example: "Lou wanted to go to the haberdashery, but Ann wanted to shop at the boutique.”

The signal word but tells the reader that an opposite thought is going to be stated.

4. Inference/General Context Clues

Sometimes a word or phrase is not immediately clarified within the same sentence.

Relationships, which are not directly apparent, are inferred or implied. The reader must

look for clues within, before, and after the sentence in which the word is used.

Example: “The haberdashery was Lou’s favorite place. He loved shopping for nice suits.

The people who worked there were so kind and helpful.”

17. Which of the following best describes the objective of an assurance engagement? a. To compare the company's information and policies to those of other entities. b. To improve the company's outcomes. c. To enhance the credibility of information in order to improve the likelihood that the information will meet the needs of an intended user. d. None of the above.


The purpose of the assurance engagement is to enhance the credibility of information in order to improve the likelihood that the information will meet the needs of an intended user (C). The goal of an assurance engagement is to gather enough relevant data to draw a conclusion, with reasonable or limited assurance, about whether the audited entity has complied with the necessary legal requirements (the "criteria") in all relevant ways. A practitioner, a responsible party, and the intended recipients of the assurance report are all involved in an assurance engagement. The information that the practitioner is relying on to make a judgment belongs to the responsible party.

The individual can have more faith in the subject being assured the higher the amount of assurance offered. However, in order for an audit team leader to offer a better level of assurance, they must lower the possibility that the subject of the audit contains a major misrepresentation. Components of a guarantee engagement:

A three-party relationship involving the practitioner, the responsible party, and the intended users.One thing that should be.Suitable criteria.Sufficient and precise evidence.An assurance report in a form appropriate to an engagement that provides reasonable assurance or an engagement that provides limited assurance.

"Islands in The Philippines"

There has always been a problem with the veracity of the data and statistics in our textbooks. The good news is that many textbooks in the difficult disciplines, including English, Filipino, and History, have been changed and, while occasionally still flawed, are usually regarded as having much better form and content than those in science and arithmetic.

History, in contrast to science and arithmetic, which are accurate, can occasionally be shaky, with one event's interpretation varying depending on the historian who wrote the book. The first stage in the reform of our textbooks does not even concern interpretation, but rather the outdated or conflicting "facts" that are found in them. What disturbs me is not that there are many diverse opinions, but rather that the textbooks have them. Our Hekasi, or history, texts must be at the very least factually accurate or at least current.

While Molina, a professor at the Royal and Pontifical University of Santo Tomas, uses 7,083 islands in his book "Catholicism in the Philippines," most of his textbooks use the same statistic, 7,100 islands.

The historical technique requires you to dig into the primary source. This random number, where did it originate from? Nobody seemed to be aware. With only 3,000 or so islands and islets having names, how confident are we that we can even create 7,000 islands and islets? I once requested some National Coast and Geodetic Survey and National Mapping Agency employees to provide me with the precise number at a party. The question caught their attention as well. I assumed—mistakenly, of course—that staff members from these agencies would know the precise number off-hand.

24. How can you tell if an author is favouring one side in his/her article?A. If he/she provides credible and reliable sources making the other look inherently weak.B. If he/she provides credible and reliable sources making both sides look inherently weak.C. He/she provides reliable sources making both sides unbelievableD. He/she provides credible sources making both sides believable​


The correct answer is A.

When an author is favoring one side in his/her article, he/she will likely use credible and reliable sources to make the other side look inherently weak or less credible. This can include cherry-picking evidence that supports their point of view, using emotional or loaded language to sway the reader's opinion, or ignoring or dismissing evidence that contradicts their argument.

Option B is less likely because if both sides are made to look inherently weak, it could lead to confusion and lack of credibility in the article.

Option C is also less likely because if both sides are made to look unbelievable, it could lead to the article being dismissed as biased or sensationalized.

Option D may also be correct, as an author who presents credible sources for both sides and makes a well-rounded argument can still have a perspective or favor one side. However, in most cases, an author who favors one side will still use credible sources to make the other side look weaker or less credible.
