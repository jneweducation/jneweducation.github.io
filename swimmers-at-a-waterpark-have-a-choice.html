2. Two swimmers having the same mass but stepping off on platforms of different heights. Swimmer A steps off the platform at a lower height. Which swimmer have more or less potential energy? Why?


Potential Energy

The swimmer that is on a platform at a lower height will have less potential energy while the swimmer that is on a higher platform will have more potential energy.

Explanation:

Potential energy is the object's energy at rest and is affected by mass, gravity, and height (PE = mgh). In the given question, since the swimmers have the same mass (m) and since the pull of gravity and acceleration due to gravity (g = 9.8 [tex]\frac{m}{s^2}[/tex]) is constant, the potential energy of the swimmers will be dependent on its position or height. The greater the height of the object, the greater will be the potential energy of the object.

8. five swimmers enter the competition four of the swimmers have had their turns there score are 9.8 second 9.75 s nine point 79 second and 9.81 set what is core must the last swimmer get in order to win the competition​


Answer:

9.7775 seconds

Step-by-step explanation:

To calculate the score that the last swimmer needs to win the competition, we first need to find the average score of the four swimmers who have already had their turns.

Average score = (9.8 + 9.75 + 9.79 + 9.81) / 4 = 9.7875 seconds

Since this is the average score, the last swimmer would need to swim faster than this time to win the competition.

To find the exact score the last swimmer needs to win, we can subtract a small margin from the average score, say 0.01 seconds, to account for any errors in timing or measurement.

So, the last swimmer would need to swim faster than:

9.7875 - 0.01 = 9.7775 seconds

Therefore, the score that the last swimmer needs to win the competition is less than 9.7775 seconds.

27. what does physics have to do with being a good swimmer?​


Answer:

Newton's Third Law of Motion states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Thus, swimmers must stroke downward in the water to stay afloat and propel forward. This movement is equal and opposite to the force the water exerts against the swimmer to stop them from moving.
