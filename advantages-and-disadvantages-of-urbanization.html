8. should jeepneys be phased out to relieve the traffic in the urban areas?what are the advantages and disadvantages of 1 phasing out jeepneys and 2 letting them stay?what are the consequences of each of these options? what else can ba done to further relieve traffic? essay please


Answer:

Advantages of Phasing Out Jeepneys:

1. Reduced traffic congestion in urban areas due to fewer vehicles on the roads.

2. Improved air quality in cities due to fewer exhaust emissions from jeepneys.

3. More efficient use of resources, as less fuel and energy will be used.

4. More modern public transportation options, such as buses and trains, can be utilized.

Disadvantages of Phasing Out Jeepneys:

1. Jeeps provide a low-cost form of transportation for many Filipinos, so their removal could lead to economic hardship for many of them.

2. Jeepney drivers could be left without jobs, as the industry is heavily reliant on jeepneys.

3. There may be an increase in the number of private cars on the roads, leading to even more traffic congestion.

Consequences of Phasing Out Jeepneys:

1. Reduced traffic congestion in cities due to fewer vehicles on the roads.

2. Improved air quality in cities due to fewer exhaust emissions from jeepneys.

3. Economic hardship for many Filipinos due to higher transportation costs, and job loss for jeepney drivers.

Consequences of Allowing Jeepneys to Stay:

1. Continued traffic congestion in cities due to the presence of jeepneys on the roads.

2. Continued air pollution in cities due to exhaust emissions from jeepneys.

3. Continued economic hardship for many Filipinos due to the low cost of jeepney transportation.

Other Ways to Further Relieve Traffic:

1. Increase public transportation options, such as buses and trains.

2. Improve infrastructure, such as widening roads and constructing new highways.

3. Implement a tax incentive program to encourage people to use public transportation instead of private cars.

4. Implement stricter traffic laws to discourage reckless driving.

5. Improve traffic management by installing traffic lights and installing sensors to monitor traffic flow.

10. In your opinion, what are the advantages of planting or urban gardening in your daily life​


Answer:

1. Learn a unique skill

People today have forgotten where their food comes from. We don’t know what it takes to grow healthy, nutritious food. Urban gardening helps you get educated about growing food. That’s a unique skill to have in today’s world.

You can educate your children about sustainable food and the health benefits. That’s something worth learning and teaching.

2. Grow food in a limited space

Urban gardening teaches you that you don’t need a lot of space to grow your own food. There are techniques like vertical gardening, container gardening, rooftop gardening, and hydroponic gardening that utilize space well.

Urban gardening helps you make the best use of space while growing all the food that you want.

3. Reduce food insecurity

Around the world, urban areas face the constant issue of food deserts, or areas where healthy food is either not available or not affordable to the area’s residents. Urban gardening can help alleviate food desserts by providing people the opportunity to grow and harvest their own fresh and nutritious food at a fraction of what the same produce would otherwise cost in a grocery store.

4. Grow high-quality food

The problem with mass-produced food is that it is often loaded with chemicals and pesticides to increase production and shelf-life. With urban gardening, you don’t have to worry about this. You can grow organic food without such chemicals. You have control over the growing conditions and the water, soil, and compost you want to use.

You can choose to grow the unique heirloom varieties of food that are high-quality and delicious but may be susceptible to disease if mass-produced. You can pluck the fresh fruits and vegetables whenever they are ready for harvest, so you don’t need to worry about the shelf-life.

15. why is it disadvantage of for trade and transport services to overcrowd in urban center like metro manila?​


Pure Answer and Explanation: Cities exist precisely because trade and transport (and governance and education and cultural services) converge and become more efficient as they concentrate in a certain area. The question, however, already begins from a negative assumption of “overcrowding” and necessarily implies that it is a “disadvantage”.

The proper question would be to ask whether it is possible to concentrate trade and transport without disadvantages to the city. And yes, it is possible. But it requires radical solutions in terms of how land is owned and used in Metro Manila.

You will notice that Metro Manila is actually a relatively flat city… perhaps 80 to 90% of structures are not more than 3 or 4 stories tall (you can go up to Antipolo and see for yourself). The population of the metropolis is so spread out that its suburbs spill into neighboring provinces, such that when that population goes to work during the day, the population of the Metro Manila area itself effectively doubles. High rise apartments and offices are the exception, and not the norm. On the other hand, more efficient cities - Singapore or Hong Kong, for instance - all tend to build up, rather than out. The differences are a result of the laws on land ownership, cultural preferences for owning land, the political economy of land owning oligarchs, and lack of effective and enforceable zoning.

The fact that the city is flat means that it is less efficient in using infrastructure. More roads, train lines, utilities etc. need to be built for less people in a given area, making it more costly to build and maintain the infrastructure (which also helps drive a feedback loop that favors cars over trains), while the low tax collection efficiency (and other governance problems) only makes things worse. Note how much fewer train lines we have compared to other Asian countries, and how the few train lines that we have are overused and yet need to operate under a heavy subsidy from the government.

So strictly speaking, the city is not really “overcrowded”. It seems overcrowded because of the poor design of land use policies (among many other socio-economic and legal problems). It can be less overcrowded while in fact increasing population density if we build up, while even freeing up space for parks and other public amenities. If we built up, trade and transport services could actually be cheaper, and more efficient to build and operate. If people didn’t live in Bulacan, Rizal or Cavite but actually lived in cheaper high rise housing within the city, and relied more on functional trains to get around, the perceived overcrowding would be much less severe.
