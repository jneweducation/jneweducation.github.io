2. What is the percent-by-mass,%(m/m),concentration of sucrose in a solution made by dissolving 7.6g of sucrose in 83.4g of water


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{7.6 g} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{83.4 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the percent by mass.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%(m/m) & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solute}} + mass_{\text{solvent}}} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{\text{7.6 g}}{\text{7.6 g + 83.4 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{8.35\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the percent by mass concentration of sucrose is 8.35%.

SOLUTION:

Step 1: Determine the mass of solute (sucrose) and solvent (water).

Assuming that the mass of solution is 100 g, there are 25 g of sucrose in 75 g of water.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{25 g} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{75 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of each component.

The molar masses of sucrose and water are 342.297 g/mol and 18.015 g/mol, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{25 g}}{\text{342.297 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.073036 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For solvent

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solvent}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solvent}}}{MM_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{75 g}}{\text{18.015 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{4.1632 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mole fraction of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} X_{\text{ethanol}} & = \frac{n_{\text{ethanol}}}{n_{\text{methanol}} + n_{\text{ethanol}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.073036 mol}}{\text{0.073036 mol + 4.1632 mol}} \\ & = 0.017241 & = \boxed{0.0172} \end{aligned}[/tex]

4. identify the following solutions: a solution containing 200 grams of sucrose (or table sugar) in 100ml of water at 25°c is saturated solution​


Answer:

A supersaturated solution contains more solute at a given temperature than is needed to form a saturated solution.

Increased temperature usually increases the solubility of solids in liquids.

For example, the solubility of glucose at 25 °C is 91 g/100 mL of water. The solubility at 50 °C is 244 g/100 mL of water.

If we add 100 g of glucose to 100 mL water at 25 °C, 91 g dissolve. Nine grams of solid remain on the bottom. We have a saturated solution.

Explanation:

If we now heat the mixture to 50 °C, the remaining 9 g of glucose will dissolve. At the new temperature, the solubility limit in 100 mL of water is 244 g glucose. With only 100 g of glucose dissolved, the solution is now unsaturated.

If we next cool the mixture back to 25 °C, 9 g of glucose should precipitate from solution.

If glucose crystals do not form, the system has more dissolved glucose (100 g) than it can hold at 25 °C (91 g). We have a supersaturated solution.

The first step in the formation of crystals is nucleation. This is when the solute molecules arrange themselves to form crystals.

Sometimes this happens at once. If it doesn't, we have a supersaturated solution. This is an unstable situation.

A piece of dust or a small crystal of the solute, a seed crystal, provides a template for crystallization of the excess solute. The excess solute starts to form crystals on the nuclei.


5. what is the molality of a 1 M aqueous solution of sucrose , C12H22O11 , if the density of the solution is 1.0638 g/mL ?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M = 1 \: M = \text{1 mol/L} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{342.3 g/mol} \\ & d_{\text{solution}} = \text{1.0638 g/mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁).

Assume that the volume of solution is 1 L or 1000 mL.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = (M)(V_{\text{solution}}) \\ & = \text{(1 mol/L)(1 L)} \\ & = \text{1 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solute}} & = (n_{\text{solute}})(MM_{\text{solute}}) \\ & = \text{(1 mol)(342.3 g/mol)} \\ & = \text{342.3 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the mass of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solution}} & = (d_{\text{solution}})(V_{\text{solution}}) \\ & = \text{(1.0638 g/mL)(1000 mL)} \\ & = \text{1063.8 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the mass of solvent (water).

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solvent}} & = mass_{\text{solution}} - mass_{\text{solute}} \\ & = \text{1063.8 g} - \text{342.3 g} \\ & = \text{721.5 g} \\ & = \text{0.7215 kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 6: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{1 mol}}{\text{0.7215 kg}} \\ & = \text{1.386 mol/kg} \\ & = \boxed{1.386 \: m} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molality of a 1 M sucrose solution is 1.386 m.

✏️PERCENT BY MASS

==============================

Problem: What is the percent-by-mass, %(m/m), concentration of sucrose in a solution made by dissolving 7.6 g of sucrose in 83.4 g of water?

Solution: In this problem, sucrose is the solute and water is the solvent. Using the formula,

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} \times 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \dfrac{\text{7.6 g}}{\text{7.6 g + 83.4 g}} \times 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent by mass} = 8.35\%}[/tex]

Therefore, the percent by mass concentration of sucrose in a solution is 8.35%.

18. How many grams of sucrose, C12H22O11 are required to prepare 2.50 L of solution whose concentration is 0.650 M?


MOLARITY

Molarity refers to the number of moles of a certain substance for every liter of solution. It is also referred to as the molar concentration, and is measured in molar, indicated by an uppercase letter M. From this definition, we can formulate a working equation which we can use in solving word problems involving this kind of concept:

[tex]M = \frac{n}{Vol_{solution}}[/tex]

where M refers to the molarity of the solution, n refers to the number of moles of the solute and Vol_solution refers to the volume of the solution in liters.

Let us try this example:

How many grams of sucrose, [tex]C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}[/tex] are required to prepare 2.50 L of solution whose concentration is 0.650 M?

Solution:

Sucrose is the chemical name for sugar.

Now, the solution for this problem can be obtained by listing down first the given:

0.650 M = which refers to the molar concentration of the substance2.50 L = which refers to the volume of the solution

After listing, we will be going to determine the required, which is the mass of the sucrose in grams.

To solve this, let us get first the number of moles that sucrose has which is being mixed to the solution:

[tex]M = \frac{n}{Vol_{solution}}\\0.65 = \frac{n}{2.5 L}\\ n = (0.65)(2.5)\\ n = 1.625[/tex]

Thus, sucrose has 1.625 moles. But this does not solve the problem yet. We still have to know the molar mass of sucrose. Going back to its empirical formula, it has 12 atoms of carbon, 22 atoms of hydrogen and 11 atoms of oxygen. Carbon's atomic mass is 12 g. Hydrogen's atomic mass is 1 g. Oxygen's atomic mass is 16 g. So:

molar mass of sucrose = 12x12 + 22x1 + 11x16 = 144 + 22 + 176 = 342

Therefore, the molar mass of sucrose is 342 grams per mol. We will just multiply this to the number of moles we solved above.

mass of sucrose = 1.625 mol x 342 g/mol = 555.75 g

Hence, in a 0.65-M concentration of a 2.5-L solution, sucrose is 555.75 g.

24. Which of the following aqueous solutions should have the lowest freezing point? a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl b. 200 mL of 0.10 m CaCl2 c. 500 mL of 0.10 m sucrose, C12H22O11 d. 100 mL of 0.20 m sucrose Answer FAST PLEASE


Aqueous solutions should have the lowest freezing point : a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl

Further explanation

Solution properties are the properties of a solution that don't depend on the type of solute but only on the concentration of the solute.

Solution properties of electrolyte solutions differ from non-electrolyte solutions because electrolyte solutions contain a greater number of particles because electrolytes break down into ions. So the Solution properties of electrolytes are greater than non-electrolytes.

The term is used in the Solution properties

1. molal

that is, the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent

[tex]\large {\boxed {\bold {m = mole. \dfrac {1000} {mass \: of \: solvent (in \: grams)}}}[/tex]

2. freezing point

Solutions from volatile substances have a higher boiling point and lower freezing points than the solvent

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = T_fsolvent-T_fsolution[/tex]

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = K_f \times m[/tex]

Kf = molal freezing point constant

m = molal solution

From the solutions below

a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl

b. 200 mL of 0.10 m CaCl₂

c. 500 mL of 0.10 m sucrose

d. 100 mL of 0.20 m sucrose

which includes electrolyte solutions are NaCl and CaCl₂, while non-electrolytes are sucrose

For electrolyte solutions there is a van't Hoff factor = i

i = 1 + (n-1) α

n = number of ions from the electrolyte

so the freezing point formula becomes:

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = K_f \times m \times i[/tex]

Because of the value of i (a Van't Hoff factor), this electrolyte solution produces a freezing point that is greater than the non-electrolyte solution.

So the choice of answers leads to the higher ΔTf which is the electrolyte solution, here is NaCl and CaCl₂

From the freezing point formula, it can be seen that  ΔTf depends on the values ​​of Kf and m (molal)

So that which has the highest ΔTf (=the lowest freezing point) is a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl, because it has a molal higher than option B
