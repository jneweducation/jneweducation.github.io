Physical changes are limited to changes that result in a difference in display without changing the composition. Some common changes (but not limited to) are:

TextureColorTemperatureShapeChange of State (Boiling Point and Melting Point are significant factors in determining this change.)Physical properties include many other aspects of a substance. The following are (but not limited to) physical properties.LusterMalleabilityAbility to be drawn into a thin wireDensityViscositySolubilityMassVolume

Any change in these physical properties is referred to as a physical change. For further information, please refer to Properties of Matter.

Chemical changes, on the other hand, are quite different. A chemical change occurs when the substance's composition is changed. When bonds are broken and new ones are formed a chemical change occurs.

The following are indicators of chemical changes:

Change in TemperatureChange in ColorNoticeable Odor (after reaction has begun)Formation of a PrecipitateFormation of Bubbles

Explanation:

1. A boulder on the shore is slowly broken apart by the force of water - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the force of the water is physically breaking the rock apart.

2. Earthworm makes tunnels in the ground which may lead to the breakdown of stones and rocks - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the tunnels made by the earthworms can loosen the rocks and cause them to break apart.

3. Some plants release chemicals which loosen the rocks and cause them to break - this is an example of chemical weathering because the chemicals released by the plants are changing the composition of the rock.

4. Water inside the rocks freezes and melts again - this is an example of mechanical weathering because as the water freezes and expands, it can cause the rock to crack or break apart.

5. The chemical composition of rocks changes when acid is produced by the roots of some plants - this is an example of chemical weathering because the acid produced by the roots of the plants is changing the composition of the rock.

6. Minerals in rocks dissolve - this is an example of chemical weathering because the minerals in the rock are dissolving due to a chemical process.

7. Tree roots can grow into cracks and break rock apart - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the roots are physically breaking apart the rock.

8. As the wind blows, it carries sand or small rock particles that scratch the rock's surface - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the sand or rock particles carried by the wind are physically scratching the rock's surface.

9. As the rocks expand, the cracks become deeper and new cracks are produced - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the expansion of the rock is physically causing cracks to form.

10. Rocks exposed to the intense heat of the sun crack and crumble when rain falls or soak in the ground - this is an example of mechanical weathering because the extreme temperature changes are causing the rock to crack and crumble.
