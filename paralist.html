10. 4 paragraphIntroductionStructuralistmoralist, Marxist, moralist conclusion about the hunchback of Notre Dame ​


Answer:

The Hunchback of Notre Dame is a classic novel written by French author Victor Hugo in 1831. The novel explores themes such as social injustice, morality, and the human condition, making it a popular subject for literary analysis. Over the years, the novel has been examined through various critical lenses, including structuralist, moralist, and Marxist perspectives.

Structuralism is a critical approach that focuses on the underlying structures and patterns in literature, rather than the content itself. When applied to The Hunchback of Notre Dame, a structuralist critic might analyze the novel's use of imagery and symbolism, or examine the relationships between the characters to uncover hidden meanings and themes.

In contrast, a moralist critic would approach the novel from a moral or ethical standpoint, examining the actions and motivations of the characters in light of their beliefs about right and wrong. A moralist might examine the novel's portrayal of justice and the moral implications of the characters' actions, particularly the way in which they treat Quasimodo, the novel's titular hunchback.

Marxist critics would analyze The Hunchback of Notre Dame from a social and economic perspective, exploring the ways in which the novel reflects the power dynamics and class struggles of nineteenth-century France. They might examine the novel's portrayal of the aristocracy and the poor, or analyze the way in which the novel critiques the capitalist system.

In conclusion, The Hunchback of Notre Dame has been the subject of critical analysis from a variety of perspectives, including structuralist, moralist, and Marxist approaches. Each of these critical lenses provides a unique way of understanding the novel and its themes, shedding light on different aspects of its complex narrative. Ultimately, the novel's enduring popularity and continued critical examination attest to its status as a literary masterpiece.

15. 15-20. Compare and contrast Moralistic approach and Reader-response approach Moralistic approach ​


Explanation:

The moralistic approach and reader-response approach are two different methods of literary analysis that focus on different aspects of a piece of literature.

The moralistic approach to literary analysis emphasizes the moral lessons and values that can be gleaned from a text. This approach assumes that literature has an inherent moral purpose, and seeks to identify that purpose through close analysis of the text. The moralistic approach often involves a search for universal truths and values that can be applied to the reader's own life. It may also involve a judgment of the characters' actions and motivations based on a moral code or standard.

On the other hand, the reader-response approach to literary analysis focuses on the reader's subjective experience of a text. This approach assumes that the meaning of a text is not fixed or objective, but is constructed by the reader as they engage with the text. The reader-response approach emphasizes the importance of the reader's personal experiences, beliefs, and cultural context in shaping their interpretation of the text. It may involve an exploration of the reader's emotional or psychological response to the text, as well as an analysis of the reader's assumptions and biases.

In summary, the moralistic approach emphasizes the moral lessons and values of a text, while the reader-response approach emphasizes the reader's subjective experience and interpretation of the text.
