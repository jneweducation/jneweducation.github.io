4. story behind bird in space​


Answer:

From the 1920s to the 1940s, the theme of a bird in flight preoccupied Brancusi. He concentrated on the animals' movement, rather than their physical attributes. In Bird in Space, the sculptor eliminated wings and feathers, elongated the swell of the body, and reduced the head and beak to a slanted oval plane.

Answer:

"Bird in Space" is a series of sculptures created by Romanian artist Constantin Brancusi between 1919 and 1940. The sculpture depicts a stylized bird in flight, with a long, slender form that emphasizes movement and fluidity. The series was created in several different materials, including marble, bronze, and brass.

Brancusi was inspired to create the "Bird in Space" series after a trip to Paris in 1903, where he was exposed to the work of the Cubist and Futurist artists of the time. He was also influenced by the traditional sculpture of his homeland, Romania, which often featured stylized birds and animals.

The "Bird in Space" series was originally met with controversy, as many critics and art experts at the time did not consider it to be a true sculpture. This was because the sculptures did not have a clear base or pedestal, and the forms were so abstract that they were not easily recognizable as birds.

However, Brancusi continued to create his "Bird in Space" sculptures, refining and simplifying the forms over time. Eventually, his work became widely recognized and celebrated for its unique and innovative approach to sculpture. Today, the "Bird in Space" series is considered a masterpiece of modern sculpture and a symbol of the artistic experimentation of the early 20th century.

“Shadow" is the story about a learned man and his shadow, his attempt to visualize and fulfil his own writings about Beauty, Truth and Goodness, and it is the story of the act of writing itself: the storyteller's attempt to maintain the potential for Poetry in a reality dominated by appearances.

Genre: Literary fairy tale

The Shadow is an exemplary story in Andersen's darker fairy tales. Throughout the tale, the writer is portrayed as a moral person, concerned with the good and true in the world. But as it says, the people around him are not much interested in his feelings on the subject. Indeed, his shadow says he does not see the world as it truly is.

The shadow claims to have seen all that is in the world, but does not own a soul himself. He strongly desires to own a shadow of himself, and later asks his former master to reverse the roles on their trip. When the learned man finally realises how far his shadow has degraded, it is already too late.

The ending is especially bleak for a fairy tale, as Andersen suggests that it is not always good that triumphs, and that evil does indeed have a powerful grip over the good and just.

14. what is the science behind the gun​


Answer:

The Physics Behind Guns

Although guns may not be everyday things for many of us, gun recoil is certainly something we're aware of (at least those of us that don't make Hollywood action films with guns whose recoil would instantly kill the person firing them!). Gun recoil is a result of momentum conservation, which is an extremely important fundamental principle. Newton was talking about momentum conservation when he wrote "every action has an equal and opposite reaction".

However, the physics behind all guns remain the same. Weapons such as cannons, shotguns and rifles, work on the basic idea of conservation of momentum and the change in energy from potential to kinetic.

17. story behind the song pamulinawen


Story behind the song pamulinawen

Pamulinawen is a popular old Ilocan folk song, possibly pre-Spanish, about a hardened-hearted girl who doesn't need a plea from her lover. It's a matter of courtesy and love.

Pamulinawen is a Filipino folk song (Ilocan) that tells of courting a girl with a heart of stone. Pamulinawen is her name.  

Pamulinawen is translated as an alabaster, a very hard stone, symbolizing the woman's resistance to the courteous gestures of her lover. In this light-hearted, courteous song, the lover pleads to be noticed, but places more emphasis on the delight of his beloved's thoughts. Like many  folk songs, Pamulinawen has different versions, lyrics and exact melody.

25. explain the science behind plane travel​


A plane's engines are designed to move it forward at high speed. That makes air flow rapidly over the wings, which throw the air down toward the ground, generating an upward force called lift that overcomes the plane's weight and holds it in the sky. ... The wings force the air downward and that pushes the plane upward.24

Answer:

Airplane wings are shaped to make air move faster over the top of the wing. When air moves faster, the pressure of the air decreases. So the pressure on the top of the wing is less than the pressure on the bottom of the wing. The difference in pressure creates a force on the wing that lifts the wing up into the air.

30. People behind the development of sociology as a science​


Answer:Auguste ComteHerbert SpencerKarl MarxEmile Durkheim

Explanation:

All 5 of these early sociologists contributed something important to sociology that has made it the recognized scientific field it is today.

August Comte is considered the Father of Sociology because he coined the name of the field - sociology, the study of society. Comte pioneered one of the most influential sociological theories that put sociology on the map as a legitimate field. He believed that all societies went through phases: from religious to metaphysical to scientific. He believed that the best societies based themselves on scientific facts, not myths and religions.

Herbert Spencer also made several important contributions to sociology as a science. He believed that society was comparable to an organism, where various parts (like education and family) had specific functions that kept the society alive, just like how organs keep living things alive. He was also the first to coin the term “survival of the fittest”.

Karl Marx believed that it was conflict, not harmony, that defined all societies. Specifically, he believed that all societies had some form of class conflict, or conflict between the rich (the bourgeoisie) and the working class/poor (the proletariat). This belief that one thing underlies all societies is a mark of his attempt to make sociological study more scientific.

Emile Durkheim was the first man to ever use scientific methods to support his sociological research. While theorists before him simply studied society and created theories, Durkheim believed that there were patterns of behavior (or "social facts") present in all societies that should be studied through scientific and data-driven methods. He is most known for his study on suicide.

All of these men with their ground-breaking theories have paved the way for students in sociology to study society through a scientific lens.
