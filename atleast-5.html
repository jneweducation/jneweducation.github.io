2. list atleast 5 abnormalities during mitosis and atleast 5 abnormalities during meiosis​


Abnormalities during mitosis:

1. Chromosome non-disjunction: Failure of chromosomes to properly separate during anaphase resulting in an uneven distribution of chromosomes in daughter cells.

2. Chromosome breakage: Damage or breakage of chromosome(s) during cell division leading to rearrangements or loss of genetic material.

3. Chromosome fusion: The fusion of two or more chromosomes leading to an abnormal chromosome number.

4. Chromosome deletion: Removal of a segment of chromosome(s) leading to a loss of genetic material.

5. Chromosome translocation: Exchange of genetic material between non-homologous chromosomes.

Abnormalities during meiosis:

1. Nondisjunction: Failure of chromosomes to properly separate during meiosis, leading to an uneven distribution of chromosomes in gametes.

2. Polyploidy: The presence of extra sets of chromosomes in cells due to errors in meiosis.

3. Aneuploidy: The presence of an abnormal number of chromosomes in cells due to errors in meiosis.

4. Chromosome inversion: The reversal of a segment of chromosome(s) leading to a change in gene order.

5. Chromosome fragmentation: Breakage of a chromosome into smaller fragments during meiosis.

20. atleast 5 contribution of medicine​


Answer:

1. METRODORA, THE FIRST WOMAN TO WRITE A MEDICAL TEXT

Little is known about Metrodora, an ancient Greek physician who likely lived sometime between the third and fifth centuries CE—except that she’s credited for being the first-known woman to write a medical text. Called On the Diseases and Cures of Women, or On Women’s Diseases, it outlined various topics related to women’s health (including gynecology), and listed various herbal remedies. Other Greek and Roman physicians relied on Metrodora’s work, and it was also referenced in Medieval Europe.

2. JAMES BLUNDELL, THE FIRST MAN TO PERFORM A SUCCESSFUL HUMAN-TO-HUMAN BLOOD TRANSFUSION

In 1818, a British obstetrician named James Blundell performed the first successful human-to-human blood transfusion. One of his patients suffered from postpartum hemorrhage, so Blundell used a syringe to extract several ounces of blood from her husband’s arm, and transferred it to the suffering mother. Blundell would go on to perform more transfusions—half of them effective—between 1825 and 1830, and he also published his findings and developed medical equipment for the procedure.
