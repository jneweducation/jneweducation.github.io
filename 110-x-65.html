The given information shows the demand and supply equations for a certain product. The demand equation is Qd = 150 - P, where Qd is the quantity demanded and P is the price. The supply equation is Qs = -60 + 2P, where Qs is the quantity supplied.

To find the quantity demanded and quantity supplied at different prices, we can substitute the values of P into the demand and supply equations and solve for Qd and Qs. For example, at a price of P = 40, the quantity demanded is Qd = 150 - P = 150 - 40 = 110, and the quantity supplied is Qs = -60 + 2P = -60 + 2*40 = 80.

We can do this for all the given prices to find the corresponding values of Qd and Qs. The resulting table is shown below:

P |Qd |Qs |

40 | 110 | 80 |

50 | 100 | 100 |

60 | 90 | 120 |

65 | 85 | 130 |

100 | 50 | 200 |

As we can see from the table, the quantity demanded and quantity supplied are not always equal at a given price. For example, at a price of P = 40, the quantity demanded is 110 and the quantity supplied is 80, so there is a surplus of 30 units. Similarly, at a price of P = 65, the quantity demanded is 85 and the quantity supplied is 130, so there is a shortage of 45 units. It is only at a price of P = 50 that the quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied, which means that the market is in equilibrium at that price.
