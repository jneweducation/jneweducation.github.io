15. natural opening in the surface of the earth where molten rocks,hot gases,smoke and ash are ejected.


Explanation:

A volcano is defined as an opening in the Earth's crust through which lava, ash, and gases erupt. The term also includes the cone-shaped landform built by repeated eruptions over time.

What is a volcano?

A volcano is a opening in the earth's crust that allows lava, volcanic ash, and gases to escape. Volcanic eruptions are fueled in part by dissolved gas pressure, similar to how escaping gases pull the cork out of a bottle of champagne. Liquid magma containing dissolved gases grows into fractures in the Earth's surface under a volcano. The pressure reduces as the magma increases, causing the gases to form bubbles. When magma (lava) approaches the atmosphere, its behavior is determined by its gas content as well as its chemical composition. Lavas with low silica content have low viscosities and flow naturally, allowing any trapped gases to escape gradually, while lavas with high silica content are more viscous (resistant to flow), stopping any trapped gases from gradually escape.

How can we tell when a volcano will erupt and how do we prepare for an eruption?

Volcanic eruptions are almost always accompanied by days, weeks, months, or years of alert activity reflecting magma movement or pressurization, especially at volcanoes that have not erupted in centuries or longer (such as all of Canada's volcanoes). Earthquakes (which may be observable only with seismographs), ground deformation (which may be detectable only with surveying devices, GPS instruments, or satellite imagery), carbon releases, and heat anomalies, as well as gravitational, magnetic, and geoelectrical phenomena, are examples of indications of turmoil. These phenomena are readily detectable, and may thus be used in volcano tracking – the systematic compilation, study, and evaluation of data prior to, after, and after eruptions in order to make short-term (days to weeks) predictions of eruptions or improvements in eruptions. The ability to accurately track volcanoes is important for minimizing the threats posed by volcanic hazards..

Most volcanic hazards just those areas within a few kilometers of a volcano are affected. Volcanic ash, on the other hand, can have an effect on areas thousands of kilometers away from the source. Volcanic ash differs from soft ash created by wood burning in that it is heavy, abrasive, mildly corrosive, and electrically charged, and it does not dissolve in water.
