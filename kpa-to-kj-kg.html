5. If a 3 m3 tank contains 45 kg of oxygen at 50°c, what is the gage pressure in the tank? the specific gas constant of oxygen is r=0.25983 kj/kg-k.


Answer:

1370.5 kPa.

Explanation:

To calculate the gauge pressure in the tank, we can use the ideal gas law, which relates the pressure, volume, temperature, and number of moles of a gas:

PV = nRT

where P is the absolute pressure of the gas, V is the volume of the tank, n is the number of moles of the gas, R is the specific gas constant, and T is the absolute temperature of the gas.

First, we need to calculate the number of moles of oxygen in the tank. We can use the mass of the oxygen and its molar mass to find the number of moles:

n = m/M

where m is the mass of the oxygen and M is the molar mass of oxygen (32 g/mol).

m = 45 kg = 45000 g

M = 32 g/mol

n = 45000 g / 32 g/mol = 1406.25 mol

Next, we need to convert the temperature to absolute temperature in Kelvin:

T = 50°C + 273.15 = 323.15 K

Now we can use the ideal gas law to solve for the absolute pressure:

P = nRT/V

P = (1406.25 mol)(0.25983 kJ/kg-K)(323.15 K)/(3 m^3) = 1471.8 kPa (absolute pressure)

Finally, we can convert the absolute pressure to gauge pressure by subtracting the atmospheric pressure. Assuming standard atmospheric pressure of 101.3 kPa, we get:

P_gauge = P_absolute - P_atm = 1471.8 kPa - 101.3 kPa = 1370.5 kPa

Therefore, the gauge pressure in the tank is 1370.5 kPa.

An isothermal process is a thermodynamic process during which the temperature of the process remains constant. It is also known as a constant temperature process. The work done during the isothermal process is calculated by the given formula:

[tex]W=P_1V_1\ln\left(\dfrac{P_1}{P_2} \right)[/tex]

Therefore, the work throughout the process is

[tex]W=(500\text{ kPa})(0.1\text{ m}^3)\ln\left(\dfrac{500\text{ kPa}}{100\text{ kPa}} \right)\\\boxed{W=80.47\text{ kJ}}[/tex]


7. There are supplied 400 kj per cycle to an ideal diesel engine operating on 500 g air, suction pressure, and temperature of 100 kpa and 50c. at the end of compression, the pressure rises to 4000 kpa. determine the compression ratio


Answer:

To determine the compression ratio, we need to find the ratio of the volume of the cylinder during the compression stroke to the volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke.

The volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke can be calculated using the ideal gas law:

PV = nRT

Where:

P = pressure (100 kPa)

V = volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke

n = number of moles of air (0.005 moles, since the mass of air is 500 g and the molar mass of air is 28.97 g/mol)

R = ideal gas constant (8.31 J/mol*K)

T = temperature (50 C, or 323 K)

Solving for V, we get:

V = (nRT)/P = (0.0058.31323)/100 = 0.0136 m^3

The volume of the cylinder during the compression stroke can be calculated in a similar manner, using the pressure at the end of the compression stroke (4000 kPa) instead of the suction pressure:

V = (nRT)/P = (0.0058.31323)/4000 = 0.00034 m^3

The compression ratio is then:

V_compression/V_intake = (0.00034 m^3)/(0.0136 m^3) = 2.5

So the compression ratio is 2.5.
