Answer

10lb=4540g approx. 1in=2.54cm approx. 0.0808 in=0.2052cm approx. Radius of wire=0.1026cm. Cross-sectional area of wire=π(0.1026)²=0.03308sq cm approx.

Density=mass/volume, volume=0.03308x cc, where x is the length in cm.

Mass=4540g, so density 2.70=4540/0.03308x, and x=4540/(2.70×0.03308)=50829cm=50829/100=508.29m.

7. What amount of heat (in Joules) required to raise the temperature of 200.0 g of aluminum by 10°C? The specific heat of aluminum is 0.21 cal/g°C.


Here, we're relating a few things: (1) the mass of the substance, (2) its specific heat, and (3) its temperature change with the equation,

q=mCsΔT

Before we start, that specific heat is all kinds of wrong, they must be positive. What that is saying is that the substance is giving off energy in order to increase its temperature, that doesn't make sense! First, let's translate that to J for an easier calculation:

0.21 cal/g ⋅ ° C ⋅ 4.184 J/cal ≈ (0.879 J )

q = 200.0g ⋅ 0.879 J/g ⋅ ° C ⋅  10 ° C

q = 175.8 J  ≈  1.8  ⋅  10 *2 J
