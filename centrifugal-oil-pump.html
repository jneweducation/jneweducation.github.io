Answer:

The gravitational potential energy gained by the fuel oil when pumped to a height of 20 m is 78,400 J.

Step-by-step explanation:

The gravitational potential energy gained by the fuel oil when pumped to a height of 20 m can be calculated using the formula:

[tex]\sf\qquad\dashrightarrow GPE = mgh[/tex]

where:

m is the mass of the fuel oilg is the acceleration due to gravityh is the height gained

First, we need to convert the given volume of 500 liters to mass. Since the density of fuel oil is 0.8 kg/L, the mass of the fuel oil is:

[tex]\begin{aligned}\sf:\implies m& =\sf V \times d \\& =\sf 500 \times 0.8\\& = \sf 400\: kg\end{aligned}[/tex]

Next, we can calculate the gravitational potential energy gained by the fuel oil:

[tex]\begin{aligned}\sf:\implies GPE& =\sf mgh\\& =\sf 400 \times 9.8 \times 20\\& =\boxed{\bold{\:\: 78,400\: J\:\:}}\:\:\:\green{\checkmark}\end{aligned}[/tex]

Therefore, the gravitational potential energy gained by the fuel oil when pumped to a height of 20 m is 78,400 J.

Specific speed Ns, is used to characterize turbomachinery speed.[1] Common commercial and industrial practices use dimensioned versions which are of equal utility. Specific speed is most commonly used in pump applications to define the suction specific speed [1]—a quasi non-dimensional number that categorizes pump impellers as to their type and proportions. In Imperial units it is defined as the speed in revolutions per minute at which a geometrically similar impeller would operate if it were of such a size as to deliver one gallon per minute against one foot of hydraulic head. In metric units flow may be in l/s or m³/s and head in m, and care must be taken to state the units used.
