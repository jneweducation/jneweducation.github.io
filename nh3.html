11. What is the percent yield of NH3 if 23.5 grams NH3 is produced from 13.0 mol H2 and excess N2.


Given:

actual yield = 23.5 g

moles of H₂ = 13.0 mol

reactants: N₂ and H₂

product: NH₃

Required:

percent yield

Solution:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of NH₃.

molar mass of NH₃ = (14.01 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 3)

molar mass of NH₃ = 17.034 g/mol

Step 3: Calculate the theoretical yield of the reaction by using dimensional analysis (factor-label method).

Since [tex]\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_{2} \bumpeq \text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}[/tex], the theoretical yield of the reaction is

[tex]\text{theoretical yield} = \text{13.0 mol} \: \text{H}_{2} × \frac{\text{2 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}}{\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_{2}} × \frac{\text{17.034 g} \: \text{NH}_{3}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{NH}_{3}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{theoretical yield = 147.628 g}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the percent yield.

[tex]\text{percent yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent yield} = \frac{\text{23.5 g}}{\text{147.628 g}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent yield} = 15.9\%}[/tex]

25. compare the % ionization of nh3 in a 0.55 M solution with that in a solution that is 0.55 M with both NH3 and NH4CI Kb=1.76x10-5​


Solution (percent ionization of NH3 in a 0.55 M solution):

Step 1: Identify the major species that can affect the percent ionization.

The major species in an ammonia solution are [tex]\text{NH}_{3}[/tex], [tex]\text{NH}_{4}^{+}[/tex], and [tex]\text{OH}^{-}[/tex].

Step 2: Express the equilibrium concentration of these species in terms of the initial concentration of the base and a single unknown x, which represents the change in concentration.

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccc} & \text{NH}_{3}(aq) & + & \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(l) & \rightleftharpoons & \text{NH}_{4}^{+}(aq) & + & \text{OH}^{-}(aq) \\ \text{Initial} \: (M): & 0.55 & & & & 0 & & 0 \\ \text{Change} \: (M): & -x & & & & +x & & +x \\ \hline \text{Equilibrium} \: (M): & 0.55-x & & & & x & & x \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Step 3: Write the weak base ionization and express the ionization constant [tex]K_{\text{b}}[/tex] in terms of the equilibrium concentrations of [tex]\text{OH}^{-}[/tex], the conjugate acid, and base.

[tex]K_{\text{b}} = \frac{[\text{NH}_{4}^{+}][\text{OH}^{-}]}{[\text{NH}_{3}]}[/tex]

[tex]1.76 × 10^{-5} = \frac{(x)(x)}{0.55 \: - \: x}[/tex]

[tex]1.76 × 10^{-5} = \frac{x^{2}}{0.55 \: - \: x}[/tex]

Applying the approximation [tex]0.55 - x ≈ 0.55[/tex], we obtain

[tex]1.76 × 10^{-5} = \frac{x^{2}}{0.55}[/tex]

[tex]x^{2} = (0.55)(1.76 × 10^{-5})[/tex]

[tex]x^{2} = 9.68 × 10^{-6}[/tex]

[tex]x = \sqrt{9.68 × 10^{-6}}[/tex]

[tex]x = 3.1113 × 10^{-3} \: M[/tex]

To test the approximation

[tex]\text{approx} = \frac{x}{\text{initial concentration}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{approx} = \frac{3.1113 × 10^{-3} \: M}{0.55 \: M} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{approx} = 0.57\%[/tex]

Since the approximation is less than 5%, the approximation is valid. Therefore, the value of [OH⁻] is

[tex][\text{OH}^{-}] = x = 3.1113 × 10^{-3} \: M[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the percent ionization.

[tex]\text{percent ionization} = \frac{\text{ionized base concentration at equilibrium}}{\text{initial concentration of base}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent ionization} = \frac{3.1113 × 10^{-3} \: M}{0.55 \: M} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{percent ionization} = 0.57\%}[/tex]

Solution (percent ionization of NH₃ in a solution that is 0.55 M with both NH₃ and NH₄Cl):

Step 1: Write the equation for the complete dissociation of NH₄Cl.

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccc} & \text{NH}_{4}\text{Cl}(aq) & → & \text{NH}_{4}^{+}(aq) & + & \text{Cl}^{-}(aq) \\ & & & 0.65 \: M & & 0.65 \: M \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Step 2: Express the equilibrium concentration of these species in terms of the initial concentration of the base and a single unknown x, which represents the change in concentration.

Since NH₄⁺ is the common ion, its initial concentration is equal to 0.55 M.

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccc} & \text{NH}_{3}(aq) & + & \text{H}_{2}\text{O}(l) & \rightleftharpoons & \text{NH}_{4}^{+}(aq) & + & \text{OH}^{-}(aq) \\ \text{Initial} \: (M): & 0.55 & & & & 0.55 & & 0 \\ \text{Change} \: (M): & -x & & & & +x & & +x \\ \hline \text{Equilibrium} \: (M): & 0.55-x & & & & 0.55+x & & x \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Step 3: Write the weak base ionization and express the ionization constant [tex]K_{\text{b}}[/tex] in terms of the equilibrium concentrations of [tex]\text{OH}^{-}[/tex], the conjugate acid, and base.

[tex]K_{\text{b}} = \frac{[\text{NH}_{4}^{+}][\text{OH}^{-}]}{[\text{NH}_{3}]}[/tex]

[tex]1.76 × 10^{-5} = \frac{(0.55 \: + \: x)(x)}{0.55 \: - \: x}[/tex]

Applying the approximation [tex]0.55 - x ≈ 0.55[/tex] and [tex]0.55 + x ≈ 0.55[/tex], we obtain

[tex]1.76 × 10^{-5} = \frac{(0.55)(x)}{0.55}[/tex]

[tex]x = 1.76 × 10^{-5} \: M[/tex]

To test the approximation

[tex]\text{approx} = \frac{x}{\text{initial concentration}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{approx} = \frac{1.76 × 10^{-5} \: M}{0.55 \: M} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{approx} = 0.0032\%[/tex]

Since the approximation is less than 5%, the approximation is valid. Therefore, the value of [OH⁻] is

[tex][\text{OH}^{-}] = x = 1.76 × 10^{-5} \: M[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the percent ionization.

[tex]\text{percent ionization} = \frac{\text{ionized base concentration at equilibrium}}{\text{initial concentration of base}} × 100[/tex]

29. If 500mL of NH3 contains 12g NH3, what is the volume of 10g NH3? N=14g H=1g (Avogadro's Law)


Good Day....

Problem:

If 500mL of NH3 contains 12g NH3, what is the volume of 10g NH3? N=14g H=1g (Avogadro's Law)

The molar mass of NH3 = 17 g / molTo convert grams to moles, divide the given mass by the molar mass of NH3.

12 g NH3 ÷ 17 g/mol = 0.706 mol

10 g NH3 ÷ 17 g/mol = 0.588 mol

Given Data:

V1 (initial volume) = 500 ml                  

V2 (final volume) = ? unknown

n1 (initial amount of gas) = 12 g (0.706 mol)

n2 (final amount of gas) = 10 g (0.588 mol)

Solution:

The formula of avogadro's law is V1/n1 = V2/n2To find V2 we can use the formula V2 = V1 × n2 / n1

V2 = V1 × n2 / n1

     = 500 ml × 0.588 mol / 0.706 mol   (cancel the unit mol)

    = 294 ml / 0.706

     = 416.43 ml

Answer:

volume will be 416.43 ml
