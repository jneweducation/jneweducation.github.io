6. If 2cos theta - sin theta = x and cos theta - 3sin theta = y . ( Prove that 2 x sq + y sq. - 2xy = 5 ) Plz answer it !!!!


We will need the identities:

[tex](a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2[/tex] [1]

[tex]cos^2a+sin^2a=1[/tex] [2]

We are given the equations:

I change theta with u,  theta = u , because it's an easier symbol

[tex]2cos(u)-sin(u)=x[/tex]

[tex]cos(u)-3sin(u)=y[/tex]

And we are asked to prove the following:

[tex]2x^2+y^2-2xy=5[/tex]

In order to prove the last statement we will substitute the other values above and through valid operations we will arrive at an equation that is true.

That is a general practice on how to prove an equation is true:

You either start from the true equations and data ,that are given to us in the exercise, and constract the equation we need to proveOr you start from the equation we need to prove (not true yet), and through valid operations, we simplify it into something that is true, for example 5=5.

Here we will start from the equation we need to prove and substitute x and y from [1] and [2]:

[tex]2x^2+y^2-2xy=5[/tex]

[tex]2(2cosu-sinu)^2+(cosu-3sinu)^2-2(2cosu-sinu)(cosu-3sinu)=5[/tex]

For easier operations lets change the following :

[tex]cosu=c[/tex]

[tex]sinu=s[/tex]

[tex]cos^2(u)+sin^2(u)=1[/tex]⇒[tex]c^2+s^2=1[/tex]

Then the equation continues as :

[tex]2(2c-s)^2+(c-3s)^2-2(2c-s)(c-3s)=5[/tex] and we use [1]

[tex]2(4c^2-4cs+s^2)+c^2-6cs+9s^2-2(2c^2-6cs-sc+3s)=5[/tex]

[tex]8c^2-8cs+2s^2+c^2-6cs+9s^2-4c^2+12cs+2cs-6s^2=5[/tex]

[tex]5c^2+0cs+5s^2=5[/tex]

[tex]5(c^2+s^2)=5[/tex] let's change it back for the last step:

[tex]5(cos^2(u)+sin^2(u))=5[/tex]

[tex]5*1=5[/tex] which is true!

Therefore the first statement must be true!

Think of the steps as a chain, if one end is true the other one is also true.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solution} & = \frac{100\%}{\text{percent by mass}} \times \text{mass of solute} \\ & = \frac{100\%}{16.2\%} \times \text{5.00 g} \\ & = \text{30.9 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solvent} & = \text{mass of solution} - \text{mass of solute} \\ & = \text{30.9 g} - \text{5.00 g} \\ & = \boxed{\text{25.9 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 25.9 g of water must be added to 5.00 g of urea to prepare 16.2% by mass solution.

Further explanation  

Solution properties are the properties of a solution that don't depend on the type of solute but only on the concentration of the solute.  

The term is used in the Solution properties  

1. molal (m)

that is, the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent  

 2. Boiling points  

Solutions from non-volatile substances have a higher boiling point and lower freezing points than the solvent  

ΔTb = Tb solution - Tb solvent

ΔTb = boiling point elevation  

  ΔTb = Kb. m  

Kb = molal boiling point increase  

m = molal solution

15 g of urea

100 g water

Required

The boiling point

= 15 g : 60.06 g/mol

= 0.25

The molality of solution :

= 0.25 : 0.1 kg water

= 2.5 m

The boiling point :

Tb solution - Tb solvent = Kb . m

Tb solution - 100 = 0.52 x 2.5

Tb solution = 101.3 °C

19. Write equilibrium constant expressions for kc, and for kp, if applicable, for the following processes: (a) 2co2(g) ↔ 2co(g) o2(g)


The equilibrium constant expression for that reaction can be written in terms of either the concentration of the species (Kc) or the partial pressure of the species (Kp).

Kc: The equilibrium constant expression for the concentration of the species is given by:

Kc = [CO2]² / [CO]²

Where [CO2] and [CO] are the concentration of CO2 and CO, respectively.

Kp: The equilibrium constant expression for the partial pressure of the species is given by:

Kp = (P_CO2)² / P_CO)²

Where P_CO2 and P_CO are the partial pressures of CO2 and CO, respectively.

Note that the equilibrium constant for a reaction does not depend on the units used to express the concentration or partial pressure of the species. The units will cancel out in the equilibrium constant expression[tex].[/tex]


20. A solution is prepared by dissolving 27.0 g of urea [(NH2)2CO], in 150.0 g of water. Calculate the boiling point of the solution. Urea is a nonelectrolyte.


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{27.0 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{60.056 g/mol} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{150.0 g = 0.1500 kg} \\ & K_{\text{b}} = 0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m \\ & T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} = 100^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{27.0 g}}{\text{60.056 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.450 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.450 mol}}{\text{0.1500 kg}} \\ & = \text{3.00 mol/kg} \\ & = 3.00 \: m \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the boiling-point elevation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta T_{\text{b}} & = K_{\text{b}}m \\ & = (0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m)(3.00 \: m) \\ & = 1.56^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the boiling point of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_{\text{b}} & = T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} + \Delta T_{\text{b}} \\ & = 100^{\circ}\text{C} + 1.56^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & = \boxed{101.56^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the boiling point of the solution is 101.56°C.
