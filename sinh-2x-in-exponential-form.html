What is radical form?

The relationship allows for the rewriting of radicals (square, cube, fourth, and so forth) as rational exponents (exponents that are fractions). broadly speaking, employing the exponents' power rule. As a result, the denominator of a rational exponent identifies the type of radical being employed; for example, a denominator of 3 denotes a cube root, a denominator of 4 the fourth root, and so on.

In mathematics, a radical is the opposite of an exponent, which is symbolized by the sign "," often referred to as root. A square root or a cube root can be used, and the number preceding the symbol or radical is regarded as an index number or degree. The exponent of this number cancels the radical and is a whole number.

A number's radical and root are interchangeable terms. The root can be an nth root, square root, cube root, or another type of root. Any number or phrase that uses a root is therefore referred to as a radical. The Latin word Radix, which meaning root, is the source of the English word radical. For a given integer, the radical can be used to explain several types of roots, including the square, cube, fourth, and so on.

What is exponential form?

It is simpler to write repeated multiplication involving base and exponents in the exponential form. In the exponential form, 5 is the base and 4 is the power, thus we may write 5 x 5 x 5 x 5 as 5⁴. The power here indicates how many times the base is being multiplied by itself.

27. Transform the given Radical form into Exponential form and Exponential form into Radical form.​


Answer:

To transform a radical form into exponential form, we can use the following general rule:

√a = a^(1/2)

So, for example, if we have the expression √2, we can write it as 2^(1/2) in exponential form.

To transform exponential form into radical form, we can use the following general rule:

a^(1/n) = nth root of a

So, for example, if we have the expression 4^(1/3), we can write it as the cube root of 4 in radical form, which is ∛4.

Here are some examples of transforming between radical form and exponential form:

Radical form to exponential form: √5 = 5^(1/2)

Exponential form to radical form: 2^(1/4) = fourth root of 2

Radical form to exponential form: √(3x + 1) = (3x + 1)^(1/2)

Exponential form to radical form: 8^(2/3) = cube root of 8 squared, which is 4∛8

Radical form to exponential form: √(a/b) = (a/b)^(1/2)

Exponential form to radical form: 16^(1/4) = fourth root of 16, which is 2
