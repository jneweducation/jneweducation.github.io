5. How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 670 g copper rod from 25°C to 32°C? The metal's specific heat is 0.385 J/g°C.


Problem #01:

Given: m = 670g

c = 0.385J/g•°C

Tf = 32°C

Ti = 25°C

Unknown: Q = ?

Formula: Q = mc∆T

where in, Q = heat transfer

m = mass

c = specific heat capacity

∆T = change in temp.

Note: ∆T = Tf - Ti

Tf = final temperature

Ti = initial temperature

Solution:

Q = mc∆T

= (670g)(0.385J/g•°C)(32°C - 25°C)

= (670g)(0.385J/g•°C)(7°C)

= (257.95J/°C)(7°C)

= 1805.65J

Note: Unit for heat transfer is Joules(J) or Kilojoules(kj), that’s why unnecessary units are cancelled out.

Tip: Cancellation of like terms or units are applicable when the other like term is on the denominator.

Final Answer: Q = 1805.65J

9. Copper wires used to transport electrical current heat up because of the resistance in the wire. If a 140-g wiregains 280 J of heat, what is the change in temperature in the wire? Copper has a specific heat of 0.384 J/g °C.


Answer:

Explanation:

To find the change in temperature of the wire, you need to use the formula:

ΔT = (Q / m) / c

where ΔT is the change in temperature, Q is the amount of heat gained by the wire, m is the mass of the wire, and c is the specific heat capacity of the material.

Plugging in the values, you get:

ΔT = (280 J / 140 g) / 0.384 J/g °C

Solving this equation gives a change in temperature of:

ΔT = 1.875 °C

This is the change in temperature of the wire due to the heat it has gained.

13. Which substance is most likely to heat up the fastest? water (specific heat of 4.19 /g • C°) copper (specific heat of .39 /g • C°) aluminum (specific heat of .90 /g • C°) dry air (specific heat of 1.01 /g • C°)


Copper (specific heat of 0.39 / g • C °) is the most likely to heat up the fastest

Further explanation

The law of conservation of energy can be applied to heat changes, i.e. the heat received / absorbed is the same as the heat released

Q in = Q out

Heat can be calculated using the formula:

Q = mc∆T

Q = heat, J

m = mass, g

c = specific heat, joules / g ° C

∆T = temperature difference, ° C / K

[tex]\rm \Delta T = \dfrac {Q} {m.c}[/tex]

Which substance is most likely to heat up the fastest means that it has the largest ∆T . With the same amount of mass and the amount of heat given, the object with the smallest c is the fastest heat, because ∆T is inversely proportional to c (specific heat)

If we look at the question, then the object that has the smallest c is copper with a specific heat of 0.39 / g • C °

Actually the specific heat itself shows the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of a substance by 1°C

So with a small specific heat means that the substance requires less heat to increase its temperature than substances that have a greater specific heat

27. it takes 487.5 J to heat 25 grams of copper from 25°C to 75°C. What is the specific heat in joule/g°C?​


Specific Heat of copper is 0.39 J/g°C

Explanation:

Step 1: Enumerate the given values.

Given:

m = 25 g

T1 = 25°C

T2 = 75°C

Q = 487.5 J

Step 2: Identify what is being asked.

Required:

Specific Heat or cp

Step 3: Solve for Specific Heat

Equation:

The formula for Heat Energy is given by:

Q = mcp∆T

where: Q - Heat Energy

m - mass

cp - specific heat

∆T = change in temperature, T2 - T1

Solution:

Substituting the value of m, Q and ∆T in the formula,

487.5 J = (25 g)(cp)(75°C - 25°C)

cp = (487.5 J)/(25 g)(75°C - 25°C)

cp = 0.39 J/g°C

Answer:

Specific Heat of copper is 0.39 J/g°C
