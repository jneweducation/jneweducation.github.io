1. Butane, C4H10, is a common fuel for heating. The equation for the combustion of butane is 2C4H10 + 13O2 --> 8CO2 + 10H2O a. If 9.43g of O2 is used in burning butane,how many grams of water result? b. How many grams of CO2 will be produced by burning 78.4g of butane, C4H10? c. How many moles of C4H10 can be burned by 16 mol of carbon dioxide? d. How many moles of water are used in reaction that produces 880g of CO2? e. How many grams of oxygen are required to produce 1.5 mol of butane?


Solution: (a)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For O₂

molar mass = 16.00 g/mol × 2

molar mass = 32.00 g/mol

For H₂O

molar mass = (1.008 g/mol × 2) + (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 18.016 g/mol

Step 2: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 13 mol O₂ : 10 mol H₂O

Step 3: Calculate the mass of H₂O formed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of H₂O = 9.43 g O₂} × \frac{\text{1 mol O₂}}{\text{32.00 g O₂}} × \frac{\text{10 mol H₂O}}{\text{13 mol O₂}} × \frac{\text{18.016 g H₂O}}{\text{1 mol H₂O}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of H₂O = 4.084 g}}[/tex]

Solution: (b)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For C₄H₁₀

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 4) + (1.008 g/mol × 10)

molar mass = 58.12 g/mol

For CO₂

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 2)

molar mass = 44.01 g/mol

Step 2: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 2 mol C₄H₁₀ : 8 mol CO₂

Step 3: Calculate the mass of CO₂ formed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of CO₂ = 78.4 g C₄H₁₀} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₄H₁₀}}{\text{58.12 g C₄H₁₀}} × \frac{\text{8 mol CO₂}}{\text{2 mol C₄H₁₀}} × \frac{\text{44.01 g CO₂}}{\text{1 mol CO₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of CO₂ = 237.5 g}}[/tex]

Solution: (c)

[tex]\text{moles of C₄H₁₀ = 16 mol CO₂} × \frac{\text{2 mol C₄H₁₀}}{\text{8 mol CO₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of C₄H₁₀ = 4 mol}}[/tex]

Solution: (d)

Step 1: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 8 mol CO₂ : 10 mol H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of H₂O used using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{moles of H₂O = 880 g CO₂} × \frac{\text{1 mol CO₂}}{\text{44.01 g CO₂}} × \frac{\text{10 mol H₂O}}{\text{8 mol CO₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of H₂O = 25 mol}}[/tex]

Solution: (e)

Step 1: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 2 mol C₄H₁₀ : 13 mol O₂

Step 2: Calculate the mass of O₂ needed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of O₂ = 1.5 mol C₄H₁₀} × \frac{\text{13 mol O₂}}{\text{2 mol C₄H₁₀}} × \frac{\text{32.00 g O₂}}{\text{1 mol O₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of O₂ = 312 g}}[/tex]

11. Given the balance equation C5H12 + O2 --> CO2 + H2O + Heat, what mass of carbon dioxide is produced by the complete combustion of 100 grams of the hydrocarbon pentane?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

C₅H₁₂ + 8O₂ → 5CO₂ + 6H₂O + ∆

Step 2: Calculate the mass of CO₂ produced by using dimensional analysis.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of C₅H₁₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 5 moles of CO₂.

The molar masses of C₅H₁₂ and CO₂ are 72.151 g and 44.009 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{CO}_2 & = \text{100 g} \: \text{C}_5\text{H}_{12} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{C}_5\text{H}_{12}}{\text{72.151 g} \: \text{C}_5\text{H}_{12}} \times \frac{\text{5 mol} \: \text{CO}_2}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{C}_5\text{H}_{12}} \times \frac{\text{44.009 g} \: \text{CO}_2}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CO}_2} \\ & = \boxed{\text{305 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 305 g of CO₂ is produced from 100 g of C₅H₁₂.

29. The complete combustion of butane, (g)


COMPLETE COMBUSTION OF BUTANE

Butane is a colourless, odourless, and highly flammable hydrocarbon gas that is mainly used in lighters to provide fuel for the flame.  Due to the nature in which it is used in lighters as a source of fuel, I will be discussing the chemical process of complete combustion in order to describe why such phenomenon occur.

The balanced equation for the complete combustion of butane is as observed below:

As observed from the balanced chemical equation above, butane reacts with oxygen gas and combusts, forming carbon dioxide and water.  The butane gas is stored in a both a liquid and gaseous state in iso-equilibrium form, and when gas is required to create the flame the butane is pressurized and thus vapourized, turning it into a gas where it reacts with oxygen and flame to combust.  This is one of the fuels that can be used in lighters, which have widespread application around the world, whether it’d be starting a fire or lighting candles on your birthday cake.  Naturally, in a combustion chemical equation, the limiting reactant is oxygen, and the excess reactant butane.  When there is not enough oxygen to create a complete combustion, carbon monoxide is thus formed and is released as soot.

Incomplete combustion has potentially hazardous results, as the gas created by incomplete combustion is carbon monoxide which is highly toxic.  Not only is it harmful to humans, but it also poses a threat to plant life as well,  as the exposure to carbon monoxide leads to the reduced oxygen delivery to the body’s organs.  Serious cases of carbon monoxide poisoning may lead to death if not treated properly.
