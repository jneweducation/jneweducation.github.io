4. Arianna borrows P86, 800 with interest at 20% compounded annually. How much should she pay to the creditor after 12 years to pay off her debt?​


To find out how much Arianna should pay to the creditor after 12 years, you can use the compound interest formula:

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where:

A is the total amount Arianna should pay to the creditor after 12 years (the unknown value you're trying to solve for)

P is the principal amount Arianna borrowed, which is P86,800

r is the annual interest rate, which is 20% or 0.2

n is the number of times the interest is compounded per year. In this case, we're told that the claim is compounded annually, so n is 1.

t is the number of years the loan is outstanding, which is 12.

Plugging these values into the formula, we get:

A = P86,800(1 + 0.2/1)^(1 * 12)

A = P86,800(1.2)^12

A = P86,800 * 2.9744

A = P259,375.52

So Arianna should pay P259,375.52 to the creditor after 12 years to pay off her debt.

14. How does an interest rate help a creditor?​

Generally, the interest rate is a percentage of the loan amount that the borrower must pay to the creditor in addition to the principal amount. The interest rate helps compensate the creditor for the risk they take in loaning out money and also helps to cover their administrative costs.

TRADE CREDITORS VS BANK

If a cash discount of 2% in 10 days is offered by a trade creditor and a bank lends funds at 20% per annum, it is wise to borrow in order that the cash discount offered by the creditor may be availed of because there is no collateral and the possible annual discount that you can get is up to 73% of the accounts payable.

How did we get this?

If we are to calculate how many times the trade creditor will give a cash discount of 2% in a year, it would be this equation:[tex]\frac{365 days}{10 days} ( 0.02)[/tex] x 100% = 73%It means that you will only have to pay 27% of the goods that they supplied you— a much better deal than a 20% per annum interest from the bank.
