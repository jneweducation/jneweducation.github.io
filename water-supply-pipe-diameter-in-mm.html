9. For Fluid pipe Formula An insulated steam pipe located where the ambient temperature is 32°C, has an inside diameter of 50 mm with 10 mm thick wall. The outside diameter of the corrugated asbestos insulation is 125 mm and the surface coefficient of still air, h0 = 12 W/m2 -K. Inside the pipe is steam having a temperature of 150 °C with film coefficient h=6000W/m2k thermal conductivity of pipe and asbestos insulation are 45 and 0.12w/mk respectively. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Step-by-step explanation:

The overall heat transfer coefficient, U can be calculated as:

1/U = 1/hi + r1/k1 + r2/k2 + r3/k3 + 1/ho

where,

hi = film coefficient on the inside of the pipe = h = 6000 W/m2K

ho = film coefficient on the outside of the insulation = h0 = 12 W/m2K

r1 = radius of the inner surface of the insulation = (50/2 + 10) / 1000 = 0.03 m

r2 = radius of the outer surface of the insulation = (50/2 + 10 + 0.125) / 1000 = 0.055 m

r3 = radius of the outer surface of the insulation plus thickness of pipe, which is the unknown we need to find

k1 = thermal conductivity of the insulation = 0.12 W/mK

k2 = thermal conductivity of the asbestos insulation = 0.12 W/mK

k3 = thermal conductivity of the pipe = 45 W/mK

Now, substituting the given values in the equation, we get:

1/U = 1/6000 + 0.03/0.12 + 0.055/0.12 + r3/45 + 1/12

Simplifying this equation, we get:

U = 6.013 W/m2K

The heat loss per unit length of the pipe, q, can be calculated as:

q = U x (T1 - T2)

where,

T1 = temperature inside the pipe = 150°C = 423 K

T2 = ambient temperature outside the insulation = 32°C = 305 K

Substituting the values, we get:

q = 6.013 x (423 - 305) = 708.37 W/m

Now, we can use the given heat loss per unit length of the pipe, q, to find the required thickness of the pipe, x:

q = k3 x 2πL x (T1 - T2) / ln(r3 / r2)

where,

L = length of the pipe = 1 m (assuming unit length)

ln = natural logarithm

Substituting the values, we get:

100 = 45 x 2π x 1 x (423 - 305) / ln(r3 / 0.055)

Simplifying this equation, we get:

ln(r3 / 0.055) = 91.72

r3 / 0.055 = e^91.72

r3 = 0.055 x e^91.72

r3 = 7.038 m

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe is:

x = r3 - 0.055 - 0.05

x = 6.933 m

ANSWER:Hence, the required thickness of the pipe is approximately 6.9 mm.

10. Problem 2-53 A differential manometer shown is measuring the difference in pressure two water pipes. The indicating liquid is mercury (specific gravity = 13.6), lh is 675 mm, It is 225 mm, and 12 is 300 mm. What is the pressure differential between the two pipes.​


Answer:

The pressure differential can be calculated using the following formula:

ΔP = (ρ * g * h)

where:

ΔP = pressure differential

ρ = density of the fluid

g = acceleration due to gravity

h = height difference between the two fluid columns

In this case, the specific gravity of mercury is 13.6, which means its density (ρ) is:

ρ = specific gravity * density of water

ρ = 13.6 * 1000 kg/m^3

ρ = 13600 kg/m^3

The height difference between the two fluid columns is:

ρ = specific gravity * density of water

ρ = 13.6 * 1000 kg/m^3

ρ = 13600 kg/m^3

The height difference between the two fluid columns is:

h = lh - It + 12

h = 675 mm - 225 mm + 300 mm

h = 750 mm

Converting mm to m:

h = 750 mm * (1 m / 1000 mm)

h = 0.75 m

The acceleration due to gravity (g) is 9.81 m/s^2.

Now we can calculate the pressure differential:

ΔP = (ρ * g * h)

ΔP = (13600 kg/m^3 * 9.81 m/s^2 * 0.75 m)

ΔP = 99918 Pa or 99.918 kPa

Therefore, the pressure differential between the two water pipes is approximately 99.918 kPa.

14. For Fluid pipe Formula An insulated steam pipe located where the ambient temperature is 32°C, has an inside diameter of 50 mm with 10 mm thick wall. The outside diameter of the corrugated asbestos insulation is 125 mm and the surface coefficient of still air, h0 = 12 W/m2 -K. Inside the pipe is steam having a temperature of 150 °C with film coefficient h=6000W/m2k thermal conductivity of pipe and asbestos insulation are 45 and 0.12w/mk respectively. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Answer:

We can use the formula for heat transfer through a cylindrical wall:

Q/t = 2πkL(T1-T2) / [ln(r2/r1) / h1 + (r2-r1) / k + ln(r3/r2) / h2]

where Q/t is the heat loss per unit length, k is the thermal conductivity, L is the length of the pipe, T1 and T2 are the temperatures of the inside and outside surfaces of the wall, r1 and r3 are the inner and outer radii of the pipe, and r2 is the outer radius of the insulation.

We can rearrange this formula to solve for the required thickness of the pipe:

r1 = (r2^2 - L / (2πkL(T1-T2)) * [ln(r2/r3) / h2 + (r2-r3) / k + ln(r2/r1) / h1]

Plugging in the given values:

r1 = (0.0625^2 - 1 / (2π * 45 * 0.1 * (150-32))) * [ln(0.125/0.145) / 12 + (0.125-0.145) / 0.12 + ln(0.125/0.05) / 6000]

r1 = 0.00598 m

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe is:

t = r2 - r1 - d/2 = 0.125 - 0.00598 - 0.05 / 2 = 0.034 m

So the required thickness of the pipe is approximately 34 mm.
