Preparing a Standard Solution from a Solid A solution of known concentration can be prepared from solids by two similar methods. Although inherent errors exist with each of the methods, with careful technique either will suffice for making solutions in General Chemistry Laboratory. In the first method, the solid solute is weighed out on weighing paper or in a small container and then transferred directly to a volumetric flask (commonly called a "vol flask"). 1. What is a solution?

A solution is a homogenous mixture produced when one or more solutes are dissolved in a solvent. The solute, a substance that is just slightly present, is soluble in the solvent (the chemical present in a larger amount). Standard (stock) solutions are referred to as solutions with precisely known concentrations.

2. What are the materials mentioned in the handout in preparing a solution using the first method?

The solid solute is weighed out using weighing paper or a tiny container in the first procedure, and then it is immediately transferred to a volumetric flask (commonly called a "vol flask"). Once the materials are combined and fully dissolved, the vol flask is covered and repeatedly inverted.

Further explanation:

Standard (stock) solutions are referred to as solutions with precisely known concentrations. These solutions can be purchased straight from the manufacturer or created by putting the desired solute amount into a volumetric flask with a predetermined volume. For use in laboratory experiments, stock solutions are routinely diluted to solutions with lower concentrations.

There are two procedures that are similar for creating a solution from solids with a known concentration. Both methods—despite the fact that they both have intrinsic flaws—can be used to create solutions in the general chemistry laboratory with careful skill.

Molarity

Molarity is the most widely used concentration unit in chemistry in general:

= moles of solute/liters of solution

= grams of solute / (molar mass solute x liters of solution)

Solution B is prepared by pipetting 25 mL of solution A into a 100-mL volumetric flask and diluting to volume. Solution C is prepared by pipetting 20 mL of solution B into a 500-mL volumetric flask and diluting to volume. What is the molar concentration of NaCl in solutions A, B, and C?


SOLUTION:For Solution A

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M_{\text{i}} = 0.100 \: M \\ & V_{\text{i}} = \text{10 mL} \\ & V_{\text{f}} = \text{250 mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar concentration of Solution A.

[tex]\begin{aligned} M_{\text{f}} & = \frac{M_{\text{i}}V_{\text{i}}}{V_{\text{f}}} \\ & = \frac{(0.100 \: M)(\text{10 mL})}{\text{250 mL}} \\ & = \boxed{4.00 \times 10^{-3} \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molar concentration of Solution A is 4.00 × 10⁻³ M.

For Solution B

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M_{\text{i}} = 4.00 \times 10^{-3} \: M \\ & V_{\text{i}} = \text{25 mL} \\ & V_{\text{f}} = \text{100 mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar concentration of Solution B.

[tex]\begin{aligned} M_{\text{f}} & = \frac{M_{\text{i}}V_{\text{i}}}{V_{\text{f}}} \\ & = \frac{(4 \times 10^{-3} \: M)(\text{25 mL})}{\text{100 mL}} \\ & = \boxed{1.00 \times 10^{-3} \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molar concentration of Solution B is 1.00 × 10⁻³ M.

For Solution C

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M_{\text{i}} = 1.00 \times 10^{-3} \: M \\ & V_{\text{i}} = \text{20 mL} \\ & V_{\text{f}} = \text{500 mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar concentration of Solution C.

[tex]\begin{aligned} M_{\text{f}} & = \frac{M_{\text{i}}V_{\text{i}}}{V_{\text{f}}} \\ & = \frac{(1.00 \times 10^{-3} \: M)(\text{20 mL})}{\text{500 mL}} \\ & = \boxed{4.00 \times 10^{-5} \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molar concentration of Solution C is 4.00 × 10⁻⁵ M.
