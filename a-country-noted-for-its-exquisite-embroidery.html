20. Different Embroidery Stitches and Its Procedure​


Answer:

1. Running Stitch

Not to be confused with the running man, the running stitch offers a quick way to outline a design. There are two methods you can use.

The first is the sewing method: Simply weave the needle and floss through the fabric in one continuous motion to create several stitches at once, as if you were sewing a seam.

The second is the “punch and poke” or “stabbing” method: Push the needle through the fabric to the back, then poke it through to the front a short distance away, creating one stitch at a time.

2. Backstitch

The backstitch is great when you need a solid line, like when you’re creating outlines or hand embroidered letters.

Begin by pulling the needle and floss up through the fabric and do one stitch forward. From underneath, space the needle out the length of your desired stitch, pull up through the fabric, and bring the needle and floss back down through the end of the previous stitch.

3. Split Stitch

Similar to the backstitch, the split stitch creates a solid line, but its braided texture is all its own. This stitch is another good option for text and outlines, but also works well for filling in designs.

To begin, pull your needle and floss up through the fabric and create one straight stitch. With your needle and floss on the underside of your hoop, bring the needle up through the center of the stitch you just created. Stitch forward the same length as your initial stitch. Repeat by bringing the needle up through the center of each stitch.

4. Stem Stitch

This stitch got its name — you guessed it — from being used to create flower stems and vines. But really, it’s a nice option for anything that needs to curve.

Similar to the split stitch, start by creating one straight stitch forward. Then, bring the needle and floss up underneath the fabric, but instead of going through the center of your initial stitch, go just to the side of the stitch.

5. Satin Stitch

When you’re creating hearts or filling in leaves, it’s likely you want the design to have a smooth appearance. That’s where this stitch comes in. It adds a nice raised texture, and gets the job done super fast.

First, draw out the shape you want to fill to use as a guide. With your needle and floss, create one stitch that extends from one end of the shape to the other.

Bring the needle up again just next to the opposite side of the initial stitch. Keep the stitches close to one another, as required to fill the pattern or design you are working with.

25. Enumerate theembroidery toolsand tell its function.​


Answer:

There are 21 types of embroidery tools:

Explanation:

Dress Maker’s Scissors - These scissors are rather large, they have very sharp blades that should only be used to cut fabric or thread.Table Stand - This is a giant hoop that is specifically designed to sit on top of a flat surface while you work, making it much easier for you to control your fabric without having to hold onto a hoop with one hand.Ruler - it prevents you from making any mistakes in your embroidery and assures you with a straight lineSmall Pointed Scissors - you will additionally need smaller scissors that are incredibly sharp and pointed so that you can easily slide the tip of the scissors underneath your threads and clip them.Emroidery Hoop - This tool is perfect for embroidery, as it will hold the fabric tight without stretching it and allow you to maintain even pressure during the project.Thimble - are used by wearing them on the tip of your finger and then using them to help push your needle through the fabric.Chenille Needles -  have incredibly sharp points and large eyes, making it very easy to not only thread these needles but also to push them through your fabric without dealing with a dull point.Needle Threader - make threading your needles significantly easier since you don’t have to try to see the eye of the needle and put the end of your thread through it.Crewel Embroidery Needles - have larger eyes and much sharper tips than regular sewing needles do, for embroidering. Punch Needles - Another type of embroidery is needle punching, which involves looping ribbon, floss, or yarn through the fabric to create a pattern.Tape Measure - so that you can measure the work that you have done, and this tool can take the place of a hard ruler if you are worried about how easy it will be to read. Pinking Scissors -  they will create a zigzag pattern along the cut edge when you are finished. Beading Needle - is used when you are going to be completing bead embroidery as well as tambour work, or a decorative chain stitch. Thread Nippers - These tools are generally fairly small and have incredibly sharp blades that can easily snip through a large number of threads without any problems.Magnifiers - will help you to see your work more easilyApplique Scissors -  these scissors have a very specific job of removing extra fabric.Pin Cushion - is used to keep all your different types of needles safe and in one place.Seam Ripper- You will be able to slide the tip of the seam ripper under any stitches that you want to remove and then simply lift it up at an angle from your work to rip through the threads.Laying Tool - If you are going to be laying multiple threads near each other and want to make sure that they are flat and smoothTweezers - will allow you to remove single threads at once without worrying about damaging your work.Embroidery Eraser -  If you want to remove large areas of your embroidery or even to start over completely, then you can use this tool to cut through all of your threads at once.
