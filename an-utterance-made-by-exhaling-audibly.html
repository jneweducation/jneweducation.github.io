3. Which of the following refers to pauses and stops made between thoughts or utterance?


The answer is juncture. In speech communication, a juncture refers to the pauses and breaks between syllables and thoughts in speech. Different junctures can create different meanings, such as the difference between "I scream" and "ice cream." Without the proper juncture, these two words will sound the same.

Types of Juncture Close Open Terminal

Close juncture

This refers to the transition from one sound to the next within the same utterance. It involves one word. For example, when saying the word ‘train,’ we transition from T to R without an intervening pause or delay.

Open juncture

This is the transition from one sound to the next in two utterances, involving pairs of words. Here, the movement is not as continuous as a close juncture, so we stop slightly after one sound before blending it with the next. Here are examples:

A name / an aim Nitrate / night rate He lies/ heal eyes I scream / ice cream

Terminal juncture

This is the pause found at the end of an utterance or a clause right before a silence. It’s a longer break than open and close junctures.

She didn't do it on purpose, it was an accident. /She didn't do it on purpose → it was an accident↘/ It's okay, Daddy’s here. / It's okay → Daddy’s here↘/

8. frequencies that are below the audible range ​


Answer:

Sound at 20-200 Hz is called low-frequency sound, while for sound below 20 Hz the term infrasound is used. The hearing becomes gradually less sensitive for decreasing frequency, but despite the general understanding that infrasound is inaudible, humans can perceive infrasound, if the level is sufficiently high.
