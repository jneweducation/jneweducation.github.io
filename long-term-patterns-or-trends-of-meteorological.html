24. pieces of evidence are presented that prove the connection between climate change and the changing meteorological pattern. (typhoon haiyan)​


Answer:

There is a significant body of evidence that supports the connection between climate change and the changing meteorological patterns that contribute to severe weather events like Typhoon Haiyan. Here are a few examples of this evidence:

Rising sea surface temperatures: The waters in the western Pacific Ocean where Typhoon Haiyan formed have been warming over the past few decades. The warmer water temperatures can increase the intensity of tropical storms and hurricanes.

Increased atmospheric moisture: As the Earth's climate warms, there is more moisture in the atmosphere. This increased moisture can contribute to more intense rainfall during storms, like the torrential rains that accompanied Typhoon Haiyan.

Changing wind patterns: Climate change can alter the patterns of global wind circulation, which can affect the paths and intensity of tropical storms. Typhoon Haiyan was able to intensify rapidly due to changes in wind patterns in the region.

These pieces of evidence all point to the role that climate change plays in contributing to the changing meteorological patterns that are leading to more frequent and severe weather events like Typhoon Haiyan.

28. What pieces of evidence are presented that prove or reinforce the connection between climate change and the changing meteorological patterns?


Answer:

TL;DR

Scientists have looked at regional meteorological patterns (e.g. precipitation, seasonal temperatures, snow/glacier melt, etc.) and quantified how they have changed over time. They then use statistics and models to determine whether those changes can be attributed to anthropogenic climate change or natural variation. This isn’t something you can easily assess on a global scale, so I use wildfires in the western US (and the conditions that cause them) as an example.

It’s really something you have to address on a case-by-case basis. On average, the world is getting warmer. However, that doesn’t mean all regions respond to this warming in the same way. Some areas may get more precipitation while others get drier. Some experience more heat waves while others have record low temperatures (mostly due to stuff like the Polar vortex).

Let’s take the western US, a region that has recently experienced major wildfires.

Warming and Earlier Spring Increase Western U.S. Forest Wildfire Activity

This is a 2006 study that looked at how wildfire activity has changed in the western US since 1970. I suggest reading it yourself if you want to know details about the methods they used, but basically they compared wildfire activity to climate conditions.

Historical wildfire observations exhibit an abrupt transition in the mid-1980s from a regime of infrequent large wildfires of short (average of 1 week) duration to one with much more frequent and longer burning (5 weeks) fires. This transition was marked by a shift toward unusually warm springs, longer summer dry seasons, drier vegetation (which provoked more and longer burning large wildfires), and longer fire seasons. Reduced winter precipitation and an early spring snowmelt played a role in this shift. Increases in wildfire were particularly strong in mid-elevation forests..

They also considered land-use, but found that there are still increased wildfires even in areas that human’s haven’t impacted much.

The overall importance of climate in wildfire activity underscores the urgency of ecological restoration and fuels management to reduce wildfire hazards to human communities and to mitigate ecological impacts of climate change in forests that have undergone substantial alterations due to past land uses. At the same time, however, large increases in wildfire driven by increased temperatures and earlier spring snowmelts in forests where land-use history had little impact on fire risks indicates that ecological restoration and fuels management alone will not be sufficient to reverse current wildfire trends.

Also note that this particularly study only looked at the correlation between climate change and wildfires. They didn’t look at the cause behind the climate change as that is better left to other more targeted studies.

These results have important regional and global implications. Whether the changes observed in western hydroclimate and wildfire are the result of greenhouse gas–induced global warming or only an unusual natural fluctuation is beyond the scope of this work. Regardless of past trends, virtually all climate-model projections indicate that warmer springs and summers will occur over the region in coming decades. These trends will reinforce the tendency toward early spring snowmelt (36, 37) and longer fire seasons. This will accentuate conditions favorable to the occurrence of large wildfires, amplifying the vulnerability the region has experienced since the mid-1980s. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's consensus range of 1.5° to 5.8°C projected global surface temperature warming by the end of the 21st century is considerably larger than the recent warming of less than 0.9°C observed in spring and summer during recent decades over the western region (37).

Fortunately, other scientists did look for this connection in 2016.

Impact of anthropogenic climate change on wildfire across western US forests

Long story short, at least half of the drying out of the forests is caused by anthropogenic climate change. The rest is likely natural climate variation.
