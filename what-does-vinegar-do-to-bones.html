8. Is there marked difference in the dissolution rate in water and vinegarboth at room temperature? What caused the difference? What doesdissolution in vinegar simulate in nature?​


Answer:

Water is polar but not ionic, so has very very little hydrogen ion present. 1 x 10^-7 in pure water.

Vinegar is a dilute weak acid and will have a considerably higher concentration of hydrogen ion.

So, it will not make a difference in solubility of a non-polar solute, but will make a difference in the solubility of polar and ionic solutes. Those will more readily dissolve in the vinegar solution.

There are two types of chemical reactions that may occur :

namely single-replacement reactions and double-replacement reactions.

Double-Replacement reactions happens if there is an ion exchange between two ion compounds in the reactant to form two new ion compounds in the product

There are 3 reactions that can indicate the existence of a Double-Replacement, namely neutralization (acid base), precipitation (solid product) and gas formation

Double replacement reactions can be written as molecular, complete ionic, or net ionic equations

NaHCO₃ (s) + CH₃COOH (aq) → CH₃COONa (aq) + CO₂ (g) + H₂O (l)

NaHCO₃ (s) + CH₃COOH (aq) → CH₃COONa (aq) + H₂CO₃

The second reaction is a decomposition reaction. The carbonic acid decomposes into water and carbon dioxide gas.

 H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O

30. The concentration of acetic acid (pka = 4.75) in vinegar is about 1.0 M. With this information, what do you predict the pH of vinegar to be?​


Answer:

Since vinegar has a concentration of 1.0 M acetic acid, we can assume that the initial concentration of acetic acid is equal to 1.0 M, and the concentration of CH3COO- and H3O+ at equilibrium will be equal, so we can simplify the expression to:

Ka = [H3O+]^2 / [CH3COOH]

We can then rearrange the expression to solve for [H3O+]:

[H3O+] = sqrt(Ka*[CH3COOH])

The pKa of acetic acid is 4.75, which means that:

Ka = 10^(-pKa) = 1.78 x 10^(-5)

Substituting this value and the concentration of acetic acid into the equation gives:

[H3O+] = sqrt((1.78 x 10^(-5)) * (1.0 M)) = 0.00422 M

Finally, we can calculate the pH of vinegar using the equation:

pH = -log[H3O+]

pH = -log(0.00422) = 2.38

Therefore, the pH of vinegar is predicted to be approximately 2.38.
