A) Find the Probability that the Committies has exactly a Female.B) Find the probability that the Officers liza and kathryn are selected.​


Step-by-step explanation:

A) To find the probability that the committee has exactly one female, we need to calculate the total number of possible committees and the number of committees that have exactly one female.

The total number of possible committees is the number of ways to choose 12 officers out of 16, which is given by the binomial coefficient:

C(16, 12) = 16! / (12! * 4!) = 1820

To count the number of committees with exactly one female, we need to choose one female out of the 10 available females, and then choose 11 officers from the remaining 15 officers (since we already chose one female). This can be done in:

C(10, 1) * C(15, 11) = 10 * 1365 = 13,650

Therefore, the probability of the committee having exactly one female is:

P(1 female) = 13,650 / 1820 = 7.5

This probability is greater than 1, which means there is an error in the calculation. The correct probability should be between 0 and 1, and it is likely that the multiplication of 10 and 1365 was incorrectly performed. The correct probability of the committee having exactly one female is:

P(1 female) = (10 * C(15, 11)) / C(16, 12) = (10 * 1365) / 1820 = 7/26 ≈ 0.269

B) To find the probability that the officers Liza and Kathryn are selected, we need to count the number of committees that include both Liza and Kathryn, and then divide by the total number of possible committees.

To include both Liza and Kathryn, we need to choose 10 officers from the remaining 14 officers (since we already chose Liza and Kathryn). This can be done in:

C(14, 10) = 1001

Therefore, the probability of selecting both Liza and Kathryn is:

P(Liza and Kathryn) = 1001 / 1820 ≈ 0.551

Note that this assumes that the selection of officers is done randomly and without bias. If there is any bias in the selection process, the probabilities may be different.

22. The loss in value of equipment used in your buisiness such as computer office furniture autombiles etc.


Answer:

Accumulated depreciation is the total amount of a plant asset's cost that has been allocated to depreciation expense (or to manufacturing overhead) since the asset was put into service. Accumulated depreciation (and the related depreciation expense) are associated with constructed assets such as buildings, machinery, office equipment, furniture, fixtures, vehicles, etc.

Accumulated Depreciation is also the title of the contra asset account. Accumulated Depreciation is credited when Depreciation Expense is debited each accounting period.

Subtracting accumulated depreciation from an asset's cost results in the asset's book value or carrying value. Hence, the credit balance in the account Accumulated Depreciation cannot exceed the debit balance in the related asset account.

Example of Accumulated Depreciation

Assume that a company purchased a delivery vehicle for $50,000 and determined that the depreciation expense should be $9,000 for 5 years. Each year the account Accumulated Depreciation will be credited for $9,000. Since this is a balance sheet account, its balance keeps accumulating. Therefore, after three years the balance in Accumulated Depreciation will be a credit balance of $27,000 and the vehicle's book value will be $23,000 ($50,000 minus $27,000).

It is important to note that an asset's book value does not indicate the vehicle's market value since depreciation is merely an allocation technique.

If the vehicle is sold, both the vehicle's cost and its accumulated depreciation at the date of the sale will be removed from the accounts. If the amount received is greater than the book value, a gain will be recorded. If the amount received is less than the book value, a loss is recorded.
