3. 15. A ball of mass 5 kg and a block of mass 12 kg are attached by a lightweight cord that passes over a frictionless pulley of negligible mass as shown in the figure. The block lies on a frictionless incline of angle 30o . Find the magnitude of the acceleration of the two objects and the tension in the cord. Take g =


Answer:

To solve this problem, we need to use Newton's laws of motion and the principle of conservation of energy.

First, let's draw a free-body diagram for the block and the ball:

Block:

Weight mg = 12 kg * 9.8 m/s^2 = 117.6 N, acting vertically downwards

Normal force N = mg cos(30°) = 102 N, acting perpendicular to the incline

Force of gravity component down the incline = mg sin(30°) = 58.8 N

Frictional force F_f = 0 (frictionless incline)

Ball:

Weight mg = 5 kg * 9.8 m/s^2 = 49 N, acting vertically downwards

Tension in the cord T, acting upwards

Force of gravity component down = mg = 49 N

Since the cord is lightweight and the pulley is frictionless and of negligible mass, the tension T is the same for both objects.

Now, let's apply Newton's second law of motion to each object:

Block:

F_net = ma, where F_net is the net force acting on the block, m is its mass, and a is its acceleration.

F_net = mg sin(30°) - T, since the block is moving up the incline and the tension is acting upwards.

Plugging in the values, we get: 117.6 N * sin(30°) - T = 12 kg * a

Ball:

F_net = ma, where F_net is the net force acting on the ball, m is its mass, and a is its acceleration.

F_net = T - mg, since the ball is moving downwards and the tension is acting upwards.

Plugging in the values, we get: T - 49 N = 5 kg * a

We can solve these two equations simultaneously to find the acceleration a and the tension T:

117.6 N * sin(30°) - T = 12 kg * a

T - 49 N = 5 kg * a

Adding the two equations, we get:

117.6 N * sin(30°) - 49 N = 17 kg * a

Simplifying, we get:

a = (117.6 N * sin(30°) - 49 N) / 17 kg = 2.56 m/s^2

Substituting the value of a into one of the earlier equations, we get:

T - 49 N = 5 kg * a = 12.8 N

Therefore, the tension in the cord is:

T = 12.8 N + 49 N = 61.8 N

So the magnitude of the acceleration of the two objects is 2.56 m/s^2 and the tension in the cord is 61.8 N.


4. Two packing crates of masses 10.0 kg and 5.00 kg are connected by a light string that passes over a frictionless pulley as shown. The 5.00 kg crate lies on a smooth incline of angle 40.0°. Find (a) the acceleration of the 5.00 kg crate and (b) the tension in the string.figure:​


Answer:

4.44 m/s² and 53.72 N

Explanation:

let:

m1 = 5 kg

m2 = 10 kg

theta = 40°

for equation in m1: T - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a (it's positive because it's accelerating upward)

for equation in m2: T - m2(g) = -m2a (it's negative because it's accelerating downward)

Subtract equation in m2 from equation in m1:

(T - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a) - (T - m2(g) = -m2a)

(- m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a) - (- m2(g) = -m2a)

m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a + m2a

With that we get the equation for acceleration:

m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a + m2a

m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) = (m1 + m2)a

m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) / m1 + m2 = (m1 + m2)a / m1 + m2

m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) / m1 + m2 = a

Plugging in the values, we get: a = 4.44 m/s²

In getting the equation for tension, substitute the equation for acceleration in equation for m1 (pwede din sa eq. for m2):

T - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1a

T - m1(g)(sin theta) = m1(m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) / m1 + m2)

T = m1(m2(g) - m1(g)(sin theta) + m1(g)(sin theta) / m1 + m2)

T = (m1m2(1+sin theta) / m1 + m2) g

Plugging in the values, we get: T = 53.72 kg.m/s² or N

7. A 15-kg block rests on a level, frictionless surface and is attached by a light string to a 10-kg hanging mass where the string passes over a massless frictionless pulley. if g = 9.8 m/s2, what is the tension in the connecting string?


This can be answered with an aid of a force diagram (see attached).

Before going over the problem, assumptions have to be made that are aligned with the Laws of Motion.

Since the block is on a frictionless surface and is attached to a hanging block, the 15-kg block must be accelerating to the right because there is no opposing force that cancels out the Ft force acting on the 15-kg block. Since the string is assumed to have no mass, and that the pulley is frictionless and massless, the tension pulling the 15-kg block to the right (see diagram) is equal to the tension pulling the 10-kg mass upward.The 15-kg block is accelerating to the right while the 10-kg block is accelerating downards. They have the same numerical value of the acceleration.

Doing the force diagram and looking at the entire system as a single body (meaning the two blocks, the string and the pulley are treated as one body), the resulting equation from Newton's 2nd Law is:

[tex]\Sigma F = m_{T}a \\F_T - F_T + W_2 = m_{T}a\\W_2 =m_{T}a\\a = \frac{W_2}{m_T} =  \frac{m_{2}g}{m_T} \\\\a = \frac{m_{2}g}{m_1 + m_2}[/tex]

It is not necessary to solve for the acceleration since what is looked for is tension on the string F_T. To solve this, we apply Newton's 2nd law on any of the two bodies (since they have the same value of tension). For simplification, the 15-kg mass will be chosen.

Again, look at the diagram as a guide.

[tex]\Sigma F = m_1 a\\F_T = m_1 (\frac{m_{2}g}{m_1 + m_2} )\\\\F_T = \frac{m_{1}m_{2}}{m_1+m_2} g\\\\F_T = \frac{15(10)}{15+10} (9.8)\\\\F_T = 58.8  N[/tex]

Thus, the tension on the string is 58.8 N

12. Applying Newton’s Laws Two blocks are connected to each other by a light cord passing over a small frictionless pulley as shown in the figure below. Block A has mass 5.00 kg and block B has mass 4.00 kg. If block B descends at a constant acceleration of 2.00 m/s2 when set in motion, find the coefficient of kinetic friction between block A and the table.


To solve the problem, we can use Newton's laws of motion. We can begin by drawing free-body diagrams for each of the blocks.

For block A, the forces acting on it are its weight (mg) pointing downwards and the tension in the cord (T) pointing upwards. Since block A is not moving vertically, the net force acting on it must be zero. Therefore, we have:

T - mg = 0

For block B, the forces acting on it are its weight (mg) pointing downwards, the tension in the cord (T) pointing upwards, and the force of kinetic friction (fk) pointing to the left. Since block B is moving with constant acceleration, the net force acting on it must be:

ma = T - mg - fk

where a is the acceleration of block B.

We know that a = 2.00 m/s^2 and that the masses of the blocks are m_A = 5.00 kg and m_B = 4.00 kg. We can use the first equation to eliminate T from the second equation:

T = mg

Substituting this into the second equation and solving for fk, we get:

fk = m_B (a + g) - T = m_B (a + g - g) = m_B a

where g is the acceleration due to gravity. Substituting the known values, we get:

fk = (4.00 kg) (2.00 m/s^2) = 8.00 N

Now we can use the coefficient of kinetic friction (μk) to relate the force of friction to the normal force (N) acting on block A. We know that:

fk = μk N

Since block A is not moving horizontally, the net force acting on it must be zero. Therefore, the tension in the cord must be equal in magnitude to the force of friction:

T = fk

Substituting the known values, we get:

μk N = 8.00 N

N = mg = (5.00 kg) (9.81 m/s^2) = 49.05 N

Solving for μk, we get:

μk = fk / N = 8.00 N / 49.05 N ≈ 0.163

Therefore, the coefficient of kinetic friction between block A and the table is approximately 0.163.

To solve this problem, we need to apply Newton's laws of motion. We'll start by drawing a free body diagram for each block:

Block A:
- Force of gravity (mg) pointing down
- Tension force (T) pointing up
- Force of friction (f) pointing left
- Normal force (N) pointing up

Block B:
- Force of gravity (mg) pointing down
- Tension force (T) pointing up

Since the pulley is frictionless, the tension force is the same for both blocks. We can use this fact to write two equations:

For Block A:
mg - f = ma

For Block B:
T - mg = mb * a

We know that Block B has a constant acceleration of 2.00 m/s^2. We can use this value to solve for T:

T = mb * a + mg
T = 4.00 kg * 2.00 m/s^2 + 4.00 kg * 9.81 m/s^2
T = 49.24 N

Now we can use this value of T to solve for the coefficient of kinetic friction between Block A and the table. We know that Block A is not accelerating, so we can set its net force equal to zero:

mg - f = 0
f = mg

We also know that the force of friction is equal to the coefficient of friction times the normal force:

f = μ * N

Substituting mg for f and solving for μ, we get:

μ = f/N = mg/N

We can find N by summing the forces in the vertical direction:

N - mg = 0
N = mg

Substituting mg for N, we get:

μ = mg/mg
μ = 1

Therefore, the coefficient of kinetic friction between Block A and the table is 1.

The mass of box A is 5.0 kg and the mass of box B is 2.0kg. The entire system will move together with the same acceleration and velocity. Find the acceleration of the system.​


[tex] \Large \mathbb{SOLUTION:} [/tex]

[tex] \begin{array}{l} \begin{cases} \: \sf T_{1} = T_{2} = T \\ \: \sf a_{A} = a_{B} = a \\ \: \sf f_{k} = \mu_{k} N \end{cases} \\ \\ \footnotesize \textsf{Refer to the attached picture.} \\ \\ \bullet \: \textsf{Projecting Newton's Second Law for box A} \\ \quad \textsf{gives us} \\ \\ \sf \sum F_x = - f_{k} + T = m_{A}a \: \:\: \& \\ \sf \sum F_y = N - m_{A}g = 0 \\ \\ \bullet \: \textsf{Projecting Newton's Law for box B on y- axis} \\ \quad \textsf{gives us} \\ \\ \sf \sum F_y = m_{B}g - T = m_{B}a \\ \\ \textsf{Now, express a in terms of }\sf m_{A}, m_{b}, \mu_{k}, \text{and } \\ \textsf{acceleration due to gravity }\sf g. \\ \\ \begin{cases} \: \sf - \mu_{k}(m_{A}g) + T = m_{A}a \quad & \sf (1) \\ \: \sf m_{B}g - T = m_{B}a & \sf (2) \end{cases} \\ \\ \textsf{Add (1) and (2) to eliminate T.} \\ \\ \sf m_{B}g - \mu_{k}(m_{A}g) = a(m_{A} + m_{B}) \\ \\ \implies \sf a = \dfrac{g(m_{B} - \mu_{k}m_{A})}{m_{A} + m_{B}} \\ \\ \textsf{Given that }\sf \mu_k = 0.20, m_A = 5.0\: kg,\: and \\ \sf m_B = 2.0\: kg, \\ \\ \sf a = 9.81 \left[\dfrac{2.0 - 0.2(5.0)}{5.0 + 2.0}\right] \\ \\ \therefore \boxed{\sf a \approx 1.401\: \text{m/s}^2} \end{array} [/tex]
