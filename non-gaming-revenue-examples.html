Price elasticity of demand refers to the degree to which the quantity demanded of a good or service changes in response to a change in its price. If the demand for a good is elastic, then a decrease in price will result in a proportionally larger increase in quantity demanded, leading to an increase in total revenue. Conversely, if the demand for a good is inelastic, a decrease in price will lead to a proportionally smaller increase in quantity demanded, resulting in a decrease in total revenue. For example, if the price of a luxury car is reduced by 10% and the quantity demanded increases by 15%, then the demand for the car is elastic, and the total revenue of the car company will increase.

Explanation:

The marginal revenue and marginal cost approach is a method used by businesses to determine the optimal level of production that will maximize profits. Here is an example to illustrate this approach:

Let's say a company produces and sells widgets. The cost of producing each widget is $5, and the company sells each widget for $10. The company wants to determine the optimal level of production that will maximize its profits.

To use the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach, the company needs to calculate its marginal revenue and marginal cost at different levels of production. Marginal revenue is the additional revenue generated by producing and selling one additional unit of the product. Marginal cost is the additional cost incurred by producing and selling one additional unit of the product.

Here is a table that shows the marginal revenue and marginal cost at different levels of production:

| Quantity | Total Revenue | Marginal Revenue | Total Cost | Marginal Cost |

|----------|--------------|-----------------|------------|---------------|

| 1 | $10 | $10 | $5 | $5 |

| 2 | $20 | $10 | $10 | $5 |

| 3 | $30 | $10 | $15 | $5 |

| 4 | $40 | $10 | $20 | $5 |

| 5 | $50 | $10 | $25 | $5 |

| 6 | $60 | $10 | $30 | $5 |

As we can see from the table, the marginal revenue remains constant at $10 for each additional unit produced and sold, while the marginal cost remains constant at $5. Therefore, the company should continue to produce and sell widgets as long as the marginal revenue is greater than or equal to the marginal cost.

In this example, the optimal level of production would be to produce and sell five widgets, where the marginal revenue ($10) is equal to the marginal cost ($5). Any production level beyond this point would result in a decrease in profits, as the marginal cost would exceed the marginal revenue.

Therefore, the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach can help businesses determine the optimal level of production that will maximize profits.

18. example of marginal revenue and marginal cost approach​


Answer:

Marginal revenue and marginal cost approach is a technique used to analyze the production processes and pricing strategies of companies. Here is an example of how this approach is used:

Suppose there is a company that produces and sells bicycles. The company wants to determine the optimal price to sell its bicycles and the optimal level of production to maximize profits. To do this, the company can use the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach.

Marginal revenue is the additional revenue generated from selling one more unit of a product. Marginal cost, on the other hand, is the additional cost incurred from producing and selling one more unit of a product.

To find the optimal price and level of production, the company will need to find the point where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.

For example, the company might find that it can produce and sell 100 bicycles at a cost of $50 per unit. If it sells the bicycles for $100 each, the company will earn $10,000 in total revenue. The marginal cost of producing one more bicycle is $50, and the marginal revenue of selling one more bicycle at this price is also $50. At this point, the company is maximizing its profits, as any increase in production beyond 100 bicycles would result in higher marginal costs than marginal revenues.

However, if the company decides to lower the price of bicycles to $80, it might be able to sell more bicycles and generate more revenue. But at what point will the additional revenue from selling more bicycles be countered by the additional cost of producing more bicycles?

Suppose the company decides to sell 150 bicycles at this lower price of $80 per unit. The marginal cost of producing 150 bicycles might increase to $65 per unit due to the need to hire additional workers and incur other expenses. But the marginal revenue of selling each bicycle at $80 remains the same. In this case, the company is still maximizing its profits as marginal revenue is still equal to marginal cost.

By using the marginal revenue and marginal cost approach, the company can determine the optimal price and level of production to maximize profit and make informed decisions about its production and pricing strategies.

20. example of total revenue minus total cost approach​


Answer:

The total revenue minus total cost approach is a method used in economics to calculate a firm's profit. It involves subtracting the total cost of production from the total revenue generated by sales in order to determine the amount of profit or loss that the firm has earned.

Explanation:

For example, let's say a firm produces and sells 1,000 units of a product at a price of $10 per unit. The firm incurs a total cost of $6,000 to produce these units. Using the total revenue minus total cost approach, we can calculate the firm's profit as follows:

Total revenue = 1,000 units x $10 per unit = $10,000

Total cost = $6,000

Profit = Total revenue - Total cost = $10,000 - $6,000 = $4,000

In this example, the firm's profit is $4,000. If the firm had incurred a total cost of $7,000 instead of $6,000, its profit would have been only $3,000, indicating that it is not operating as efficiently as it could be.

The total revenue minus total cost approach is a simple and straightforward method of calculating a firm's profit and is commonly used by businesses of all sizes. By using this approach, firms can quickly assess their profitability and make informed decisions about pricing, production levels, and other aspects of their operations.
