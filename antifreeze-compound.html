ΔTf = Kf x molality

molality = ΔTf / Kf

molality = (-1.05 degrees C) / (Kf for benzene)

21. The antifreeze commonly used in car radiators is ethylene glycol, C2H4(OH)2. How many grams of ethylene glycol should be added to 1.00 kg of water to produce a solution that freezes at -15.0°C?​


Given:

mass of solvent = 1.00 kg

[tex]\text{FP}_{solution} = -\text{15.0°C}[/tex]

solute: ethylene glycol [C₂H₄(OH)₂]

solvent: water

Required:

mass of solute

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the freezing point depression of the solution.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = \text{FP}_{solvent} - \text{FP}_{solution}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = \text{0°C} - (-\text{15.0°C})[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = \text{15.0°C}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\Delta T_{\text{f}}}{K_{\text{f}}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{15.0°C}}{\text{1.86°C/molal}}[/tex]

molality = 8.064516 molal

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

Note: 1 molal = 1 mol/kg

moles of solute = molality × mass of solvent

moles of solute = 8.064516 mol/kg × 1.00 kg

moles of solute = 8.064516 mol

Step 4: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

molar mass of solute = (12.01 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 6) + (16.00 g/mol × 2)

molar mass of solute = 62.068 g/mol

Step 5: Calculate the mass of solute.

mass of solute = moles of solute × molar mass of solute

mass of solute = 8.064516 mol × 62.068 g/mol

30. 4. You add 1.00 kg of ethylene glycol (C2H602) antifreeze to 4450 g of water in your car'sradiator. What are the boiling and freezing points of the solution?​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{1.00 kg = 1000 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{62.068 g/mol} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{4450 g = 4.45 kg} \\ & K_{\text{b}} = 0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m \\ & K_{\text{f}} = 1.86^{\circ}\text{C/}m \\ & T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} = 100^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & T^{\circ}_{\text{f}} = 0^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{1000 g}}{\text{62.068 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{16.111 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{16.111 mol}}{\text{4.45 kg}} \\ & = \text{3.6204 mol/kg} \\ & = 3.6204 \: m \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the boiling-point elevation and the freëzing-point depression.

• For ∆Tb

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta T_{\text{b}} & = K_{\text{b}}m \\ & = (0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m)(3.6204 \: m) \\ & = 1.9^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For ∆Tf

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta T_{\text{f}} & = K_{\text{f}}m \\ & = (1.86^{\circ}\text{C/}m)(3.6204 \: m) \\ & = 6.73^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the boiling and the freëzing point of the solution.

• For the boiling point

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_{\text{b}} & = T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} + \Delta T_{\text{b}} \\ & = 100^{\circ}\text{C} + 1.9^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & = \boxed{101.9^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For the freëzing point

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_{\text{f}} & = T^{\circ}_{\text{f}} - \Delta T_{\text{f}} \\ & = 0^{\circ}\text{C} - 6.73^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & = \boxed{-6.73^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the boiling point and the freëzing point of the solution are 101.9°C and -6.73°C, respectively.
