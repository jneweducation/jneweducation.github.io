Justify your answer​


It is ultimately up to the individual investor to decide whether they want to be a stockholder or bondholder in Namnama Corporation. Both options have their own benefits and risks, and the decision should be based on the investor's financial goals and risk tolerance.

One benefit of being a stockholder is the potential for capital appreciation. If the company performs well and its stock price increases, the value of the investor's shares will also increase, potentially leading to a profit when the shares are sold. However, there is also the risk that the stock price could decrease, leading to a loss.

On the other hand, being a bondholder means receiving a fixed rate of interest on the investment. This can provide a steady stream of income for the investor, but there is also the risk that the company may default on its bond payments, leading to a loss.

Ultimately, the investor should carefully consider their financial goals and risk tolerance before deciding whether to invest in Namnama Corporation as a stockholder or bondholder. It may also be helpful to seek the advice of a financial advisor or do further research on the company and the investment options available.

15. A putable bond gives the bondholder the right to sell the bond


Putable Bond

When and if the corporation takes specific acts, such as being purchased, acquiring another company, or issuing a sizable amount of extra debt, putable bonds provide the bondholders the opportunity to sell the bond at a price higher than par value by the amount of one year's interest payment.

True

A putable bond gives the bondholder the right to sell the bond

A putable bond, also known as a put bond or retractable bond, is a type of bond that gives the bondholder (investor) the right but not the responsibility to demand that the issuer repay the bond before its maturity date.It is a bond with a put option built into it, in other words. Bonds that can be put are the inverse of bonds that can be called.

The justification for putable bonds is connected to the inverse relationship between interest rates and bond prices, just like callable bonds. Bonds offer investors security from prospective interest rate hikes because their value decreases as interest rates climb.

By offering lower yields on the bonds, bond issuers lower their cost of debt concurrently. Investors are willing to accept lower returns in exchange for the freedom to sell their investments should the market turn against them.
