25. pangungusap tungkul kay william h. taft​


Answer:

William Howard Taft (September 15, 1857 – March 8, 1930) was the 27th president of the United States (1909–1913) and the tenth Chief Justice of the United States (1921–1930), the only person to have held both offices. Taft was elected president in 1908, the chosen successor of Theodore Roosevelt, but was defeated for reelection by Woodrow Wilson in 1912 after Roosevelt split the Republican vote by running as a third-party candidate. In 1921, President Warren G. Harding appointed Taft to be chief justice, a position he held until a month before his death.

29. Baed on william kay' and kohlberg' tage of moral development point in which tage are you.


WILLIAM KAY AND KOHLBERG STAGE OF DEVELOPMENT POINT

According to William Kay, there are three phases of moral development: pre-conventional, conventional, and post-conventional. People make moral judgements in the pre-conventional stage based on self-interest and deference to authority.

People make decisions during the conventional stage based on cultural norms and expectations. In the post-conventional stage, people base their moral judgments on their own set of values and beliefs.

William Kay's emphasis on the importance of cultural and sociological factors on moral reasoning is one of the most significant contributions to his theory of moral growth. According to Kay, exposure to various cultural norms, beliefs, and practices has an impact on how people develop their moral character.

In Kay's theory, the significance of cultural relativism is emphasized. According to this theory, moral standards and values can differ between cultures and should be assessed in the context of each one. This viewpoint has important ramifications for appreciating and comprehending cultural variety as well as fostering intercultural understanding.

In addition, Kay's theory highlights how crucial socializing is for the formation of morality. Through interactions with their family, friends, and other societal members, children pick up moral beliefs and conventions. As a result, Kay's theory emphasizes how crucial it is to foster a social environment that encourages moral conduct and ideals.

Overall, Kay's theory highlights the value of cultural variety and socialization in fostering ethical conduct and offers a useful framework for understanding the significance of cultural and societal influences in moral growth.
