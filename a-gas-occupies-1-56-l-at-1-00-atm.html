1. 1. A gas occupies 4.31 L at a pressure of 0.755 atm. Determine the volume occupied by the gas if the pressure is increased to 2.25 atm


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{0.755 atm}[/tex]

[tex]V_{1} = \text{4.31 L}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{2.25 atm}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]V_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]P_{1}V_{1} = P_{2}V_{2}[/tex]

[tex]V_{2} = V_{1} × \frac{P_{1}}{P_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]V_{2} = \text{4.31 L} × \frac{\text{0.755 atm}}{\text{2.25 atm}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{V_{2} = \text{1.45 L}}[/tex]

Further explanation

Some of the laws regarding gas, can apply to ideal gas (volume expansion does not occur when the gas is heated), among others

Boyle's law at constant T, P = 1 / VCharles's law, at constant P, V = TAvogadro's law, at constant P and T, V = n

So that the three laws can be combined into a single gas equation, the ideal gas equation

[tex]PV= nRT[/tex]

In Boyle's law, it is stated that

In a closed system, the absolute pressure by an ideal gas is inversely proportional to the volume it occupies if the temperature is constant

So it can be stated

P₁ . V₁ = P₂ . V₂

A gas occupies 1.56 L at 1.00 atm. and the pressure becomes 3.00 atm

So:

Initial conditions:  

P₁ = 1 atm

V₁ = 1.56 L

Final conditions

P₂ = 3 atm

The volume of this gas (V₂):

V₂ = P₁ V₁/ P₂

[tex]V_2 = \frac{1 x 1.56}{3}[/tex]

V₂ = 0.52 L

4. a gas occupies 1.56 L at 1.00 atm. what will be the volume of this gas if the pressure becomes 3.09 atm?​


Answer:

To solve for the new volume, we can use Boyle's law, which states that the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure, assuming that temperature and amount of gas are constant.

Boyle's Law: P1V1 = P2V2

Where:

P1 = initial pressure = 1.00 atm

V1 = initial volume = 1.56 L

P2 = final pressure = 3.09 atm

V2 = final volume (unknown)

Solving for V2:

P1V1 = P2V2

(1.00 atm)(1.56 L) = (3.09 atm)(V2)

1.56 L = (3.09 atm)(V2) / (1.00 atm)

V2 = (1.56 L)(1.00 atm) / (3.09 atm)

V2 = 0.505 L

Therefore, the new volume of the gas is 0.505 L.

9. a gas occupies 1.56L at 1 atm. what will be the volume of this gas if the pressure become 4 atm​


[tex]\huge\color{lavender}{\boxed{Answer:}}[/tex]

The volume of the gas at a pressure of 4 atm would be approximately 0.39 liters, assuming that the temperature remains constant.

[tex]\huge\color{lavender}{\boxed{Solution:}}[/tex]

To solve this problem, we can again use the combined gas law equation:

(P1 x V1) / T1 = (P2 x V2) / T2

where P1, V1, and T1 are the initial pressure, volume, and temperature of the gas, respectively, and P2 and V2 are the pressure and volume of the gas at the new conditions.

We can rearrange this equation to solve for V2:

V2 = (P1 x V1 x T2) / (P2 x T1)

Assuming that the temperature remains constant, we can substitute the given values:

V2 = (1 atm x 1.56 L x T2) / (4 atm x T1)

We don't have specific values for T1 or T2, but since they are assumed to be constant, we can cancel them out in the equation. This leaves us with:

V2 = (1 atm x 1.56 L) / 4 atm

V2 = 0.39 L (rounded to two decimal places)

PV = nRT

P = pressure, atm  

V = volume, liter  

n = number of moles  

R = gas constant = 0.08205 L.atm / mol K  

T = temperature, Kelvin

Given

mass = 4.96 g

volume = 2.13 L

temperature = 20 °C = 293 K

pressure = 1 atm

Required

The molar mass

MM = 4.96 : 0.0886

30. A gas occupies 1.56 L at 1.00 atm. Wha will be the volume of this gas if the pressure ecomes 3.00 atm? ​


Answer:

Assuming the temperature and amount of gas remain constant, the new volume can be calculated using Boyle's Law, which states that the pressure and volume of a gas are inversely proportional to each other at constant temperature. Using the formula:

P1V1 = P2V2

where P1 and V1 are the initial pressure and volume, and P2 and V2 are the final pressure and volume, we can solve for V2:

V2 = (P1 x V1) / P2

Substituting the given values:

V2 = (1.00 atm x 1.56 L) / 3.00 atm

V2 = 0.52 L

Therefore, the volume of the gas at 3.00 atm would be 0.52 L.
