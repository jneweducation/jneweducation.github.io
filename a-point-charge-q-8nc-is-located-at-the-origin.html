4. Four point charges are equally separated by 1.00m in air. If q1=3nC, q2=-4nC, q3= 0nC, and q4=-5.nC, what is the electric field at a point half way between q2 and q3?​


Answer:

To find the electric field at a point halfway between q2 and q3, we can use the principle of superposition, which states that the net electric field at a point due to multiple point charges is the vector sum of the electric fields at that point due to each individual charge.

First, we need to find the distance between the midpoint and each of the four charges. Since the charges are equally separated by 1.00 m, the distance between the midpoint and each charge is 0.50 m.

Next, we can use Coulomb's law to find the electric field at the midpoint due to each individual charge. Coulomb's law states that the electric field at a point due to a point charge is given by:

E = k * q / r^2

where E is the electric field, k is the Coulomb constant (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2), q is the charge, and r is the distance between the point charge and the point where the electric field is being calculated.

Using this formula, we can calculate the electric field at the midpoint due to each of the four charges:

E1 = k * q1 / r1^2 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2) * (3 x 10^-9 C) / (0.5 m)^2 = 1.08 x 10^6 N/C

E2 = k * q2 / r2^2 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2) * (-4 x 10^-9 C) / (0.5 m)^2 = -1.44 x 10^6 N/C

E3 = k * q3 / r3^2 = 0, since q3 is zero

E4 = k * q4 / r4^2 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2) * (-5 x 10^-9 C) / (0.5 m)^2 = -1.8 x 10^6 N/C

To find the net electric field at the midpoint, we add the electric fields due to each individual charge:

E = E1 + E2 + E3 + E4 = (1.08 x 10^6 N/C) + (-1.44 x 10^6 N/C) + 0 + (-1.8 x 10^6 N/C) = -2.16 x 10^6 N/C

Therefore, the electric field at the midpoint between q2 and q3 is -2.16 x 10^6 N/C.

V = k q/r

V     is the electric potential, unit is in volts, V

k      is the coulomb constant, 9 x 10⁹ Nm²/C²

q     is the charge, unit is in Coulombs, C

q = 2 µC = 2 x 10⁻⁶ C

V = k q/r

V = k q/r

W = q ΔV

20. A +40μC point charge is placed at the origin. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the electric field created by the point charge at the following locations: Point A (5,0) Point B (3,4)​


Answer:The electric field created by a point charge is given by:

E = k * q / r^2

where E is the electric field, k is Coulomb's constant (9 x 10^9 N*m^2/C^2), q is the charge, and r is the distance from the charge.

a) Point A (5,0):

The distance between the point charge at the origin and point A is r = 5 m.

The direction of the electric field is radial and points directly away from the point charge at the origin.

Substituting the values into the equation, we get:

E = (9 x 10^9 N*m^2/C^2) * (40 x 10^-6 C) / (5 m)^2

= 2.88 x 10^6 N/C

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric field at point A is 2.88 x 10^6 N/C, and it points directly away from the point charge at the origin.

b) Point B (3,4):

The distance between the point charge at the origin and point B is r = 5 m.

The direction of the electric field is radial and points directly away from the point charge at the origin.

The x-component and y-component of the electric field can be calculated as:

Ex = k * q * x / r^3

= (9 x 10^9 N*m^2/C^2) * (40 x 10^-6 C) * 3 m / (5 m)^3

= 4.104 x 10^5 N/C

Ey = k * q * y / r^3

= (9 x 10^9 N*m^2/C^2) * (40 x 10^-6 C) * 4 m / (5 m)^3

= 5.472 x 10^5 N/C

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric field at point B is:

E = sqrt(Ex^2 + Ey^2)

= sqrt((4.104 x 10^5 N/C)^2 + (5.472 x 10^5 N/C)^2)

= 6.89 x 10^5 N/C

And the direction of the electric field is:

θ = arctan(Ey/Ex)

= arctan((5.472 x 10^5 N/C) / (4.104 x 10^5 N/C))

= 53.13°

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric field at point B is 6.89 x 10^5 N/C, and it points at an angle of 53.13° above the x-axis (in the second quadrant).

In this problem, we have two point charges, q₁=+10nC at the origin and q₂=-20nC at x=4m. We need to find the net electric field at two points, P1 at x=2m and P2 at x=6m.

We can solve this problem by using the superposition principle, which states that the net electric field at any point is the vector sum of the electric fields due to each of the individual charges.

Let's start by finding the electric field due to q₁ at P1 and P2.
