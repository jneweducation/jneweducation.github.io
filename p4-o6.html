11. calculate the molality of 78.3 g glucose C6 H12 O6 in 1Liter of water​


Answer:

molality = 0.435 m

Explanation:

Note: The density of water is 1 kg/L.

Calculate the mass of water (solvent)

mass solvent = 1 L × (1 kg / 1 L) = 1 kg

mass solvent = 1 kg

Calculate the number of moles of glucose (solute).

n solute = 78.3 g × (1 mol / 180 g)

n solute = 0.435 mol

Calculate the molality of the solution.

molality = n solute / mass solvent

molality = 0.435 mol / 1 kg

molality = 0.435 mol/kg

molality = 0.435 m

16. calculate the percent composition of each element in c6 h12 o6​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of C₆H₁₂O₆.

Note that the molar masses of C, H, and O are 12.01 g, 1.008 g, and 16.00 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass of} \: \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 & = \text{6(12.01 g) + 12(1.008 g) + 6(16.00 g)} \\ & = \text{72.06 g + 12.10 g + 96.00 g} \\ & = \text{180.16 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Solve for the percentage composition of C₆H₁₂O₆.

• For C

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{C} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of C}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{6 \times \text{12.01 g}}{\text{180.16 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{40.00\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For H

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{H} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of H}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{12 \times \text{1.008 g}}{\text{180.16 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{6.714\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For O

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{O} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of O}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{6 \times \text{16.00 g}}{\text{180.16 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{53.29\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, C₆H₁₂O₆ is composed of 40.00% carbon, 6.714% hydrogen, and 53.29% oxygen by mass.


17. B. Consider a 1.245 grams sample of glucose (C6 H12 O6). Calculate the ff:Moles of C6 H12 O6 =Molecules of glucose in the sample =Moles of Carbon in the sample =Mass of Carbon in the sample =Particles of Carbon in the sample =​


Answer:

Molecular weight of glucose (C6 H12 O6) = 6(12.01) + 12(1.01) + 6(16.00) = 180.18 g/mol

Moles of glucose = Mass of sample (in grams) / Molecular weight of glucose

Moles of glucose = 1.245 g / 180.18 g/mol = 0.006907 mol

To calculate the number of molecules of glucose in the sample, we need to use Avogadro's number.

Number of molecules = Moles of glucose x Avogadro's number

Number of molecules = 0.006907 mol x 6.022 x 10^23 molecules/mol = 4.16 x 10^21 molecules

Each molecule of glucose contains 6 carbon atoms.

Moles of carbon = Moles of glucose x 6

Moles of carbon = 0.006907 mol x 6 = 0.04144 mol

Mass of carbon = Moles of carbon x Atomic weight of carbon

Mass of carbon = 0.04144 mol x 12.01 g/mol = 0.498 g

Particles of carbon = Moles of carbon x Avogadro's number

Particles of carbon = 0.04144 mol x 6.022 x 10^23 particles/mol = 2.494 x 10^22 particles
