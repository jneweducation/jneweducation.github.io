We would need to know the magnitudes and positions of the other charges in the square to determine the electric field and hence the charge on q2.

However, we can determine the direction of the electric field at a point due to multiple charges by calculating the vector sum of the electric fields due to each individual charge at that point. The direction of the electric field at any point will be along the direction of this vector sum.

When the switch S is open, there is no current flowing in the circuit, and the potential difference across each resistor is equal to the voltage of the source (V). This is because the charges are unable to flow through the circuit, and thus there is no potential difference across any conductor.

When the switch S is closed, the circuit becomes a series circuit. The current I flows through the resistors R1 and R2 in a loop, starting from the positive terminal of the voltage source, flowing through R1, then R2, and back to the negative terminal of the voltage source.

Using Ohm's law (V = IR), we can calculate the current I in the circuit. The potential difference across each resistor is equal to I times the resistance of the resistor. Thus, the potential difference across R1 is IR1, and the potential difference across R2 is IR2. The sum of these potential differences must be equal to the voltage of the source (V).

V = IR1 + IR2

I = V / (R1 + R2)

27. Four equal charges of 2.0x10^6 C are situated at the corners of a square of side 1.00 m. What is the electric field at the center of the square?​


We can start by finding the electric field contribution from each of the charges at the center of the square, and adding them up.

The magnitude of the electric field from a point charge q at a distance r is given by:

E = k*q/r^2

where k is the Coulomb constant (8.99x10^9 Nm^2/C^2).

Since the charges are equal and the distance from each charge to the center of the square is the same (the diagonal of the square), we can simplify the calculation by first finding the electric field from one charge at a diagonal distance of d, and then multiplying it by the number of charges (4).

The diagonal of a square with side length s is:

d = sqrt(2)*s

Plugging in the values, we get:

d = sqrt(2)*1.00 m = 1.41 m

The distance from one corner charge to the center of the square is therefore r = d/2 = 0.705 m.

The electric field from one charge at that distance is:

E = k*q/r^2 = (8.99x10^9 Nm^2/C^2)*(2.0x10^6 C)/(0.705 m)^2

E = 4.87x10^12 N/C

Multiplying by 4 to account for all four charges, the total electric field at the center of the square is:

E_total = 4*E = 1.95x10^13 N/C

Therefore, the electric field at the center of the square is 1.95x10^13 N/C.

28. 2. Point charges of 2.0 x 10-⁹ C are situated at each of three corners of a square whose sides are 0.2 m. What would be the magnitude and direction of the resultant electrostatic force (Fe) on a point charge q = - 1 x10-⁹ C if it was placed at the vacant corner of the square? q2 = +2nC 0.2 m q1 = +2nC 0.2m 0.2m 93 = +2nC 0.2 m q=1 x10-⁹ C​


Using Coulomb's law, the electrostatic force (Fe) between the point charge at the vacant corner and the two other point charges can be calculated as:

Fe1 = k * q1 * q / d^2

Fe2 = k * q2 * q / d^2

where k is the Coulomb constant (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2), q1 and q2 are the charges at the other corners (2.0 x 10^-9 C each), q is the charge at the vacant corner (-1.0 x 10^-9 C), and d is the distance between the charges (0.2 m).

Fe1 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (2.0 x 10^-9 C) * (-1.0 x 10^-9 C) / (0.2 m)^2

Fe1 = -4.5 x 10^-7 N

Fe2 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (2.0 x 10^-9 C) * (-1.0 x 10^-9 C) / (0.2 m)^2

Fe2 = -4.5 x 10^-7 N

The magnitudes of the two forces are equal and opposite due to the charges being the same but opposite in sign.

The net force on the point charge at the vacant corner is the vector sum of Fe1 and Fe2, which can be found using the Pythagorean theorem:

Fnet^2 = Fe1^2 + Fe2^2

Fnet = sqrt((-4.5 x 10^-7 N)^2 + (-4.5 x 10^-7 N)^2)

Fnet = 6.4 x 10^-7 N

The direction of the net force can be found using trigonometry:

tan(theta) = Fe2 / Fe1

theta = atan(-4.5 x 10^-7 N / -4.5 x 10^-7 N)

theta = 45 degrees (to the left of the positive x-axis)

Therefore, the magnitude of the resultant electrostatic force on the point charge q is 6.4 x 10^-7 N and the direction is 45 degrees to the left of the positive x-axis.
