1. What volume will 5.6 moles of sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆) gas occupy if the temperature and pressure of the gas are 128°C and 9.4 atm?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P = \text{9.4 atm} \\ & n = \text{5.6 mol} \\ & T = 128^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{401.15 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Solve for the volume of the gas by using ideal gas equation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} V & = \frac{nRT}{P} \\ & = \frac{(\text{5.6 mol})\left(0.082057 \: \dfrac{\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}}\right)(\text{401.15 K})}{\text{9.4 atm}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{19.6 L}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the volume of the gas is 19.6 L.

A sample of 6.0 mole of sulfur hexafluoride (SF6) is present in a container with a volume of 22.4L. what is the pressure (in atm) of the gas in the temperature is 130°C?​

Note:

130°C + 273 = 403 K

Given Data:

V (volume) = 22.4 L

n (moles of gas) = 6 moles

T (Ktemperature) = 403 K

R (constant) = 0.0821 L•atm/mol•K

P (pressure) = Unknown

Solution:

We can solve this problem using the ideal gas law formula (PV=nRT), and to find P, we can use the formula P = nRT/V

P = nRT/V

  = 6 × 0.0821 × 403 / 22.4

  = 198.5178 atm / 22.4

  = 8.86 atm

Answer:

4. A sample of sulfur dioxide occupies a volume of 652 mL at 40oC and 720 mm Hg. What volume will the sulfur dioxide occupy at 760 mm Hg and zero degrees Celsius?


Explanation:

This is a combined gas law. Therefore, the equation is  [tex]\frac{P_{1} V_{1} }{T_{1} }[/tex]= [tex]\frac{P_{2}V_{2} }{T_{2} }[/tex]

And your given is:

P1 = 720 mmHg

V1 = 652 mL (convert to liters) -> 652 mL / 1000 = 0.652L

T1 = 40 degrees Celsius (convert to Kelvin) -> 40 + 273.15 = 313.15 K

P2 = 760 mmHg

V2 = ???

T2 = 0 degrees Celsius (convert to Kelvin) -> 0 + 273.15 = 273.15 K

Now, let's derive the formula so that V2 is isolated so that it's easier to solve.

[tex](\frac{P_{1} V_{1} }{T_{1} })(\frac{T_{2} }{P_{2} })[/tex] = [tex]\frac{P_{2}V_{2} }{T_{2} } (\frac{T_{2}}{P_{2}} )[/tex]

V2 = [tex]\frac{P_{1} V_{1}T_{2} }{T_{1} P_{2} }[/tex]

Then, substitute all the given values

V2 = (720mmHg * 0.652L * 273.15K) / (760mmHg *313.15K)

and calculate

V2 = 0.539 L is the volume :)

13. What is the volume in liters of 20.5 g of sulfur hexafluoride if sulfur hexafluoride has a density of 6.164 g/L? ​


Answer:

3.325 L

Explanation:

Density

Density is the space of a substance occupied with respect to the amount of matter in that substance. Density is also defined as the mass per unit of volume. The higher the density of the substance, the heavier it is. Density can be calculated by the formula:

Density = [tex]\frac{mass}{volume}[/tex]

In this problem, we are given the mass of sulfur hexafluoride of 20.5 grams and its density of 6.164 grams/Liter. We need to know the volume.

Step 1: Rearrange the formula in terms of volume. Hence,

Density = [tex]\frac{mass}{volume}[/tex]

Volume = [tex]\frac{mass}{density}[/tex]

Step 2: Substitute the given values to the resulting formula.

Volume = [tex]\frac{mass}{density}[/tex]

Volume = [tex]\frac{20.5 grams}{6.164 grams / Liter}[/tex] = 3.325 Liters

Therefore, the volume is 3.325 Liters.

19. Sulfur hexafluoride is a colorless, ordorless, very unreactive gas. Calculate the pressure (in atm) exerted by 187 grams of the gas in a steel vessel of volume 4.50 L at 79.5°C.​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & V = \text{4.50 L} \\ & mass = \text{187 g} \\ & T = 79.5^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{352.65 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of the gas (SF₆).

The molar masses of S and F are 32.06 g/mol and 18.998 g/mol, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} MM & = \text{32.06 g/mol + 6(18.998 g/mol)} \\ & = \text{146.05 g/mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of the gas.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n & = \frac{mass}{MM} \\ & = \frac{\text{187 g}}{\text{146.05 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{1.2804 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Solve for the pressure of the gas by using ideal gas equation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} P & = \frac{nRT}{V} \\ & = \frac{(\text{1.2804 mol})\left(0.082057 \: \dfrac{\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}}\right)(\text{352.65 K})}{\text{4.50 L}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{8.23 atm}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the pressure exerted is 8.23 atm.

26. Ideal Gas Law ( Use the GRESA method )1. 6.5 moles of carbon dioxide gas are present in a container with a volume of 31.5 L. What is the pressure of this gas (in atm) if the temperature is 65'C?2. How much space will 5.0 moles of sulfur hexafluoride occupy if the temperature and pressure of the gas are 125°C and 8.5 atm respectively?3. What is the mass of 6.2 L of nitrogen gas at 250C and 12.0 atm? ( molar mass of nitrogen= 14 g/mol)


Problem 1:Given:

[tex]V = \text{31.5 L}[/tex]

[tex]n = \text{6.5 mol}[/tex]

[tex]T = \text{65°C + 273 = 338 K}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]P[/tex]

Equation:

[tex]PV = nRT[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]P = \frac{nRT}{V}[/tex]

[tex]P = \frac{(\text{6.5 mol})(0.0821 \: \frac{\text{L • atm}}{\text{K • mol}})(\text{338 K})}{\text{31.5 L}}[/tex]

Answer:

[tex]\boxed{P = \text{5.7 atm}}[/tex]

Problem 2:Given:

[tex]P = \text{8.5 atm}[/tex]

[tex]n = \text{5.0 mol}[/tex]

[tex]T = \text{125°C + 273 = 398 K}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]P[/tex]

Equation:

[tex]PV = nRT[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]V = \frac{nRT}{P}[/tex]

[tex]V = \frac{(\text{5.0 mol})(0.0821 \: \frac{\text{L • atm}}{\text{K • mol}})(\text{398 K})}{\text{8.5 atm}}[/tex]

Answer:

[tex]\boxed{V = \text{19.2 L}}[/tex]

Problem 3:Given:

[tex]V = \text{6.2 L}[/tex]

[tex]P = \text{12.0 atm}[/tex]

[tex]T = \text{250°C + 273 = 523 K}[/tex]

[tex]\text{MM}_{\text{N}} = \text{14 g/mol}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{mass}_{\text{N}_{2}}[/tex]

Equations:

[tex]PV = nRT[/tex]

[tex]\text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{MM}_{\text{N}} × 2[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} × n[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of N₂.

[tex]n = \frac{PV}{RT}[/tex]

[tex]n = \frac{\text{(12.0 atm)(6.2 L)}}{(0.0821 \: \frac{\text{L • atm}}{\text{mol • K}})(\text{523 K})}[/tex]

[tex]n = \text{1.733 mol}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of N₂.

[tex]\text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{MM}_{\text{N}} × 2[/tex]

[tex]\text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{14 g/mol} × 2[/tex]

[tex]\text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{28 g/mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of N₂.

[tex]\text{mass}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{MM}_{\text{N}_{2}} × n[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{28 g/mol × 1.733 mol}[/tex]

Answer:

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass}_{\text{N}_{2}} = \text{48.5 g}}[/tex]

193 mL of O2 was collected over water on a day when the atmospheric pressure was 762 mmHg. The temperature of the water was 23.0 o C. How many grams of oxygen were collected?

Strategy:

Use Dalton's law and the vapor pressure of water at 23.0 o C to correct the pressure to units of atmoshperes.

PT = Poxygen +Pwater

At 23.0 o C the vapor pressure of water is 21.1 mmHg. (This can be found on a vapor pressure table.)

762 mmHg = Poxygen + 21.1 mmHg

Poxygen = 762 mmHg - 21.1 mmHg

Poxygen =741 mmHg

Convert the corrected pressure to atmospheres.

(741 mmHg) (1 atm / 760 mmHg) = 0.975 atm

PV = nRT (remember to put volume in liters and temperature in Kelvin)

(0.975 atm) (.193 L) = n (.0821 L atm / mol K) (298 K)

n = (0.975 atm) (.193 L) / (.0821 L atm / mol K) (298 K)

n = 7.69 X 10-^4 mol

(7.69 X 10-^4 mol) (32.0 g / 1 mol) = 2.46 X 10-^2 g
