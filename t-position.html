Based on the calculations performed, we came to the conclusion that:

[tex] \bold{a) \:\:\boxed{\boxed{\bold{ \: \: S(t) = \int \sin(t)\cdot\cos {}^{2} (t) \: dt \: \: }}}}[/tex][tex]\bold{b) \: \: \boxed{\boxed{ \bold{ \: \: S(t) = - \frac{ \cos {}^{3}(t) }{3} + \frac{1}{3}} \: \: }}}[/tex]

Explanation:

We have the following velocity function:

[tex]\: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \:\:\:\boxed{ \sf V(t) = \sin(t) \cdot \cos {}^{2} (t) }[/tex]

The objective is to determine the position function associated with velocity.

V(t) as the derivative of S(t).

In the derivative study part, we learned that the velocity function is the derivative of the space function with respect to time. Mathematically:

[tex] \sf{\: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \bullet \: \: \: \frac{dS(t)}{dt} = V(t)} \\[/tex]

Since we have the velocity function, we can substitute in this expression above.

[tex] \sf \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \frac{dS(t)}{dt} = \sin(t) \cdot \cos {}^{2} (t) \\[/tex]

Let's multiply both sides of this equation by the time differential.

[tex] \qquad \sf{\: \: \: \:\frac{dS(t)}{dt} \cdot dt = \sin(t)\cdot\cos {}^{2} (t)dt} \\ \\ \qquad \sf{ \: \: \: \: {dS(t)} = \sin(t)\cdot\cos {}^{2} (t) \: dt}[/tex]

This expression tells us that in a small infinitesimal variation of space with respect to time, it is given by the trigonometric expression.

As we know, the indefinite integral adds infinite parts, aiming to find the expression that represents the whole.

So let's integrate both sides:

[tex] \qquad \displaystyle \sf\int {dS(t)} = \int \sin(t)\cdot \cos {}^{2} (t) \: dt \\ \\ \qquad \displaystyle\boxed{\bold{ \: \: S(t) = \int \sin(t)\cdot \cos {}^{2} (t) \: dt \: \: }}[/tex]

So we have that this is the integral requested in item a).

Particular solution:

For item b), we must find the result of this integral above. The method we are going to use is basically integration by variable substitution.

Let's say then that [tex]\bold{ u = \cos(t)u=cos(t)} [/tex], then let's differentiate it.

[tex] \sf{u = \cos (t) \: \to \: \frac{du}{dt} = - \sin(t) \: \to \: - {du}= \sin(t) \: dt} \\[/tex]

Substituting the data into the integral:

[tex] \sf S(t) = \int \cos {}^{2} (t) \cdot \sin(t)dt \: \to \: S(t) = \int u {}^{2} \cdot ( - du) \\ \\ \sf S(t) = - \frac{u {}^{ 1 + 1} }{1 + 1} \: \: \to \: \: S(t) = - \frac{u {}^{3} }{3} \\ \\ \sf S(t) = - \frac{ \cos {}^{3} (t)}{3} + C, C\in\mathbb{R}[/tex]

Initial value problem:

In the statement of the question, we are provided with a condition, which is [tex]\bold{S(0) = 0}[/tex] , so we can find the particular solution by substituting this condition in the above expression.

[tex] \sf S(t) = - \frac{ \cos {}^{3} (t)}{3} + C, \: S(0) = 0 \\ \\ \sf 0 = - \frac{ \cos {}^ {3}(0) }{3} + C \: \: \to \: \: 0 = - \frac{1}{3} + C \\ \\ \boxed{ \sf C = \frac{1}{ 3} }[/tex]

So the particular solution is:

[tex]\: \: \: \: \: \: \: \: \boxed{\boxed{ \bold{ \: \: S(t) = - \frac{\cos {}^{3}(t) }{3} + \frac{1 }{3}} \: \: }} \\[/tex]
