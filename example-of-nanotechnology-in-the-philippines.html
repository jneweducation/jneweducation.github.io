4. In your own perspective, what is the main drawback of nanotechnology?Cite an example and expound your answer.


Answer:

Nanotechnology is too big of a subject to be included in one answer, and I certainly won’t write 1–2 book chapters for free. Pick a particular technology or process, and then we can beat it to death wrt. its drawbacks.

Too expensive? I can’t say that because sometimes we use physical processes as a catalyist to save on consumables, such as platinum meshes in chemical reactors or fuel cells.

Too new & untested? Can’t say that, because stained glass for instance has existed for more than 2 millenia.

Too complicated to design with? Not necessarily. Wet etching could be seen as an example of micro and nano-fabrication. Yet it’s as simple as a process can get.

Commercially uncompetitive? Not every nanotech is unobtanium like graphene. (the problem is that we can’t make extremely large surface areas of graphene for applications like aircraft fuselages for instance, and the other issue is that graphene loses a lot of its properties if it’s anything but perfect). Crystallography is nanotech per excellence used in manufacturing millions of lasers devices every day.

Fragility? Many products are very vulnerable to environmental factors (mechanical, electrostatic, ionising and nonionising radiation, bio-fouling) in their initial state, but lots of consumer and industrial products end up with some form of encapsulation. For Instance, any thin film of any material would fail any conventional impact test if not attached to a substrate; the question is whether the nuisance of adding the nanotech process to the already existing part can be justified economically by not adding it (reduced maintenance cost for instance).

Biotoxicity(nanotoxicology)? that also depends on the exact type of nanotech. For instance, carbon nanotubes and high aspect ratio nanomaterials(fibrous rigid molecules which are many times longer than wide) are suspected of potentially causing a disease similar to asbestos lung silicosis. Guidance at Oxford University: Nanotechnology. The biggest risk is to researchers and manufacturers working directly with such materials. Again, this is not a universally valid critique of nanotechnology.

Long-term product cycle (including disposal)? That’s another tricky area. We now know that colloidal nanosilver used for food preservation to reduce sugar content is not necessarily a good idea (argyria), and recycling old plastic to make synthetic material clothing is contaminating our drinking water with plastic fibres which are not easy to filter (see Blue Planet II documentary series). I think it would be unfair to talk about this, because product life cycles have only began to be studied in the last 16 years or so with the rising eco-trends from trade bodies like the European Commission (EU), and we constantly find every day that certain established technologies are more, or less polluting overall than we thought.

8. example of a nanotechnology and how does it work


Nanotechnology has the ability to transform a wide variety of industries, including health care and engineering.   In the other hand, nanotechnology (also known as molecular manufacturing) is the design, manufacture, and use of materials, components, and systems at the nanoscale. This is being celebrated as having the ability to improve energy quality, help clean up the atmosphere, and address big health issues. It is said that it would be able to dramatically increase manufacturing output at significantly lower prices. Nanotechnology products can be smaller, cheaper, lighter, and more efficient, and will take less resources and raw materials to produce.

Examples of Nano Technologies  

Food safety, medicine and health care, electricity, transportation, electronics, environmental protection, and manufacturing are all fields where nanotechnology is helping to develop goods. It's used in the automobile, electronics, and computer markets, as well as in consumer goods, textiles, and cosmetics, to name a few.  

Food - Salmonella and other toxins in food can be detected using nanosensors in packaging. Medicine - Scientists are working hard to develop medication delivery mechanisms for a variety of disorders, including cancer, heart disease, diabetes, and other age-related illnesses. The prospect of using nanotechnology to increase nerve cells is another promising advancement.   Energy - Nanotechnology is being used in a variety of energy-related applications, including improving the performance and cost-effectiveness of solar panels, developing new types of batteries, improving the efficiency of fuel production by improved catalysis, and developing better lighting systems.   Environment - Researchers are working on nanostructured filters that can strip virus cells and other impurities from water, potentially allowing for the development of safe, affordable, and plentiful drinking water. Chemical spill clean-up can be done using a nanofabric paper towel that can withstand 20 times its weight in oil.   Cosmetics - Nanoscale materials are used in a variety of cosmetics to enhance coverage, absorption, and cleaning.   Electronics - Organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) are nanostructured polymer films that are used in many new screen-based appliances (TVs, tablets, iPads, and so on) (OLEDs). These screens, among other aspects, are brighter, thinner, and have a higher image quality.

23. RESEARCH ABOUT THE NANO TECHNOLOGY AVAILABLE IN THE PHILIPPINES 1. LIST DOWN AT LEAST 3 NANOTECHNOLOGY AVAILABLE IN THE PHILIPPINES 2. EXPLAIN THE USE OF EACH NANOTECHNOLOGY​


Nanotechnology is the synthesis, manipulation, design, and application of a functional system or material at the nanoscale. It draws on multiple disciplines and has a wide variety of applications from agriculture, food safety, biosensors to biomedical diagnostics. In fact, in 2008 alone, global investment in nanotechnology R&D reached 10B USD. The Philippines has also launched its nanotechnology research initiative, the Department of Science and Technology-Philippine Council for Advanced Science and Technology Research and Development (DOST-PCASTRD) has the following sectors in its priority - semiconductor, information technology, energy, agriculture, medicine, and environmental protection. In the area of agriculture, notable products developed in the strengthened nanotechnology programs of the University of the Philippines Los Baños include: 1) nanosilica-based fertilizer that increases germination and growth rate of tomatoes; 2) fruitect® coating technology which boosts shelf life of high valued fruits such as mango and papaya; and 3) nanoencapsulated plant growth regulators which enhances germination and root development of coffee, banana, and cassava. The growing number of novel nanotechnology products in the country is surprisingly unsupported by low patent applications. However, due to the sheer number of potential applications and an overall increase in funding for nanotechnology R&D, there is a projected surge of patent applications. The USA alone had tripled patent applications on nanotechnology-derived products in 2014. Moreover, due to its interdisciplinary nature and an overall lack of distinction between nanotech and traditional patents, there is a tendency for overlapping IP claims. There is a need to revise local IP laws to accommodate advances in nanotechnology research. The World Health Organization in general and the World Intellectual Property Organization are making the first step to revise the guidelines concerning products developed through nanotechnology. This chapter presents the status of nanotechnology research and potential in the Philippines, the commercially available nanotech products, the R&D plan of the Philippine government concerning nanotechnology.

The Philippine government has identified nano- technology as one its major areas of development, with a significant allocation of R&D funding. The DOST has appointed a Technical Panel for Nanotechnology which is tasked to oversee government-funded R&D funding in this area.
