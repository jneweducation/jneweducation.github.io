Communication: Philae needed to communicate with Rosetta and Earth to send data and receive commands. By being launched from Rosetta, it was able to establish a reliable communication link with Rosetta, which acted as a relay to Earth.

28. who is the rightful owner of Rosetta stone​?200 words


Answer:

The Rosetta Stone is one of the most important and celebrated archaeological discoveries of all time. It is a fragment of a larger stone stele, or pillar, created in 196 BC during the reign of Pharaoh Ptolemy V Epiphanes. The stone is made of granodiorite, a hard, igneous rock and is inscribed in two languages, Ancient Egyptian hieroglyphic, and Ancient Greek. It contains a decree passed by a council of priests that states that Ptolemy V is to be worshiped as a god, and describes his various offerings to Egyptian temples and shrines.

The Rosetta Stone was discovered by a French soldier in 1799 in the Egyptian city of Rosetta and soon became an object of intense archaeological interest. Upon its discovery, it was annexed to the British Museum, where it has remained ever since. In the original preamble to the 1802 decree liberally translating the Stone's inscriptions, Britain declared itself the rightful owner of the Stone.

The legitimacy of Britain's claim to the Stone has long been a point of contention. Before its discovery, Britain had no claim to the Stone, considering the fact that it was buried in the sands of the Nile Delta for nearly two centuries. While Britain has argued that the Rosetta Stone falls under the doctrine of prescriptive rights, which states that ownership of abandoned objects belong to the finder, this argument is disputed as modern international law holds that any valuables discovered in a foreign country belong to the country of origin.

This legal dispute has yet to be fully settled. Whether Britain is the rightful owner of the Rosetta Stone or not may be determined in the future, but it is currently housed in the British Museum in London and accessible to anyone who is interested in viewing it. Despite its controversial ownership issues, the Stone continues to be a symbol of ancient Egyptian culture, and a prime example of the historical importance of multilingual communication.
