1. The pressure on 6.0 L of a gas is 200 kPa. What will be the volume if the pressure is doubled


Answer:

The volume will be 3 L.

Explanation:

The relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas is given by Boyle's Law;

                                   [tex]P_{1} V_{1} = P_{2}V_{2}[/tex]

The given are:

                   [tex]V_{1} = 6 L\\P_{1} = 200 kPa[/tex]   [tex]P_{2} = 2 P_{1}[/tex]

Therefore,

                                   [tex]P_{1} V_{1} = P_{2}V_{2}\\(200kPa)(6L) = (2 P_{1} )(V_{2} )\\(200kPa)(6L) = [(2)(200 kPa)](V_{2} )\\V_{2} = \frac{(200kPa)(6L)}{(2)(200 kPa)}\\V_{2} = 3 L[/tex]

5. There are supplied 400 kj per cycle to an ideal diesel engine operating on 500 g air, suction pressure, and temperature of 100 kpa and 50c. at the end of compression, the pressure rises to 4000 kpa. determine the compression ratio


Answer:

To determine the compression ratio, we need to find the ratio of the volume of the cylinder during the compression stroke to the volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke.

The volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke can be calculated using the ideal gas law:

PV = nRT

Where:

P = pressure (100 kPa)

V = volume of the cylinder during the intake stroke

n = number of moles of air (0.005 moles, since the mass of air is 500 g and the molar mass of air is 28.97 g/mol)

R = ideal gas constant (8.31 J/mol*K)

T = temperature (50 C, or 323 K)

Solving for V, we get:

V = (nRT)/P = (0.0058.31323)/100 = 0.0136 m^3

The volume of the cylinder during the compression stroke can be calculated in a similar manner, using the pressure at the end of the compression stroke (4000 kPa) instead of the suction pressure:

V = (nRT)/P = (0.0058.31323)/4000 = 0.00034 m^3

The compression ratio is then:

V_compression/V_intake = (0.00034 m^3)/(0.0136 m^3) = 2.5

So the compression ratio is 2.5.

8. 3. Air fills a room with a volume a room with a volume of 5600L atmospheric pressure is 740 tort. What will be the pressure if all of the gas is pumped into an 80L tank? Convert pressure to kPa​


We can solve this problem using Boyle's Law, which states that the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume when temperature is constant.

First, we need to find the initial pressure of the air in the room:

P1 = 740 torr

Next, we can use the following formula to solve for the final pressure in the tank:

P2 = (P1 x V1) / V2

where P2 is the final pressure in the tank, V1 is the initial volume of the room (5600 L), and V2 is the volume of the tank (80 L).

P2 = (740 torr x 5600 L) / 80 L

P2 = 51,800 torr

To convert torr to kPa, we can use the conversion factor 1 torr = 0.133 kPa:

P2 = 51,800 torr x 0.133 kPa/torr

P2 = 6,890 kPa

Therefore, the pressure in the tank is 6,890 kPa.

10. How many moles of gas must be present in a gas cylinder of volume 0.01m3 pressurized to 200 KPa at a temperature of 25 degrees ?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: List the given values.

Note that the given temperature in degrees Celsius (°C) must be converted to kelvin (K) before plugging in the formula.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P = 200 \: \cancel{\text{kPa}} \times \frac{\text{1 atm}}{101.325 \: \cancel{\text{kPa}}} = \text{1.9738 atm} \\ & V = 0.01 \: \cancel{\text{m}^3} \times \frac{\text{1000 L}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{m}^3}} = \text{10 L} \\ & T = 25 + 273.15 = \text{298.15 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the number of moles of gas using ideal gas equation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n & = \frac{PV}{\text{RT}} \\ & = \frac{(\text{1.9738 atm})(\text{10 L})}{\left(0.082057 \: \dfrac{\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}}\right)(\text{298.15 K})} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.807 mol}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the number of moles of gas in the gas cylinder is 0.807 mol.

13. the pressure in a car tire is 198 kPa at 27.after a long drive, the pressure is 225kPa what is the temperature of the air in the tire?


Answer:

340.9°k ~ 67.8°C

Explanation:

This is an example of Gay-Lussac's law, which states that the pressure of a given amount of gas held at constant volume is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature. This means that if the volume increases, so does the temperature, and vice versa.

The equation for this gas law is:

P1/T1 = P2/T2

Known

P1 = 198 kPa

T1 = 27°C + 273.15 = 300°K ← Temp. must be in Kelvins

P2 = 225 kPa

T2 = ?

Solution

Rearrange the equation to isolate T2

Input the known values and solve.

T2 = T1P2/P1

Where:

T2 = 300°K × 225kPa/198kPa = 340.91°K

T2 in °C = 340.91°K − 273.15 = 67.8°C

note: answers were rounded off to one decimal point.

15. 1. A cylinder contain a gas which has a pressure of 125kPa at a temperature of 200. K. Findthe temperature of the gas which has a pressure of 100.kPa if the volume remainsconstant​


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{125 kPa}[/tex]

[tex]T_{1} = text{200 K}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{100 kPa}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]T_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\dfrac{P_{1}}{T_{1}} = \dfrac{P_{2}}{T_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = T_{1} \times \dfrac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = \text{200 K} \times \dfrac{\text{100 kPa}}{\text{125 kPa}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{T_{2} = \text{160 K}}[/tex]

Therefore, the final temperature of the gas is 160 K.

17. A motorist equips her automobile tires with a relief– type valve so that the pressure inside the tire never willexceed 240 kPa (gage). She stars a trip with a pressure of 200 kPa(gage) and a temperature of 23o C in the tires. Duringthe long drive the temperature of the air in the tires reaches80o C. Each tire contains 0.11kg of air. Determine (a)the mass of air escaping each tire; (b) the pressure of the tirewhen the temperature returns to 23oC.


We treat the air in the tires as an ideal gas and solve the missing values accordingly.

Ideal gas law:

[tex] PV = nRT [/tex]

Values:

[tex] P_{max} = 240 kPa[/tex]
[tex] P_{i} = 200 kPa[/tex]

these two pressures are gauge pressures, which means the actual pressure is benchmarked to 1 atm, which is 101.325 kPa. For consistency, we add that to our given pressures so that we solve for the actual pressure.

[tex] P_{max} = 240 kPa + 101.325 kPa = 341.325 kPa[/tex]
[tex] P_{i} = 200 kPa + 101.325 kPa = 301.325 kPa [/tex]

We also convert the temperatures to Kelvin, since this is the basic unit for ideal gases:

[tex] T_{i} = 23 \textdegree C = 296 K[/tex]
[tex] T_{max} = 80 \textdegree C = 353 K[/tex]
[tex] m_{i} = 0.11 kg[/tex]
[tex] R_{air} = 0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K}[/tex],

since we're using this constant, it's safe to change the n in the equation to m, as in mass.

First thing we need to do is solve for the volume of the tires initially. We're gonna need this since this is going to be a useful constant throughout the solution. Again, ideal gas law's gonna be perfect for it:

[tex] PV = mRT[/tex], we need volume so we twist it around to get a solution for volume

[tex] V = \frac{mRT}{P} [/tex],

We plug in the values at the start of the trip to solve this:

[tex] V = \frac{m_i R_{air}T_i}{P_i} [/tex]
[tex] V = \frac{(0.11 kg)(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(296 K)}{301.325 kPa}[/tex]
[tex] V= \frac{(0.11 kg)(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(296 K)}{301.325 \frac{kJ}{m^3}}[/tex]
[tex] V = 0.03102 m^3 [/tex]

For a)

We plug in this V to get the mass at the point of the trip where her tires got 240 kPA at 80 °C:

[tex] m = \frac{P_{max} V}{R_{air} T_{max}} [/tex]
[tex] m = \frac{(341.325 kPa)(0.03102 m^3)}{(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(353 K)} [/tex]
[tex] m = \frac{(341.325 \frac{kJ}{m^3})(0.03102 m^3)}{(0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K})(353 K)}[/tex]
[tex] m = m_f = 0.10448 kg [/tex]

We subtract this mass to the initial mass to get the actual mass loss in the tires:

[tex] \Delta m = m_i - m_f = 0.11 kg - 0.10448 kg = 0.005517 kg [/tex]

∴ the mass loss in the tires is 0.005517 kg

For b)

We apply the final mass to solve for the pressure after the relief:

[tex]P = \frac{m_f R_{air} T}{V} [/tex]
[tex]P = \frac{m_f R_{air} T}{V} [/tex]
[tex]P = \frac{(0.10448 kg) (0.28707 \frac{kJ}{kg K}) (296 K)}{0.03102 m^3} [/tex]

∴ P = 286.20083 kPa (actual pressure), 184.876 kPa (gauge pressure)

21. a 5.0 L air sample at 50°C has a pressure of 107 kPa. The temperature is raised to 100°C and the volume expands to 7.0L. What is the new pressure?


Answer:

We can use the combined gas law to solve this problem, which states that:

(P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂

where P₁, V₁, and T₁ are the initial pressure, volume, and temperature, respectively, and P₂, V₂, and T₂ are the final pressure, volume, and temperature, respectively.

Plugging in the given values, we get:

(107 kPa x 5.0 L)/(50°C + 273.15) = (P₂ x 7.0 L)/(100°C + 273.15)

Simplifying and solving for P₂, we get:

P₂ = (107 kPa x 5.0 L x (100°C + 273.15))/((50°C + 273.15) x 7.0 L)

P₂ = 154 kPa (rounded to three significant figures)

Therefore, the new pressure is approximately 154 kPa.

25. a 5.0 L air sample at 50°C has a pressure of 107 kPa. The temperature is raised to 100°C and the volume expands to 7.0L. What is the new pressure?


Answer:

123 kPa

Explanation:

We can use the ideal gas law to solve this problem. The ideal gas law states that PV = nRT, where P is the pressure, V is the volume, n is the number of moles of gas, R is the universal gas constant, and T is the temperature in kelvins.

First, we need to convert the temperatures from degrees Celsius to kelvins. To do this, we add 273.15 to each temperature. So 50°C is equivalent to 323.15 K and 100°C is equivalent to 373.15 K.

Next, we can use the initial conditions to find the number of moles of gas in the sample. Plugging in the initial values for pressure (107 kPa), volume (5.0 L), and temperature (323.15 K) into the ideal gas law equation and solving for n gives us:

The universal gas constant, denoted by the symbol R, is a physical constant that appears in the equation of state of an ideal gas. It relates the pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas to the number of particles (or moles) present.

The value of the universal gas constant is approximately 8.314, depending on the units used for pressure, volume, and temperature in a particular calculation.

n = PV / RT = (107 kPa)(5.0 L) / (8.314 L kPa/K mol)(323.15 K) ≈ 2.03 mol

Now that we know the number of moles of gas in the sample, we can use the final conditions for volume (7.0 L) and temperature (373.15 K) to find the final pressure. Plugging these values into the ideal gas law equation and solving for P gives us:

P = nRT / V = (2.03 mol)(8.314 L kPa/K mol)(373.15 K) / (7.0 L) ≈ 123 kPa

So the new pressure of the air sample after its temperature is raised to 100°C and its volume expands to 7.0 L is approximately 123 kPa.

29. Find the partial pressure in kpa of oxygen and nitrogen in atmospheric air of 115.5 kpa which contains by weight 27.7% oxygen and 72.3% nitrogen.


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Assume that the mass of air is 100 g.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{O}_2} = \text{27.7 g} \\ & mass_{\text{N}_2} = \text{72.3 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of each gas.

The molar masses of O₂ and N₂ are 31.998 g and 28.014 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & n_{\text{O}_2} = \text{27.7 g} \times \frac{\text{1 mol}}{\text{31.998 g}} = \text{0.86568 mol} \\ & n_{\text{N}_2} = \text{72.3 g} \times \frac{\text{1 mol}}{\text{28.014 g}} = \text{2.5809 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mole fraction of each gas.

• For O₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} X_{\text{O}_2} & = \frac{n_{\text{O}_2}}{n_{\text{O}_2} + n_{\text{N}_2}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.86568 mol}}{\text{0.86568 mol + 2.5809 mol}} \\ & = 0.25117 \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For N₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} X_{\text{N}_2} & = \frac{n_{\text{N}_2}}{n_{\text{O}_2} + n_{\text{N}_2}} \\ & = \frac{\text{2.5809 mol}}{\text{0.86568 mol + 2.5809 mol}} \\ & = 0.74883 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the partial pressures of each gas.

• For O₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_{\text{O}_2} & = X_{\text{O}_2}P_{\text{T}} \\ & = \text{(0.25117)(115.5 kPa)} \\ & = \boxed{\text{29.01 kPa}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For N₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_{\text{N}_2} & = X_{\text{N}_2}P_{\text{T}} \\ & = \text{(0.74883)(115.5 kPa)} \\ & = \boxed{\text{86.49 kPa}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the partial pressures of oxygen and nitrogen gas are 29.01 kPa and 86.49 kPa, respectively.
