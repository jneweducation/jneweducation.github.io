17. Why is the moon considering a satellite of Earth?


THE MOON

The Moon or Luna, a satellite of planet Earth is roughly 238, 900 miles away from Earth. It is 2,160 miles in diameter and has almost no atmosphere. The dusty surface is covered with craters. The Moon takes the same time to rotate on its axis as it does to orbit Earth (27 days, 7 hours, 43 minutes). This is why one side of the Moon is always facing Earth. The Moon has no lights of its own but reflects light from the Sun.

The Moon is considered a satellite because it fits to the general description of a satellite which means an object that moves or revolves around a larger object. Earth is also a satellite because it moves around the sun. The moon is a satellite because it moves around Earth. Earth and the moon are called "natural" satellites.

Artificial Satellites however, are machines launched into space and orbit Earth or another body in space.

Formation of the Moon

•There are different theories about Luna's formation. According to one theory, a protoplanet or a young planet that had not yet fully formed may have collided with Earth more than 4 billion years ago. The collision produced debris that eventually came together, as a result of gravitaty the Moon eventually formed.

Answer:

No, we cannot say that Earth is the Sun's satellite. Satellites are objects that orbit around a larger object, and the Moon orbits around Earth. However, Earth does not orbit around the Sun as a satellite. Instead, Earth orbits around the Sun as a planet, along with other planets in our solar system. So, we can say that Earth is a planet that orbits around the Sun, but we cannot say that it is a satellite of the Sun.
