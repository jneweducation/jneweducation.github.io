12. What are some common EKG artifacts that can occur during recording, and how can they be recognized and corrected?


Answer:

There are several common EKG artifacts that can occur during recording. Here are some of them and how they can be recognized and corrected:

Muscle artifact: This is caused by muscle movement or tension, and it can result in a baseline shift or interference with the EKG signal. It can be recognized by its irregular and sporadic nature. To correct it, the patient should be asked to relax and breathe normally, and electrodes should be repositioned if necessary.

60 Hz interference: This is caused by electrical interference from other devices or power lines and appears as a regular pattern of fine, uniform spikes on the EKG tracing. To correct it, the EKG machine should be grounded, and the patient should be shielded from any nearby electrical equipment.

Loose or disconnected electrodes: This can cause a loss of signal or baseline shifts. To correct it, the electrode should be repositioned or replaced, and the skin should be cleaned and dried before applying the electrode.

Patient movement: This can cause irregularities in the EKG signal or baseline shifts. To correct it, the patient should be asked to remain still and avoid talking or moving during the recording.

Electrode interference: This can be caused by the presence of metal objects or clothing near the electrodes. To correct it, the electrodes should be placed in a different location or covered with a shield to reduce interference.

Respiratory artifact: This is caused by the patient's breathing and appears as a regular pattern of spikes on the EKG tracing. To correct it, the patient should be asked to breathe deeply and slowly, and the electrode should be repositioned if necessary.
