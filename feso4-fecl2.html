FeS + 2 HCl → H2S + FeCl2

Step 3: Calculate moles FeCl2

Moles FeCl2 = 326 grams / 126.75 grams

Moles FeCl2 = 2.57 moles

For 1 mol H2S and 1 mol FeCl2 produced, we need 1 mol FeS and 2 moles HCI

Mass FeS = 2.57 moles* 87.92 g/mol

26. A piece of metallic iron (10 moles) was dissolved in concentrated HCI. The reaction formed H2 and FeCl2. Write and balance the equation for this reaction and determine the amount of formed FeCl2 and the amount of used HCI. How much of the excess reactant remained after the completion of reaction?​


Answer:

The balanced chemical equation for the reaction between metallic iron (Fe) and hydrochloric acid (HCl) to form hydrogen gas (H2) and ferrous chloride (FeCl2) is:

Fe + 2 HCl → H2 + FeCl2

Using the given information that 10 moles of Fe was dissolved in concentrated HCl, we can use stoichiometry to calculate the amount of formed FeCl2 and the amount of used HCl, as well as the amount of excess HCl that remained after the reaction.

a. Amount of formed FeCl2:

From the balanced equation, we can see that 1 mole of Fe reacts with 2 moles of HCl to produce 1 mole of FeCl2.

Therefore, if 10 moles of Fe were used, then the theoretical yield of FeCl2 would be: 10 moles Fe x (1 mole FeCl2 / 1 mole Fe) = 10 moles FeCl2

b. Amount of used HCl:

From the balanced equation, we can see that 1 mole of Fe reacts with 2 moles of HCl.

Therefore, the number of moles of HCl used in the reaction is: 10 moles Fe x (2 moles HCl / 1 mole Fe) = 20 moles HCl

c. Amount of excess HCl:

To determine the amount of excess HCl, we need to know the amount of HCl that was in excess compared to the amount required to react with all the Fe.

From the balanced equation, we know that 1 mole of Fe reacts with 2 moles of HCl, so 10 moles of Fe would require 20 moles of HCl.

However, we used 20 moles of HCl, so there was no excess HCl remaining after the reaction.

Therefore, the reaction between 10 moles of Fe and 20 moles of HCl produced 10 moles of FeCl2 and consumed all 20 moles of HCl without any excess remaining.

Intermolecular forces (IMF) (or secondary forces) are the forces which mediate interaction between molecules, including forces of attraction or repulsion which act between atoms and other types of neighboring particles, e.g. atoms or ions. Intermolecular forces are weak relative to intramolecular forces – the forces which hold a molecule together.
