19. Adjusting EntriesAdditional information: 1. Doubtful account is 3% of net sales. 2. The estimated life of store furniture is 10 years without scrap value. 3. Prepaid rent as of December 31 is P2,400. 4. Unused store supplies, P300. 5. Accrued salaries, P3,200 6. Ending merchandise inventory, P58,000.


ADJUSTING ENTRIES - Make modifications to previously recorded diary entries.

   Adjusting entries are modifications to previously recorded journal entries. They specifically ensure that the values you have recorded correspond to the relevant accounting periods. Journal entries show how money flows through your business—how it enters, exits, and transfers between accounts.

The following adjusting entries may be made:

1. Doubtful account:

Debit - Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (asset)Credit - Accounts Receivable (asset)

Explanation: An allowance for doubtful accounts is an estimate of the amount of accounts receivable that may not be collected. If 3% of net sales is expected to be uncollectible, this amount can be recorded as an allowance for doubtful accounts by debiting the allowance account and crediting accounts receivable.

2. Depreciation of store furniture

Debit - Depreciation Expense (expense)Credit - Accumulated Depreciation (contra-asset)

Explanation: Depreciation is an expense that represents the wear and tear or obsolescence of an asset over its useful life. If the estimated life of store furniture is 10 years without scrap value, an annual depreciation expense can be recorded by debiting depreciation expense and crediting accumulated depreciation.

3. Prepaid rent:

Debit - Rent Expense (expense)Credit - Prepaid Rent (asset)

Explanation: If there is prepaid rent as of December 31 in the amount of P2,400, this amount can be recognized as an expense by debiting rent expense and crediting prepaid rent.

4. Unused store supplies:

Debit - Store Supplies (asset)Credit - Store Supplies Expense (expense)

Explanation: If there are unused store supplies in the amount of P300, this amount can be recorded as a reduction in store supplies expense by debiting store supplies and crediting store supplies expense.

5. Accrued salaries:

Debit - Salaries Expense (expense)Credit - Salaries Payable (liability)

Explanation: If there are accrued salaries in the amount of P3,200, this amount can be recognized as an expense by debiting salaries expense and crediting salaries payable.

6. Ending merchandise inventory:

Debit - Cost of Goods Sold (expense)Credit - Merchandise Inventory (asset)

Explanation: The ending merchandise inventory can be recorded by debiting cost of goods sold and crediting merchandise inventory. The amount of the debit and credit will depend on the value of the ending inventory, which is P58,000 in this case.

25. At the beginning of the year, SnapIt had $10,000 of inventory. During the year, SnapIt purchased $35,000 of merchandise and sold $30,000 of merchandise. A physical count of inventory at year-end shows $14,000 of inventory exists. Prepare the entry to record inventory shrinkage.


Answer and Explanation:

The inventory shrinkage can be calculated as follows:

Beginning inventory + Purchases - Cost of goods sold - Ending inventory = Inventory shrinkage

$10,000 + $35,000 - $30,000 - $14,000 = $1,000

This means that $1,000 of inventory has been lost or damaged during the year.

To record the inventory shrinkage in the books of SnapIt, the following journal entry should be made:

Inventory Shrinkage Expense $1,000

Inventory $1,000

This entry will increase the inventory shrinkage expense account by $1,000 and decrease the inventory account by the same amount, reflecting the loss of inventory.

30. Why does Merchandise Inventory account appear in a Merchandising business Balance Sheet but not in a Service business? Explain your answer.​


Answer:

Merchandise Inventory

Merchandise inventory is the cost of goods on hand and available for sale at any given time. Merchandise inventory (also called Inventory) is a current asset with a normal debit balance meaning a debit will increase and a credit will decrease.

To determine the cost of goods sold in any accounting period, management needs inventory information. Management must know:

its cost of goods on hand at the start of the period (beginning inventory)

the net cost of purchases during the period

and the cost of goods on hand at the close of the period (ending inventory).

Since the ending inventory of the one period is the beginning inventory for the next period, management already knows the cost of the beginning inventory. Companies record purchases, purchase discounts, purchase returns and allowances, and transportation-in throughout the period. Therefore, management needs to determine only the cost of the ending inventory at the end of the period in order to calculate cost of goods sold.

Cost of goods sold is the inventory cost to the seller of the goods sold to customers. Cost of Goods Sold is an EXPENSE item with a normal debit balance (debit to increase and credit to decrease). Even though we do not see the word Expense this in fact is an expense item found on the Income Statement as a reduction to Revenue.

Accountants must have accurate merchandise inventory figures to calculate cost of goods sold. Accountants use two basic methods for determining the amount of merchandise inventory—perpetual inventory procedure and periodic inventory procedure.
