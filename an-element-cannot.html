21. why are elements cannot be broken down into simpler form?​


Answer:

Elements

Any substance that contains only one kind of an atom is known as an element. Because atoms cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction, elements such as phosphorus (P4) or sulfur (Sg) cannot be broken down into simpler substances by these reactions.

Example: Water decomposes into a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen when an electric current is passed through the liquid. Hydrogen and oxygen, on the other hand, cannot be decomposed into simpler substances. They are therefore the elementary, or simplest, chemical substances - elements.

Each element is represented by a unique symbol. The notation for each element can be found on the periodic table of elements.

The elements can be divided into three categories that have characteristic properties: metals, nonmetals, and semimetals. Most elements are metals, which are found on the left and toward the bottom of the periodic table. A handful of nonmetals are clustered in the upper right corner of the periodic table. The semimetals can be found along the dividing line between the metals and the nonmetals.

25. which element of the scientific process cannot be changed


Answer:

principle of empirical verification

Explanation:

The element of the scientific process that cannot be changed is the principle of empirical verification, which means that scientific knowledge must be based on empirical evidence that can be observed, measured, and replicated. This principle is essential to the scientific process because it ensures that scientific claims are based on objective evidence that can be tested and verified by others.

While other elements of the scientific process such as formulating hypotheses, designing experiments, and interpreting data can be revised and improved based on new information, the requirement for empirical verification remains a fundamental principle of science that cannot be compromised.


The element of the scientific process that cannot be changed is the fundamental principle of empirical evidence. This principle states that scientific knowledge should be based on observable, measurable, and testable evidence. In other words, scientific claims must be supported by empirical evidence that can be independently verified and replicated by other scientists.

This principle is essential to the scientific process because it ensures that scientific knowledge is objective, reliable, and accurate. Without empirical evidence, scientific claims would be based on subjective opinions, beliefs, or biases, which would undermine the credibility of science as a discipline.

While other elements of the scientific process, such as hypotheses, theories, and experimental methods, can be modified or refined based on new evidence or insights, the principle of empirical evidence cannot be changed or compromised. It is the foundation of scientific inquiry and the hallmark of scientific knowledge.
