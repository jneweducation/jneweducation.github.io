23. 5. Calcium carbonate decomposes at high temperature to form carbon dioxide and calcium oxide. Determine the heat of reaction. CaCO3(s)  CO2(g) + CaO(s)


The heat of reaction for the decomposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) to form carbon dioxide (CO2) and calcium oxide (CaO) can be determined by using the heats of formation for the reactants and products.

The balanced chemical equation for the reaction is:

CaCO3(s) → CO2(g) + CaO(s)

The heats of formation for CaCO3, CO2, and CaO are:

ΔHf°(CaCO3) = -1206.9 kJ/mol

ΔHf°(CO2) = -393.5 kJ/mol

ΔHf°(CaO) = -635.1 kJ/mol

The heat of reaction (ΔHrxn) can be calculated using the following formula:

ΔHrxn = ΣΔHf°(products) - ΣΔHf°(reactants)

Substituting the values into the equation, we get:

ΔHrxn = [ΔHf°(CO2) + ΔHf°(CaO)] - ΔHf°(CaCO3)

ΔHrxn = [(-393.5 kJ/mol) + (-635.1 kJ/mol)] - (-1206.9 kJ/mol)

ΔHrxn = (-1028.6 kJ/mol) + 1206.9 kJ/mol

ΔHrxn = 178.3 kJ/mol

Therefore, the heat of reaction for the decomposition of calcium carbonate to form carbon dioxide and calcium oxide is 178.3 kJ/mol.

30. Explain why much higher mobility of nitrate than calcium in soils?


Nitrate has a higher mobility than calcium because it is actually used by the plants as food. For this reason, it actually travels up to the plant which it nourishes as it grows. Through the process of nitrification, ammonium is converted into nitrate which is then taken by the plant .

Calcium, on the other hand, is almost insoluble, and if it ever manages to get inside the plant it tends to stay in one place, mostly in the cell walls. This makes it less mobile compared to nitrate.
