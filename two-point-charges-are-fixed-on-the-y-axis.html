7. A +2.00 nC point charge is at the origin, and a second -5.00nCpoint charge is on the x-axis at x = 0.800 m. a) Fine the electricfield (magnitude and direction) at each of the following points onthe x-axis: i) x = 0.200 m; ii) x = 1.20 m; iii) x = -0.200 m. b)Find the net electric force that the two charges would exert on anelectron placed at each point in part (a).


The electric field at any point can be given by taking the superposition (or simply the sum) of all the individual charges affecting that point. The equation for taking the electric field is given by

[tex]E = k \frac{q}{d^2}[/tex]

we let the charge at x= 0 be [tex]q_1[/tex] with a value 2.00 nC [tex]10^{-9} C[/tex]
and the charge at x = 0.800 be [tex]q_2[/tex] with value -5 nC [tex]10^{-9} C[/tex].


a) Find the electric field at:

i) x = 0.200 m

[tex]E_{q_1} = k \frac{q_1}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{2.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(0.200 m - 0 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1}[/tex] = 449.5 N/C

[tex]E_{q_2} = k \frac{q_2}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{-5.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(0.800 m - 0.200 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2}[/tex] = -124.86 N/C

[tex]E_{total} = E_{q_1} + E_{q_2} = 449.5 N/C + (-124.86 N/C)[/tex]

[tex]E_{total}[/tex] = 324.64 N/C, going to the +x direction.


ii) x = 1.200 m

[tex]E_{q_1} = k \frac{q_1}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{2.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(1.200 m - 0 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1}[/tex] = 12.49 N/C

[tex]E_{q_2} = k \frac{q_2}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{-5.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(1.200 m - 0.800 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2}[/tex] = -280.94 N/C

[tex]E_{total} = E_{q_1} + E_{q_2} = 12.49 N/C + (-280.94 N/C)[/tex]

[tex]E_{total}[/tex] = 268.45 N/C, going to the -x direction.

iii) x = -0.200 m

[tex]E_{q_1} = k \frac{q_1}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{2.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(-0.200 m - 0 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_1}[/tex] = -449.5 N/C

[tex]E_{q_2} = k \frac{q_2}{d^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2} = (8.99 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2)\frac{-5.00 \times 10^{-9} C}{(-0.200 m - 0.200 m)^2}[/tex]

[tex]E_{q_2}[/tex] = 280.94 N/C

[tex]E_{total} = E_{q_1} + E_{q_2} = -449.5 N/C + 280.94 N/C[/tex]

[tex]E_{total}[/tex] = 168.6 N/C, going to the -x direction.

b) Find the electric force caused by the two charges to an electron.

This can simply be solved by taking the electric fields we obtained and multiplying it to the charge of the electron:

[tex]F = qE[/tex]

i)[tex]F = (1.602 \times 10^{-19} C)(324.64 N/C) = 5.201 \times 10^{-17} N[/tex]
ii)[tex]F = (1.602 \times 10^{-19} C)(268.45 N/C) = 4.301 \times 10^{-17} N[/tex]
ii)[tex]F = (1.602 \times 10^{-19} C)(168.6 N/C) = 4.701 \times 10^{-17} N[/tex]

12. Did you mean: a charge q1=7uC is at the origin and a charge q2= -5 uC is on the axis .300m from the origin. find the electric field strength at a point P, which is on the y-axis .400m from the origin​


Answer:

Yes, I understand your question. To find the electric field strength at point P, which is on the y-axis 0.400 m from the origin, we can use Coulomb's Law and the principle of superposition.

First, let's find the electric field E1 due to q1 at point P. The distance between q1 and P is 0.4 m, so the magnitude of the electric field due to q1 is:

E1 = k * q1 / r1^2

where k is Coulomb's constant (k = 9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2), q1 is the charge of q1 (q1 = 7 uC), and r1 is the distance between q1 and P (r1 = 0.4 m). Substituting these values, we get:

E1 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (7 uC) / (0.4 m)^2

E1 = 3.9 x 10^6 N/C

Next, let's find the electric field E2 due to q2 at point P. We can use the principle of superposition to find the net electric field at P, which is the vector sum of E1 and E2. The distance between q2 and P is 0.300 m, and the angle between the y-axis and the line connecting q2 and P is 90 degrees. Therefore, the magnitude of the electric field due to q2 is:

E2 = k * |q2| / r2^2

where |q2| is the absolute value of q2 (|q2| = 5 uC), and r2 is the distance between q2 and P (r2 = 0.3 m). Substituting these values, we get:

E2 = (9.0 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (5 uC) / (0.3 m)^2

E2 = 5.0 x 10^6 N/C

Since q2 is negative, its electric field is directed towards it. So, the electric field vector due to q2 points in the negative y direction.

Finally, the net electric field E at P is given by the vector sum of E1 and E2:

E = E1 + E2

Since E1 and E2 are perpendicular to each other, we can use the Pythagorean theorem to find the magnitude of E:

|E| = sqrt(E1^2 + E2^2)

Substituting the values we found earlier, we get:

|E| = sqrt[(3.9 x 10^6 N/C)^2 + (5.0 x 10^6 N/C)^2]

|E| = 6.3 x 10^6 N/C

The direction of the net electric field is along the positive y-axis, since the electric field due to q1 is also directed along the positive y-axis. Therefore, the electric field strength at point P is 6.3 x 10^6 N/C, directed along the positive y-axis.
