15. Predict the shape or molecular geometry of a substance using the following steps: Step 1: Determine the central atom of a molecule. The central atom is the least electronegative element. (e.g., Oxygen difluoride, OF ₂) Step 2: Draw the appropriate Lewis dot structure for the molecule. Step 3: Count the number of bonding pairs of electrons and non- bonding (or lone pairs) around the central atom. Step 4: Determine the electron pair orientation using the total number of electron pairs. Step 5: Name the shape of based on the location of the atoms.​


Answer:

Explanation:

Yes, that is a correct set of steps to predict the shape or molecular geometry of a substance. Here is a more detailed explanation of each step:

Step 1: Determine the central atom of a molecule. The central atom is usually the least electronegative element, except in cases where hydrogen is the central atom. For example, in oxygen difluoride (OF₂), oxygen is the central atom because it is less electronegative than fluorine.

Step 2: Draw the appropriate Lewis dot structure for the molecule. This involves representing the valence electrons of each atom as dots around the symbol, and connecting the atoms with single or double bonds as necessary to fulfill the octet rule.

Step 3: Count the number of bonding pairs of electrons and non-bonding (or lone pairs) around the central atom. This will give you the electron pair geometry of the molecule.

Step 4: Determine the electron pair orientation using the total number of electron pairs. This will give you the molecular geometry of the molecule. For example, if the electron pair geometry is tetrahedral and there are four bonding pairs and no lone pairs, then the molecular geometry is also tetrahedral.

Step 5: Name the shape based on the location of the atoms. For example, if the molecular geometry is linear (two atoms), bent (three atoms), or tetrahedral (four atoms), you would name the shape accordingly.
