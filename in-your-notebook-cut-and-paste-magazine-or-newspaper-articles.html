Why teach exposition?

Let's think about the type of writing that most of us encounter in our daily lives. When you pick up and read a non-fiction book, magazines, or newspaper article the author uses expository writing to inform you, the reader, about the topic. At school, students are required to submit school exams and research papers as a means for their teachers to grade their progress. Finally, at work, people are required to produce business reports and memorandums to inform their superiors and co-workers about the occurrences that take place at other levels of the company. In addition, oral exposition is primarily observed in oral academic presentations, business talks, and speeches that are delivered to a group of people. As each of these different cases illustrate, expository writing and speech surround us in our everyday lives. The primary intent of the Expository Writing Program contained at this web site will be to help move students closer to mastering the hows, whens, and wheres to select different oral and written expository styles for a variety of real world contexts.

Students will greatly benefit from understanding the varying types of oral and writing styles they can use for academic and workplace activities. 26. ASSIGNMENT Look for at least three (3) samples of expository texts in books, newspapers, or magazines. Cut them out and paste them in your notebook. Indicate the patterns of idea development used by the writers.​


Answer:

Here are three examples of expository texts:

Example 1:

Text: "The process of photosynthesis is the way that plants convert sunlight into energy. During photosynthesis, plants use energy from the sun to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. The glucose is used by the plant as a source of energy, while the oxygen is released into the atmosphere as a byproduct. Photosynthesis is a vital process for all plants and is also important for the overall balance of the Earth's ecosystem. Without photosynthesis, there would be no oxygen on Earth and life as we know it would not be possible."

Pattern of idea development: The writer starts by explaining the general concept of photosynthesis, and then goes into more detail about the specific steps involved in the process. They also discuss the importance of photosynthesis for plants and the overall ecosystem.

Example 2:

Text: "The human respiratory system is responsible for bringing oxygen into the body and removing carbon dioxide. The system is made up of the nose, mouth, trachea, bronchi, and lungs. When you breathe in, air enters your body through your nose or mouth and travels down the trachea, which is a tube that runs down the front of your neck. The trachea splits into two smaller tubes called bronchi, which lead to the lungs. Once the air reaches the lungs, it passes through small air sacs called alveoli, where the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide takes place. Oxygen is absorbed into the bloodstream and transported to cells throughout the body, while carbon dioxide is exhaled out of the body."

Pattern of idea development: The writer begins by introducing the main function of the respiratory system and then describes the various components that make up the system. They then explain the process of how air moves through the respiratory system and how oxygen is exchanged with carbon dioxide.

Example 3:

Text: "The water cycle, also known as the hydrologic cycle, is the process by which water moves through the Earth's surface, atmosphere, and back again. Water evaporates from the surface of the Earth and rises into the atmosphere as water vapor. As the vapor cools, it condenses into clouds and eventually falls back to the Earth's surface as precipitation, such as rain or snow. This precipitation can then be absorbed into the ground, where it becomes part of the groundwater system, or it can flow over the surface of the land and eventually make its way back into oceans, lakes, and other bodies of water. The water cycle is an important process that helps to regulate the temperature and moisture levels of the Earth's surface."

Pattern of idea development: The writer first explains the general concept of the water cycle and then goes into detail about the specific steps involved in the process. They also discuss the importance of the water cycle in regulating the temperature and moisture levels of the Earth's surface.
