Given:

d₁ = 23 cm = 0.23 m

v₂ = 1.66 m/s

Solution:

Using the equation of continuity:

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂

(π/4)(0.23)²(0.73) = (π/4)(d₂²)(1.66)

d₂ = 0.15 m

d₂ = 15 cm <== answer

Q = A₁v₁ = (π/4)(0.23)²(0.73)

Q = 0.03 m³/s

Q = 30,000 cm³/s <== answer

8. An insulated steam pipe located where the ambient temperature is 32°C, has an inside diameter of 50 mm with 10 mm thick wall. The outside diameter of the corrugated asbestos insulation is 125 mm and the surface coefficient of still air, h0 = 12 W/m2 -K. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Answer:

Using this formula, we can calculate the thickness:

Heat Loss (W/m) = (h0 * (T1 - T2)) / (Rtotal)

Where:

h0 = 12W/m2 -K

T1 = 32 (Ambient Temperature in Celsius)

T2 = 0 (Steam Temperature)

Rtotal = (1/h1) + L/k + (1/h2)

h1 = Thermal conductivity through the pipe wall

L = Length of the pipe in m

k = Thermal conductivity through the insulation

h2 = Heat transfer coefficient of the outside surface

The heat loss per unit can be converted to Rtotal.

Rtotal = (100W/m) / (12W/m2-K)

Rtotal = 8.33 m2-K/W

Now, we can solve for the thermal conductivity through the pipe wall (h1):

h1 = 1/Rtotal – L/k – (1/h2)

h1 = 1 / 8.33 - (0.5m / 0.04W/m-K) - (1 / 12W/m2-K))

h1 = 0.086W/m-K

The thickness of the pipe can now be calculated using the thermal conductivity and the inside and outside diameters of the pipe:

Thickness (δ) = (h1 * (Do - Di)) / k

Where:

Do = Outside diameter of the pipe

Di = Inside diameter of the pipe

Thickness (δ) = (0.086W/m-K * (125 - 50)) / 0.04W/m-K

Thickness (δ) = 8.5mm

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe to achieve a heat loss of 100w/m is 8.5mm.

Answer:

To determine the required thickness of the pipe insulation, we can use the heat transfer equation:

Q = 2πLk (Ti - To) / ln(Do / Di)

where:

Q = heat loss per unit length of pipe (W/m)

L = length of pipe (m)

k = thermal conductivity of insulation (W/m-K)

Ti = inside temperature of pipe (°C)

To = outside temperature of insulation (°C)

Do = outside diameter of insulation (m)

Di = inside diameter of pipe (m)

We are given:

Ti = 100°C (since it is a steam pipe)

To = 32°C

Di = 0.05 m

Do = 0.125 m

h0 = 12 W/m2-K

Q = 100 W/m

We need to find k and L.

First, we can calculate the inside surface temperature of the insulation:

Ti' = Ti - Q / (2πLh0Di)

Ti' = 100 - 100 / (2πL * 12 * 0.05) = 93.3°C

Next, we can use the equation for heat flow through the insulation:

Q = 2πLk (Ti' - To) / ln(Do / Di)

Rearranging the equation, we get:

k = Q ln(Do / Di) / (2πL (Ti' - To))

Substituting the given values, we get:

k = 100 ln(0.125 / 0.05) / (2πL (93.3 - 32)) = 0.050 W/m-K

Now we can use the equation for thermal resistance:

R = ln(Do / Di) / (2πLk)

Rearranging the equation, we get:

L = ln(Do / Di) / (2πkR)

Substituting the given values, we get:

L = ln(0.125 / 0.05) / (2π * 0.050 * 100) = 0.067 m

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe insulation is:

d = Do / 2 - Di / 2 - t

where t is the thickness of the insulation. Substituting the given values, we get:

t = (Do - Di) / 2 - d = (0.125 - 0.05) / 2 - 0.067 = 0.004 m or 4 mm

So the required thickness of the insulation is 4 mm.


9. For Fluid pipe Formula An insulated steam pipe located where the ambient temperature is 32°C, has an inside diameter of 50 mm with 10 mm thick wall. The outside diameter of the corrugated asbestos insulation is 125 mm and the surface coefficient of still air, h0 = 12 W/m2 -K. Inside the pipe is steam having a temperature of 150 °C with film coefficient h=6000W/m2k thermal conductivity of pipe and asbestos insulation are 45 and 0.12w/mk respectively. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Step-by-step explanation:

The overall heat transfer coefficient, U can be calculated as:

1/U = 1/hi + r1/k1 + r2/k2 + r3/k3 + 1/ho

where,

hi = film coefficient on the inside of the pipe = h = 6000 W/m2K

ho = film coefficient on the outside of the insulation = h0 = 12 W/m2K

r1 = radius of the inner surface of the insulation = (50/2 + 10) / 1000 = 0.03 m

r2 = radius of the outer surface of the insulation = (50/2 + 10 + 0.125) / 1000 = 0.055 m

r3 = radius of the outer surface of the insulation plus thickness of pipe, which is the unknown we need to find

k1 = thermal conductivity of the insulation = 0.12 W/mK

k2 = thermal conductivity of the asbestos insulation = 0.12 W/mK

k3 = thermal conductivity of the pipe = 45 W/mK

Now, substituting the given values in the equation, we get:

1/U = 1/6000 + 0.03/0.12 + 0.055/0.12 + r3/45 + 1/12

Simplifying this equation, we get:

U = 6.013 W/m2K

The heat loss per unit length of the pipe, q, can be calculated as:

q = U x (T1 - T2)

where,

T1 = temperature inside the pipe = 150°C = 423 K

T2 = ambient temperature outside the insulation = 32°C = 305 K

Substituting the values, we get:

q = 6.013 x (423 - 305) = 708.37 W/m

Now, we can use the given heat loss per unit length of the pipe, q, to find the required thickness of the pipe, x:

q = k3 x 2πL x (T1 - T2) / ln(r3 / r2)

where,

L = length of the pipe = 1 m (assuming unit length)

ln = natural logarithm

Substituting the values, we get:

100 = 45 x 2π x 1 x (423 - 305) / ln(r3 / 0.055)

Simplifying this equation, we get:

ln(r3 / 0.055) = 91.72

r3 / 0.055 = e^91.72

r3 = 0.055 x e^91.72

r3 = 7.038 m

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe is:

x = r3 - 0.055 - 0.05

x = 6.933 m

ANSWER:Hence, the required thickness of the pipe is approximately 6.9 mm.

Next, we can calculate the value of Q:

Q = 100 W/m

We also have the values of k for the insulation and the thermal conductivity of the pipe:

k_insulation = 0.12 W/m-K

k_pipe = 45 W/m-K

We can now substitute the values into the formula and solve for L:

100 = 2πL x 0.12 [(1/0.062505) - (1/0.025)]/ln(0.062505/0.025)

28. For Fluid pipe Formula An insulated steam pipe located where the ambient temperature is 32°C, has an inside diameter of 50 mm with 10 mm thick wall. The outside diameter of the corrugated asbestos insulation is 125 mm and the surface coefficient of still air, h0 = 12 W/m2 -K. Inside the pipe is steam having a temperature of 150 °C with film coefficient h=6000W/m2k thermal conductivity of pipe and asbestos insulation are 45 and 0.12w/mk respectively. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Answer:

We can use the formula for heat transfer through a cylindrical wall:

Q/t = 2πkL(T1-T2) / [ln(r2/r1) / h1 + (r2-r1) / k + ln(r3/r2) / h2]

where Q/t is the heat loss per unit length, k is the thermal conductivity, L is the length of the pipe, T1 and T2 are the temperatures of the inside and outside surfaces of the wall, r1 and r3 are the inner and outer radii of the pipe, and r2 is the outer radius of the insulation.

We can rearrange this formula to solve for the required thickness of the pipe:

r1 = (r2^2 - L / (2πkL(T1-T2)) * [ln(r2/r3) / h2 + (r2-r3) / k + ln(r2/r1) / h1]

Plugging in the given values:

r1 = (0.0625^2 - 1 / (2π * 45 * 0.1 * (150-32))) * [ln(0.125/0.145) / 12 + (0.125-0.145) / 0.12 + ln(0.125/0.05) / 6000]

r1 = 0.00598 m

Therefore, the required thickness of the pipe is:

t = r2 - r1 - d/2 = 0.125 - 0.00598 - 0.05 / 2 = 0.034 m

So the required thickness of the pipe is approximately 34 mm.

Inside the pipe is steam having a temperature of 150 °C with film coefficient h=6000W/m2k thermal conductivity of pipe and asbestos insulation are 45 and 0.12w/mk respectively. So If the heat loss per unit becomes 100w/m determine the required thickness of the pipe?


Answer:

To determine the required thickness of the pipe, we need to use the heat transfer equation:

Q = (2πL/k) × (T1 - T2) / [1/hiA1 + ln(r2/r1)/k + x/ka + ln(ro/r2)/k + 1/hoA2]

where:

Q = heat loss per unit length of the pipe (given as 100 W/m)

L = length of the pipe (we can assume it to be 1 m for simplicity)

k = thermal conductivity of the pipe material (given as 45 W/mK)

T1 = temperature of the steam inside the pipe (given as 150°C)

T2 = ambient temperature outside the pipe (given as 32°C)

hi = inside film coefficient (given as 6000 W/m2K)

ho = outside film coefficient (given as 12 W/m2K)

A1 = inside surface area of the pipe (calculated as πdL, where d is the inside diameter of the pipe and L is the length)

A2 = outside surface area of the insulation (calculated as πD(L+2x), where D is the outside diameter of the insulation and x is the thickness of the insulation)

r1 = inside radius of the pipe (calculated as d/2)

r2 = outside radius of the pipe (calculated as (d+2x)/2)

ro = outside radius of the insulation (calculated as D/2)

ka = thermal conductivity of the insulation material (given as 0.12 W/mK)

Substituting the given values and solving for x, we get:

100 = (2π/k) × (150 - 32) / [1/(6000π(0.05)(1)) + ln(0.125/0.05)/45 + x/(0.12) + ln(0.125/0.125)/45 + 1/(12π(0.125)(1+2x))]

x = 0.084 m or 84 mm (approximately)

Therefore, the required thickness of the insulation is approximately 84 mm.
