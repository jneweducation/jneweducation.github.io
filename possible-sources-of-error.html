HOW TO RATIONALIZE ERRANT DATA AND HOW THEY HAVE AFFECTED THE OUTCOME

Errant data refers to data that is either incorrect, incomplete, or simply doesn't fit with the other data in a dataset. There are many possible sources of error that can lead to errant data, including measurement errors, human error, and problems with the data collection process.

One common source of error is measurement error, which occurs when the data is collected using instruments or methods that are not precise or accurate. For example, if a scale is used to measure the weight of an object, but the scale is not properly calibrated, the resulting data may be inaccurate.

Another common source of error is human error, which can occur at any stage of the data collection process. For example, a researcher may make a mistake while recording data, or a data entry clerk may enter the wrong information into a database.

Problems with the data collection process can also lead to errant data. For example, if a survey is not designed properly, it may not accurately reflect the population it is intended to study. Additionally, if the sample size is too small, the resulting data may not be representative of the population as a whole.

Overall, there are many potential sources of error that can lead to errant data. The specific sources of error in a given dataset will depend on the specific circumstances of the data collection process. In order to minimize the potential for error, researchers should take care to use precise and accurate measurement methods, minimize human error, and carefully design the data collection process to avoid potential problems

22. Explain or rationalize errant data or describe possible sources of error and how they may have affected the outcome.


HOW TO RATIONALIZE ERRANT DATA AND HOW THEY HAVE AFFECTED THE OUTCOME

Errant data is data that is incorrect or does not conform to the expected values or patterns. There are many possible sources of error that can cause data to be errant, and these errors can have a significant impact on the outcome of a study or analysis.

One possible source of error is measurement error. This occurs when the measurements used to collect data are not accurate or precise, leading to data that does not reflect the true values of the variables being measured. For example, if a researcher uses a ruler that is not properly calibrated to measure the length of an object, the measurements will be inaccurate and the data will be errant.

Another possible source of error is observer error. This occurs when the person collecting the data makes mistakes or biases in the way they observe or record the data. For example, if a researcher is asked to count the number of birds in a park, they may accidentally count the same bird multiple times, leading to an overestimate of the number of birds.

Another possible source of error is sampling error. This occurs when the data is collected from a sample that is not representative of the entire population. For example, if a researcher conducts a survey to determine the average income of residents in a city, but only surveys residents in one wealthy neighborhood, the data will not be representative of the entire city and will be errant.

Overall, errant data can have a significant impact on the outcome of a study or analysis. To prevent or mitigate the effects of errant data, it is important to identify and address the sources of error in the data collection process. This can help to ensure that the data is accurate and reliable, and that the conclusions drawn from the data are valid.
