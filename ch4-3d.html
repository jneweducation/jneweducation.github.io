3. Convert 10g of CH4 to molecules of CH4​


Answer:

—To convert 10g of CH4 to molecules of CH4, we can use the formula:

moles of CH4 = mass of CH4 / molar mass of CH4

The molar mass of CH4 is 16.04 g/mol. So, we can substitute this value into the formula:

moles of CH4 = 10g / 16.04 g/mol

By calculating it, we get:

moles of CH4 = 0.623 mol

To convert moles to molecules, we can use Avogadro's number which is 6.022 x 10^23. So we can multiply the number of moles by Avogadro's number

molecules of CH4 = 0.623 mol x 6.022 x 10^23

By calculating it we get:

molecules of CH4 = 3.742 x 10^23

So, 10g of CH4 is equal to 3.742 x 10^23 molecules of CH4.

8. Methane (CH) is the principal component of natural gas. How many moles of CH4 are present in 7.51 g of CH4?​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of methane (CH₄).

The molar masses of C and H are 12.01 g and 1.008 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass} & = \text{12.01 g + 4(1.008 g)} \\ & = \text{12.01 g + 4.032 g} \\ & = \text{16.04 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of methane by using dimensional analysis.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{CH}_4 & = \text{7.51 g} \: \text{CH}_4 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CH}_4}{\text{16.04 g} \: \text{CH}_4} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.468 mol}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 0.468 mol of CH₄ are present in 7.51 g of CH₄.

(a + b) * c = ac + bc

Using this property, we can expand each pair of parentheses in the expression as follows:

(2b+3d)(2b+3d)(2b+3d)

= (2b+3d) * (2b+3d) * (2b+3d)

= [(2b)(2b) + (2b)(3d) + (3d)(2b) + (3d)(3d)] * (2b+3d)

= [(4b^2) + (12bd) + (9d^2)] * (2b+3d)

= 8b^3 + 24b^2d + 6bd^2 + 18b^2d + 54bd^2 + 27d^3

= 8b^3 + 42b^2d + 60bd^2 + 27d^3

Therefore, the solution for (2b+3d)(2b+3d)(2b+3d) is 8b^3 + 42b^2d + 60bd^2 + 27d^3.

20. CH4+2H2O→ 4H2 (g) + .CO2(g) IF 60.0 g of CH4 reacted with 6.3g of water, what is the limiting reactant?


Answer:

The limiting reactant of a reaction is the substance present in lesser amounts. In this case, it is H 2O :))

Explanation:

This is obtained by, first, computing the number of moles of CH4 and H2O from the given mass.

To convert from mass to moles, one only needs to multiply the given mass to the molar mass of the compound.

60 g CH4 • (1 mol CH4/16.05 g CH4) ≈ 3.78 mol CH4

6.3 g H20 • (1 mol H2O/18.01 g H20) ≈ 0.35 mol H20

Second, compute for the mole ratio to confirm how much of each substance would be consumed theoretically in the reaction.

A 3.78 mol CH4 would use up around 7.56 mol of H2O, while a 0.35 mol of H20 would use up approximately 0.18 mol of CH4.

It is presented in this computation:

3.78 mol CH4 • (2 mol H2O/1 mol CH4) = 7.56 mol H2O

0.35 mol H2O • (1 mol CH4/ 2 mol H2O) = 0.18 mol CH4

So, if all 3.78 mole of CH4 is used, at some point there would be no more moles of H20 left for the reaction to proceed (because we are only given 0.35 moles of the said substance). The lesser amount of H20 would inhibit further reaction. Hence, it is the limiting reactant.
