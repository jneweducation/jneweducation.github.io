6. discriminant and nature of roots of x2 + 6x + 9=0


QUADRATIC EQUATIONDirection: Determine the discriminant of x²+6x+9=0 and the nature of the roots.

Final Answer:

D: 0 Nature of the roots: real and equal

Step-by-step explanation:

x² + 6x + 9 = 0

First, identify the value of a, b, and c.

Given:

a = 1b = 6c = 9

Second, write the formula.

Formula to be used: [tex] \bold{ {b}^{2} - 4ac}[/tex]

Lastly, solve using the formula then write the nature of its roots.

[tex] {b}^{2} - 4ac \\ {(6)}^{2} - 4(1)(9) \\ 36 - 36 \\ 0[/tex]

Thus, the discriminant is 0 and its nature of roots is real and equal.

✏️DISCRIMINANT

- Identify the discriminant and the nature of roots of:

[tex] \large \sf x² + 6x + 9 = 0[/tex]

- The standard form of the equation is ax² + bx + c = 0, then find the values of a, b, and c

[tex] \sf a = 1,b = 6,c = 9[/tex]

- Substitute the values to the formula of how to find the discriminant then classify the nature of roots.

[tex] \large \boxed{D = \sf {b}^{2} - 4ac}[/tex]

[tex] {D = \sf {6}^{2} - 4(1)(9)}[/tex]

[tex] {D = \sf 36 - 4(9)}[/tex]

[tex] {D = \sf 36 - 36}[/tex]

[tex] {D = \sf 0}[/tex]

- Since, the discriminant is 0 then the nature of roots of the equation is real and equal.

Quadratic equations or polynomial equations (many terms) with the highest power of two can be written as

ax² + bx + c = 0

a, b, and c are real numbers and a ≠ 0.

The formula for the discriminant value of the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 is as follows.

D = b² - 4ac

Following are the properties and discriminant functions in the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0.

If D > 0, then the quadratic equation has two distinct real roots (p ≠ q).If D = 0, then the quadratic equation has two equal roots, real and rational (p = q)If D < 0, then the quadratic equation has imaginary roots or is not real.

The solutions to solving a quadratic equation are called the roots of the quadratic equation. What is meant by the roots of a quadratic equation is the value of the variable x that satisfies when substituted into the quadratic equation.

In general, there are three ways to find the roots of a quadratic equation, namely the method of factoring, completing the perfect square, and the abc formula.

In finding the roots of a quadratic equation by factoring, we make the quadratic equation a product of two linear equations.

Some of the steps that need to be taken are:

Find any two numbers, for example p and q, which when added together the result is equal to b (p + q = b) and multiplying the result equals ac (p × q = ac) and since a = 1, then p × q = c. To determine the pair of numbers p and q, we can look for numbers that are factors of only ac or c because a = 1.

After the p and q values ​​have been determined, substitute the p and q values ​​in the factoring formula (ax + p)(ax + q) = 0
