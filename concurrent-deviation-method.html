The legislative branch is made up of the House and Senate, known collectively as the Congress. Among other powers, the legislative branch makes all laws, declares war, regulates interstate and foreign commerce and controls taxing and spending policies.

Congress is responsible for enabling laws to ensure that the spirit of the constitution is preserved in the country and that the constitution itself is often revised or amended. The legislative body comes up with two primary papers in order to craft laws: bills and resolutions.

Resolutions express the Senate's or the House of Representatives' values and feelings. Three related components can be further separated into these resolutions:

Joint resolutions require the approval of both houses of Congress and the President's signature and if approved, have the power and effect of a statute.

Concurrent resolutions are used for issues concerning the activities of both chambers of Congress and must be accepted by both houses in the same manner, but are not submitted for signature to the President and thus have no force and effect of a statute.

Simple resolutions deal entirely with issues within the privilege of one Congress chamber, are not forwarded to the President for his signature, and thus do not have the power and effect of a statute

14. Explain what is meant by concurrent vectors.


Answer:

Concurrent vectors are those which pass through the same point. In other words, a concurrent vector system is a set of two or more vectors whose lines of action intersect at a point. It is not necessary for all or any of the vectors to be actually in contact with the common point.
